Winner in Diversity Week contest

by Bridgett Brumbaugh / Staff Writer
Spring quarter, the Office of Minority Affairs sponsored a contest to promote Diversity Week. Students were asked to summit a one-page essay following the theme, “Beyond our Comfort Zones: Understanding the Importance of a Diverse Campus.” According to the Director of Minority Affairs, “ This office held the contest because we at Minority Affairs wanted to get students ideas on the importance of a diverse campus.” Eight students entered the contest to compete for the $100.00 prize. Computer Science major Nadia Hughley won the essay contest:

 

Beyond Our Comfort Zones: Understanding the Importance of a Diverse Campus

Most people grow up in neighborhoods, surrounded by persons with backgrounds very similar to their own. They go to school and play with the same group of friends all of their lives. They are never directly exposed to those from varying cultural, racial, religious, or economic backgrounds. All of their knowledge and opinions about other groups of people are based on what they hear from their friends, their parents, and the media. Unfortunately, much of this knowledge is inaccurate and prejudicial. When they venture out from the neighborhoods, and enter the workplace, they unwittingly add to the tension that already exists between so many segments of society.

Many people harbor prejudicial views and opinions, sometimes without recognizing them as such. These opinions are formed without firsthand knowledge or examination of the facts. This prejudice will manifest itself in how they treat people. It may be blatant or very subtle.

It could be as simple as showing a person less common courtesy because they do not happen to come from the same background. It could be much more serious. Maybe a certain person is hired for a job, because historically his or her group is better suited to the work. Maybe another person, who was more qualified, was denied the same job because his or her ethnic or cultural background did not seem conducive to the work. The employer failed to see the individual. Instead, the focus was on some preconceived stereotypes. Unfortunately, this is not uncommon. If more people were taught to separate the person from the group they happen to belong to, the world would certainly improve.

Colleges and universities could play a major role in helping to eliminate prejudice. Since most people attend public schools with the same people from their neighborhoods, college is the ideal forum to introduce the idea of cultural diversity. The enrollments of most major universities consist of students from vastly diverse backgrounds. This allows students to gain firsthand knowledge about different cultures. By providing an arena for social interaction between students, universities help to lower the walls that separate people into groups. This allows the students to see the person on the other side, and they can realize that the other person is not all that different from themselves. Ignorance is the breeding ground for prejudice. Universities offer the knowledge necessary to combat this infestation, and leave the garden of peace and understanding free to blossom.
