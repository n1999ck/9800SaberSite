Lee Ann Womack
7 p.m. and 9 p.m. Tuesday, April 7, presented by WKCN

Prior to her 1997 self-titled debut recording, the 31 -year-old Texas native had been known to Nashville insiders as a local performer and songwriter who had collaborated with such heavyweights as Bill Anderson, Sam Hogin and Ed Hill.

Her debut, according to Billboard magazine, has drawn heavy airplay and stamped a distinctive style: “Great voice, great songs ... Womack pays homage to country’s rich tradition without sounding retro. She can handle hard-driving, truck-driving tunes, gospel songs, and tender ballads with equal aplomb.”

Womack had attracted top-notch studio support in making the album: a duet with Mark Chestnut on “Make Memories with Me,” backing vocals by Ricky Skaggs and Sharon White on “Never Again, Again” (her first single) and “Get Up in Jesus’ Name,” and famed producer Tony Brown adds piano on “You’ve Got To Talk To Me.”

Drivin’ n’ Cryin’
7 p.m. Thursday, April 9, presented by. WVRK and WRBL

With ‘Influences ranging from Bob Dylan and Hank Williams to The Clash and the Ramones, Drivin’ n’ Cryin’ followed REM and the B-52s to out of the mid-80’s Athen’s -alternative” scene to broadened recognition. The 1986 debut Scarred but Smarter was a college-radio staple and catapulted them from a top Atlanta/Athens-club draw to extensive touring and major-label interest.

The emergence led to high-profile tours in 1989 — co-headlining with Soul Asylum and as an opening act for REM’s arena-sized “Green Tour.” Also, a guest appearance by R.E.M.’s Peter Buck added to the group’s third release, Mystery Road (1989).

A commercial peak may have occurred with 1991's gold-selling Fly Me Courageous. It spawned two videos (the title track and “Build a Fire”) as among MTV’s most requested, and heavy airplay on rock radio. The success culminated on tour — with legendary Nell Young and Seattle’s Sonic Youth, prompting a live EP and home video, Live on Fire.

The group last summer released a seventh, self-titled album featuring another collaboration with R.E.M.’s Buck (“Passing Through”) and a cover of John Denver’s “Leaving on a Jet Plane” — the first son- front man Kevin Kinney had learned to play, as a 15-year-old with his first guitar.

Swingin’ Medallions
7 p.m. Saturday, April I I

Lone-original member Jon McElrath continues to preserve the Medallions’ 30-plus-year reign as “the party band of the South.” The Greenwood, SC-based act is commonly associated with beach music, rhythm and blues, frat rock or shaggin’ music.

The Medallions (including a four-piece horn section) feature a set list covering James Brown, the Four Tops, the Temptations, Tom Jones and other names synonymous with 60’s pop/r&b, in addition to the more contemporary likes of Prince, MC Hammer, George Michael and Garth Brooks.

The high-energy shows also include Medallion top-40 originals from the 60’s including “She Drives Me Out of My Mind,” “Hey, Hey Baby” and “Double Shot (of My Baby’s Love)” — a million-copy seller in 1966.
