Campus Candid: Professor Harry Kruger

by Bridgett Brumbaugh
Professor Harry Kruger has been teaching music at CSU for 33 years. At one point he was teaching part time at CSU while conducting the Columbus Symphony orchestra. Kruger also created the LaGrange Symphony Orchestra and still conducts there.

Before coming to CSU, Kruger was involved in other activities. He was assistant conductor at for the Atlanta Symphony Orchestra for six years. He was also the Director of Cultural Affairs at Bolling Green University for 4 years.

Kruger currently holds a Master’s degree from the New England Conservatory of Music in Boston. He attended Emory for two years.

In 1991, Kruger was given an Honorary Doctorate from LaGrange College. He was also the third finalist for the Educator of the Year award.

Kruger is married and has three children. His wife, Natalie W. Kruger, teaches music here at Columbus State University. She has been a faculty member for 25 years. One of his daughters plays the violin for the Cincinnati Symphony Orchestra. His other daughter plays the viola for the world famous Lars String Quartet. His son, the youngest child, is the Marketing Executive of Swift Textiles in Atlanta

Hobbies are also a part of Kruger’s life. Even though he has bad knees and cannot jog, swimming is still an option. He also enjoys reading all sorts of literature and traveling.

In the last ten years, he and his wife have traveled to Alaska, Israel, Italy, Greece, North Africa, Portugal, the Scandinavian countries, and Spain. Last Christmas he and his wife traveled to Russia and enjoyed Moscow’s beautiful surroundings. Kruger also traveled to Korea in 1983 and conducted the Seoul Philharmonics. In the future, he would like to travel to South Africa, China, Egypt and many more countries.

Kruger feels CSU has greatly improved over the years. He says, ‘When I first came here, the student population was only 500. Now we have over 5,000 students here.” He also says that W. Frank Brown has done a wonderful job with the University as is his successor at the Columbus Symphony Orchestra, W. Del Gobo.
