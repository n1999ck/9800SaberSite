Roughing it down under

by Michael Cox / Editor-in-Chief
A Columbus State University student recently took her education to the outback. Laura Forrester recently returned from her trip to Australia where she not only studied, but gained valuable life experiences as well. As part of a program branching from Boston University, Forrester spent a month in Australia this past summer taking classes.

Forrester is a transfer student from Bainbridge College pursuing her Bachelor’s Degree in Biology. She is a senior and says that she plans to pursue her Master’s in Environmental Science soon after graduation. She came to CSU as a result of her involvement in Student Government and was encouraged to attend Columbus State University by the Dean of Students at her previous institution.

During her trip to Australia, Forrester achieved and experienced many things at the same time. As part of a team who conducted a Tropical Rainforest Restoration Study, she learned a lot about the environment in Australia and the culture as well. She was able to actually live in a rainforest for an entire month where she was unable to take a shower.

Forrester states that, “It really does rain all of the time in a rain forest.”

Forrester was also a part of a group who wrote a constitution for an environmental plan regarding a small area in Australia named Yungaber. She was also able to meet other students representing different counties such as France, Germany, England and Asia. However, her one month stay turned out to be longer than expected. Forrester decided to embark on a great journey and stay an entire extra month on her own.

She says, “I planned to stay because I never thought that I would go back any time soon.”

Her extended stay proved to be valuable as she experienced many things. Laura traveled over 5,000 miles on a Greyhound Bus while covering all of the east coast and outback of Australia. She spent an overwhelming thirty-six hours on a bus while traveling to Melbourne. Forrester learned how to live outdoors as she slept anywhere she could to get by for the night. She slept mainly in youth hostiles, but an occasional bench or flowerbed was never out of the question.

Forrester’s trip allowed her to go scuba diving near the Great Barrier Reef. She was also able to experience the economy and food as well. Laura says that pork is the primary food in Australia, but overall it is not that different. Economically, there is a little difference. Fruit is the major business in Australia, and minimum wage starts at $10.00 an hour.

She also enjoyed many encounters with such animals as kangaroos and koala bears. Overall, Laura felt that her trip was a success and that she would love to go back again.

She concludes by saying, “I came back knowing that I am independent and that I can do things myself.”
