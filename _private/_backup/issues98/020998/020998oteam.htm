"O" Team teaches leadership

by Athena Curry / Editor-in-Chief

 
The Orientation Team at CSU is a group of students who are trained to help people who are new to the campus.

Orientation leaders are available to help people find their way around campus and to answer questions regarding general academic information.

Team leaders also give guided tours of the campus and explain to students how to drop and add classes, acquire degree progress sheets, catalogs and directories.

Grant Salter, a CSU student and Orientation leader, says, “I think it gives you a great chance to give back to the school. It makes a positive image for the institution.”

Lisa Rios has been a Orientation leader since last spring. She says, “I had the chance to apply to be an Orientation leader. I had to submit an application and find references for myself. I then had to meet with the advisor, Cecilia Buffkin, the orientation graduate assistant, and a small group of other applicants. After the whole process was finished, I was selected for the 97-98 team. I spent the summer bonding with my fellow leaders, working on public speaking techniques, familiarizing myself with the campus, and learning to deal with large numbers of people.”

Each year the Orientation Team goes through a series of training sessions and then goes on a retreat. Last years retreat was held in the General Coffee State Park in South Georgia. This year’s retreat will most likely be held in North Georgia.

Rios also says that even though it was a lot of work, especially since she works full time in addition to attending classes, she saw the experience as a wonderful opportunity to contribute to the CSU community.

“Being a member of the “O” team is rewarding in several ways. First, I get to know about my school in depth. I probably would not have taken the time if I had not joined the Orientation Team. I know things like, what the first four original buildings on campus are, what’s under the clock tower, and where the “boom room” is located. Second, I get a chance to meet new people at the orientations,” Rios says.

According to Rios, Orientation leaders aim for making a good, lasting impression. She says it’s a wonderful feeling to have a new student call out your name as you pass on campus.

Bobby Ferrone is another Orientation leader with good things to say about the program. He says, “It is a great opportunity to develop leadership skills and be able to present myself in front of a large body of people.

Those interested in the Orientation team should contact Cecilia Buffkin in the Student Development office in the Davidson Center, room 271.
