CSU daycare question continues

by Sharon Langford / Staff Writer

 
Every few years, a new study is done to look into the possibility of a daycare program at CSU. Now that the percentage of non-traditional students is at an estimated 45%, the subject has been brought up again.

Army Captain candidates who ran the SGA about fifteen years ago started a childcare program. They helped renovate a house on Sellers Circle where they installed child-sized toilets and other necessities to get it started.

“At first the program ran fine because there weren’t any outside daycare programs,” said Larry Kees, Asst. Vice President for Student Affairs.

After a few years daycares began springing up everywhere and the College program began to dwindle. Fewer people were bringing their children and the price was about the same as the other daycares so the College abandoned the program. Also, the facility was supposed to be self-supporting but the college ended up supplementing the daycare by thousands of dollars.

CSU has one of the largest populations of non-traditional students in the Southeast and the average student age is 26. Because of this, we are in more need of a childcare facility now than ever before. The problems stem from the lack of space and money but are also due to the licensing and certifications required by the State.

“The best idea would be to have a private facility move or build adjacent to campus,” said Kees. Privatization seems to be the current answer to some of the areas on campus like housing and the cafeteria. It is cheaper to have a company who specializes in a certain field to take charge of facilities and be responsible for regulations and codes. The case would be the same for childcare.

“Years ago, we had to serve a lot of masters like the local and state Fire Marshals and Health Department,” said Kees. But after all the horror stories about daycares, the State has many licenses, laws and codes to be met which would be costly to CSU.

Augusta State University (ASU) and CSU are about the same size and have a similar student body demographic. A representative of their school said they have had a daycare for quite some time but, if they had it to do over again, they would never have begun the program.

The ASU daycare facility is located in an older building on campus and is funded by student activity fees. The cost of running the program is about $45,000 per year, which does not include the rent or the utility bills.

ASU students want to take their children there for the full day, which was a problem at the CSU daycare also. According to State regulations it is illegal to keep the children the full day because the daycare cannot provide a hot meal.

Some people think the Department of Education could use a daycare as a study program but the Department cannot help with the funding and the State maintains that workers must be certified.

Others think that if CSU can afford to build a gym, they should be willing to have a daycare also. The construction of the gym is being funded by the State’s money, a daycare would have to be funded from limited Student Activity fees.

In a few more years, the question of a daycare will come up again and the same answers will be given. If CSU wants the program, it will be best to build a new facility and have it run by a private organization. In the meantime, don’t take your kids to class with you because it’s against policy.

Maybe one day La Petite or the Growing Room will be affiliated with the campus and the need for day care will be met.
