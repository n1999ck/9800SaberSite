CSU establishes competitive edge

by Evelyn Tolbert-Owens

 
Administrations of every successful university attempt to attract sharp and motivated students. They also work to keep the students at their university. CSU, preparing for semester conversion, is offering some special perks to make the students want to come to CSU and stay at CSU.

To keep the competitive edge with other universities in the Southeast CSU announces the Honors Program to start Fall semester 1998. The program will provide significant challenges and opportunities to enrich an exceptional student’s education and will take educational experience beyond the ordinary. It will strive to create a community of outstanding scholars as well as to stimulate involvement in campus and community activities.

The Honors Program was designed to challenge and reward highly motivated students with special intellectual, cultural, and social opportunities. Activities in and out of the classroom will offer a breadth of perspective and depth of understanding specifically tailored for Honor students. The Program will offer friendships and camaraderie by providing opportunities for students to participate in a variety of enrichment activities.

Honors courses will not involve any more work or time than regular courses but a different kind of work. Generally, they will involve more independent study, more stimulating and imaginative assignments, more experiential learning, and greater opportunities to utilize and develop writing, critical thinking and computer skills.

Because honors classes will be small, students will receive more individual attention from their professors than do students in regular classes. Honors professors will get to know their students well, take pride in nurturing their abilities and will be ideally suited to write effective letters of recommendation for the student’s career placement.

Students in the Honors Program will take 3 honors courses that substitute for core curriculum courses. These and most honors courses will have an H designator following the course number or be listed as an HONS course. These courses, limited to 15 students per course, will be designed to encourage discussion, independent work, critical and reflective thinking, and significant writing, creative or computer projects.

Several honors core courses will be taught each semester. Courses offered in Fall 1998 are Principles of Biology ,Art Appreciation and Pre-Calculus. Courses offered for Spring 1999 are Freshmen Composition, American Government, and Communications.

Each student enrolled will take a minimum of 9 upper-division semester hours (exclusive internships and cooperative programs) in the major or related area as designed by colleges in cooperation with departments. The maximum number of independent study semester hours within the Honors Program is 3. Generally, upper division honors courses will encourage more advanced work or allow for greater creativity. Flexibility will be allowed in programs. The Honors Program Committee must approve program designs.

Each student enrolled will also engage in an off-campus enrichment activity such as a service program (for example, CSU Ambassadors), a service learning program, study abroad, or other significant experience. Students need to submit proposals to the Honors Program Committee for approval and sign up for HONS 2000, a non-credit course.

Furthermore, each enrolled student will participate in four specified honors events, one per semester for four semesters. To fulfill this obligation, students will sign up for HONS 3000, a non-credit class, and submit critical evaluations of events to the Associate Dean of University College. The general education learning outcomes for HONS 3000 will vary because the events will vary. Most events will emphasize contemporary phenomena, aesthetics, critical thinking and communication.

The thesis and oral defense are capstones of the Honors Program. Topics need to be approved within the student’s major department. An appropriately chosen college committee will hear the oral defense.

To earn credit of the thesis and defense, students need to sign up for HONS 4000 (3-0-3). The general education learning outcomes are Communications, Critical Thinking, and others, depending on major.

Under special circumstances, the Honors Program Committee will consider an alternative, equivalent activity to the thesis and defense.

There will be a special luncheon held for honors students every semester. The luncheon will include a guest speaker.

Transcripts of honors students will include the designation “Graduated from Honors Program”and special recognition will be given at graduation to honors students.

There will be a ceremony and banquet prior to graduation, special access to distinguished visitors to campus, extra-curricular activities such as social and cultural events, waiver of out-of-state tuition (limited number available), paid books, early registration before all other students and smaller classes (15 students per class) for students participating in the program.

There are currently 10 honors scholarships available, sponsored by the Columbus Foundation. These scholarships are in addition to the HOPE scholarships.

Currently, the Honors scholarship pays the student $3,300/year.

The amount varies from year to year and is expected to go even higher within the next 1 to 2 years.

The scholarship is at this time still reserved for students right out of high school, but changes are in the works to also accommodate Freshmen and Sophomores.

Enrolled students will also have to opportunity to study abroad.

They will spend approximately 5 to 6 weeks in a foreign country such as France or England to learn about the country’s culture and people. Initially, these trips will be partially offset by the CSU foundation but eventually will be fully paid for the students on the Honors scholarships.

Plans to raise the number of available Honors scholarships to 20 instead of ten are in the works.

In certain cases, the Honors Program Committee may consider exceptions to the admission criteria.

Once a student is admitted to the Honors Program, he must enroll in a minimum of 9 semester hours per semester and must maintain a 3.50 cumulative GPA. If the student’s cumulative GPA falls below 3.50, the student will be placed on probation for one semester but can continue to take honors courses.

If the student’s cumulative GPA is below 3.50 at the end of the probationary semester, the student will be suspended from the Honors Program but may reapply to enter the Program if his/her cumulative GPA returns to 3.50 or higher.

Students may not enter the program while enrolled in developmental studies courses or in courses to remediate CPC deficiencies.

Students not in the Honors Program may enroll in honors courses, space permitting during regular registration, so long as they have a minimum cumulative GPA of 3.50.
