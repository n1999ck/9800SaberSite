ICAPP receives nationwide attention

by Heather Warren / Associate Editor

 
Vice President Al Gore said in a speech Monday that he agrees with a Columbus State University (CSU) student who called Georgia’s Intellectual Capital Partnership Program (ICAPP) an “excuse eradicator”.

The largest single business investment in the South East, ICAPP, helps educate people in computer based knowledge to provide local companies the high tech employees needed to run their companies.

CSU is the designer and first implementer of ICAPP in Georgia. According to an ICAPP brochure, the aim is to help Georgia business and industry acquire a well-educated employment pool.

CSU’s program began as a partnership with Total System Services, Inc. Now, other Georgia companies are using the program.

The program works due to business collaboration. A company meets an individual and agrees to sponsor them until graduation. The company agrees to hire the student after graduation from the ICAPP program.

The program is a collaboration among CSU, private industry, the University System of Georgia and the State of Georgia to increase availability of high tech education, according to an ICAPP brochure.

It began when Total System Services of Georgia experienced a shortage of computer programmers. CSU, along with the Georgia Department of Industry and Trade and the Columbus Chamber of Commerce, repackaged seven of its computer science courses to meet TSYS’ educational needs. This program was funded in part by Georgia’s Quickstart and was called the COMPASS Program.

In 1992, an agreement reached among CSU, TSYS, and IBM, resulted in a first-of-its-kind applied computer science curriculum developed by CSU.

In the Winter of 1994, CSU and TSYS undertook their largest cooperative education program to date,called Quickstart 2. Quickstart 2 continued into 1996. By the end of this time, CSU had educated more than 550 computer professionals.

In March 1996, Georgia Governor Zell Miller escalated the collaboration between CSU and TSYS and called it ICAPP. The ICAPP program has become a model for economic development efforts in Georgia. This landmark initiative made it possible for CSU to provide the facilities, equipment, and personnel to educate 400 computer professionals a year.

A student that has a sponsor can receive a service cancelable loan. The loan is to help with living costs and extra educational fees. After the individual works at a computer related job for any Georgia company in the state for four years the loan is canceled.

According to an ICAPP brochure, by the end of 1998, CSU will have educated approximately 1200 computer professionals.
