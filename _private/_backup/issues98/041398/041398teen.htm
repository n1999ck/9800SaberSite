Court rules on teen sex

by Athena Curry / Editor-in-Chief
On April 10, a California appeals court unanimously ruled that teenagers have no constitutional right to have sex.

The court upheld a lower juvenile court ruling that said the right to privacy does not include sex, even consensual, between minors.

A 16 year old boy in Alameda County, California was arrested and charged with rape after having sex with his 14 year old girlfriend, who consented to the act.

The boy challenged the arrest as unconstitutional on the grounds that sex is a fundamental right covered by privacy laws that apply to minors as well as adults.

Lawyers for the boy argued that the statutory rape laws are in place in order to protect minors from adult perpetrators. The argued that in this case, the two minors were both consenting and therefore, neither should be penalized by law.

Justice Ignazio Ruvolo wrote in the court’s opinion, “There are freedoms which adults enjoy which are beyond those afforded minors.”

The court ruled that while the reality is that many California teenagers are sexually active, that fact does not mean that minors have a right to privacy to engage in sexual intercourse.

Earlier court rulings have been overturned, which would have required parental consent for minors to have abortions. However, in this the court says this earlier ruling does not give minors behave as they like sexually.
The lower court’s decision to put the boy on probation was upheld.
