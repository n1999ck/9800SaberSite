New President: Dr. Brown

by James W. England / Editor-in-chief of 1988 Saber Staff

This article was taken from the January 20, 1988 edition of the Saber.
While 1988 had a bumpy start, with bad weather and computer-printer problems, things quickly smoothed out. The long awaited appointment of a new college president finally took place.

The announcement, January 13th, that the College’s own Dr. Frank Brown would be appointed president, effective January 14th, brought smiles and an almost audible sigh of relief across the campus.

While clearly a favorable decision, he seemed to be either the first or second choice of almost everyone, Brown will have a rocky road ahead. Some will be looking for instant solutions. The past president was not the problem. Most of the problems are still with us - only the leadership problem has been (hopefully) solved.

Dr. Brown now has the difficult task of pulling the college together and leading it forward. Columbus College has tremendous potential that only inspired leadership can bring out. We can only hope he will be given the chance to do so, and the cooperation and support necessary to make it work.

The “healing” process has already begun. It started with the establishment of the Presidential Search Committee, ably chaired by Dr. William J. Frazier. The meeting in which the search was conducted, especially by the interviews on-campus of the six semi-finalists, allowed issues to be raised and problems to be brought out into the sun light. It was a worthwhile process.

During Dr. Brown’s press conference, following the announcement of his selection, he spoke of the office being a sacred trust, the challenges ahead that face us, and the need to “...decide who we are as a college.” He also stressed the need to “...sell ourselves and tell our story ...... a point raised by every presidential candidate.

When questioned on his approach and methods Dr. Brown stressed his desire for improved communication, open administration, and easy access to administrators. A transition team composed of faculty and staff members will be formed to start the ball rolling, with advisory groups established as the need arises.

In a separate interview, when questioned about the roles of the SGA and Faculty Senate, Dr. Brown indicated that while the roles of the Faculty Senate and Student Government may not change he would like to see improved results, more ideas, and no closed doors. He generally felt that what was needed was better communications and working relationships.

Dr. Brown also indicated he would like to see some type of open forum periodically that would include students, faculty, staff, and so on, to discuss campus issues and address areas of concern to all members of the college community.

He felt the issue of declining enrollment was a pressing one that required effort from everyone. Areas such as peer recruitment, high school visits by past graduates, students telling their story, and involvement with social
organizations could reap benefits.

When asked about his goals and expectations Dr. Brown stressed the desire to “...be out of the office, around the campus and the community ... the physical office of the president has been one of our problems.” He further indicated that “...I just want us to do the usual and do it well ... restore pride ... everyone try to make a difference, and ... (come) back into the mainstream.”

Dr. Brown is clearly searching for ideas and support - the college deserves no less. One particular area he would like input on is the course College 105. Is it helpful? What should be changed and what should stay the same? For those students who have taken the course in the past drop Dr. Brown a short note letting him know your thoughts about the class - good, bad, or indifferent.
