Reo Speedwagon talk about their early days

by David Barr / News Editor of 1987 Saber Staff

This was taken from the November 4, 1987 edition of the Saber.
A little known fact about REO SPEEDWAGON was that they were formed in college—the University of Illinois, to be exact, back in the late ‘60s. It was an environment that served as a launching pad for the’ group to go on and become one of rock’s premier groups.

Formed by REO members Newal Doughty (keyboards) and Alan Gratzer (drums), who shared rooms in the same dorm, the first incarnation of the group played its first gigs at fraternity parties, where they would sometimes be caught in the midst of food fights. “You got paid $40 and all the mashed potatoes we could lick off the drums,” recalls Doughty with a laugh. “That was our initiation into rock & roll.”

“It was an exciting time to put a band together. We were the first band on campus doing underground music—covers of songs by some exciting artists of the time. Alan and I were the only guys on campus with long hair. People thought we were from outer space.”

Says Alan Gratzer of those early days: “There were 30 other bands at the time. Champaign was a real mecca for rock & roll. We concentrated on having lots of fun,” he adds. “One of the things we would do was go down into the steam tunnels, to which we had access. We’d go down there late at night, walk along, and then turn up in the middle of the sidewalk on campus, and freak everyone out. It was a subterranean world. We’d fall for anything weird.”

One of the songs REO has been performing in concert lately is “Golden Country,” written by Gary Richrath (lead guitar) back in the late ‘60s as a response to the madness of the Vietnam War. It was also one of the songs they played on the college circuit. “From the beginning, we’ve always enjoyed college audiences. Crowds have always been knowledgeable, appreciative, and open to new things.” Richrath himself attended college in his hometown of Peoria before linking up with the group.

Kevin Cronin went to college for three semesters-at Loyola in Chicago-before moving to Champaign to join the group. He says: “My dad always told me to stay in school and pursue a college education. It seemed like good advice, but back in 1969 and 1970, it was a time for people to express themselves, and I guess I was on the fringe at the time. I heard rock & roll calling, and that’s where I headed.”

Things, of course, have paid off nicely for REO SPEEDWAGON: they’ve made their mark on contemporary music with their celebrated live shows, powerful songs about honest real emotions and record sales nudging 30 million worldwide. Life As We Know It, their current and gold-selling LP, is rock & roll that tells it all: 10 songs that zero in on life, relationships and all the side effects with unusually sharp insight.
