It's all in the name
by Wes Carroll / Guest Writer

HOME

We begin our four-part series on the shortcomings in entertainment with a discussion of band names.

A band’s name is important. If you hear a catchy new song on the radio and want to buy the cd, you need to be able to remember it when the d.j. says it. So, many bands will have catchy names to get your attention. But, do you ever stop to think about what the names mean?

Many say that the Gin Blossoms had “a pretty name.” In actuality, a gin blossom is a red mark on the face of a drunk person. Toad the Wet Sprocket took their name from a Monty Python sketch. Veruca Salt named themselves after the snotty little rich girl in “Willy Wonka and the Chocolate Factory.”

These are all examples of “clever” names (as well as examples of bands who are no longer together). But some bands will be very forward and tell you everything that you need to know about them in their name.
One of these groups is The Cherry Poppin’ Daddies. Now I know that swing is “in” and that we, the sheep-like public, have no choice but to buy swing music and put on our “khakis” and do the “Charleston” until our feet are “red,” but do we really have to support these clowns?

It is my belief that The Cherry Poppin’ Daddies (and bands like them) are 95% of what is wrong with music. (The other 5% involves rappers who sample the song “Islands in the Stream.”)

So, why do people (especially women) support these guys? My only guess is that people aren’t stopping to think about what the name means. So, to help anyone out there who may still be confused, I’ve thought up some alternative names for the group that will be true to their current name. How about the “Virgin Busters”? That would be good. Or maybe the “Innocence Takers”. Oh, no, maybe the “Tactful Rapists”. Yeah, that’s better.

So, how do we correct this problem in our music? We stop buying their cds. That’s all we can do. So, please, I beg of you, do the world a favor and, if you must buy swing music, buy something that doesn’t insult women. (And then we can start to work on the other problem before someone takes a sample from “the Gambler” and raps to it.)
