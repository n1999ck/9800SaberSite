Pearl Jam at best on tour

by Brian Hale / Entertainment Editor
I had the pleasure of getting to attend two Pearl Jam shows two weeks ago, and let me tell you, if you missed them, you missed something special.

The first show of the week for me was the Atlanta show at Lakewood Amphitheater on September 1. Mudhoney was the opening act, and only about 50 of the 19,000 plus in attendance really cared that they were playing. I suppose they were ok, but we just wanted Pearl Jam to take the stage as soon as possible.

Mudhoney knew as much and held their set to 45 minutes. This show was special for me, because I was able to be there with a dear friend and most beautiful person I know- who’s name will not be mentioned for the sake of my health.

Finally it was time for Eddie Vedder and company to take the stage. The stage was decorated with five candles and several angel-like ornaments. “Hail Hail” was the opener and it was crazy from there on out.

The band sailed through news songs, old songs, and a few cover songs. It is terribly difficult to pick a favorite moment of the night, but there were several highlights. “Do the Evolution” and “Wishlist” took on new forms when done live, and “Alive” was better than ever with the new guitar solo tacked on at the end by Mike McCready.

Magazines and television shows have stated that the band appears to be more relaxed and that they look like they are finally enjoying the success that they have attained. Well, the reports appear to be correct. The evidence is in the bands songs and their performance. Smiles could be seen from everybody on stage, especially frontman Eddie Vedder. At times he would just lean back, smile and watch the rest of the band do their stuff.

Ex-Soundgarden drummer Matt Cameron did a great job filling in for the absent Jack Irons on drums. If their appearance is not enough to prove their new found happiness, then just listen to the lyrics. “I’m not so overwhelmed. It seems so simple now..” is what Vedder sings in the song “In Hiding.”

“Black” was another favorite of the night for me. “And all I taught her was everything” got a nudge from the beautiful girl. Interesting.

Eddie was very crowd friendly, and that gave the stage crew fits. He actually came out into the crowd right in front of me during the song “Porch” and seemed right at home being held upright by several very happy fans.
The two tack on songs to “Daughter” and “All Those Yesterdays” are REM songs, and the Yeastie Girls song was a little rap done by Eddie from a California band he likes.

The full setlist for the Atlanta show is as follows: “Hail Hail,” “Animal,” “Given to Fly,” “Do the Evolution,” “Dissident,” “Corduroy,” “I Got Shit,” “Wishlist,” “Even Flow,” “In Hiding,” “Last Exit, (Yeastie Girls song),” “Off He Goes,” “MFC,” “Better Man,” “Brain of J,” “Alive,” “Go,” “No Way,” “Rearviewmirror,” “Daughter/ (Talk About the Passion)” “(All Those Yesterdays)/(The Wrong Child),” “Black,” “Porch,” and “Yellow Ledbetter.”

The Birmingham show was September 3 at Jefferson Colloseum. Mudhoney was the opener again and got about the same repsone as at the Atlanta show. Eddie and company - Mike, Jeff, Stone, and Matt were at the top of their game again. Jeff and Mike hardly opened their eyes the whole show, and Eddie and Stone danced around and seemed to be having a blast.

They changed the setlist up a bit from the other show. They played the basic songs that were expected, but threw in some surprises like “Footsteps,” Faithful,” “Elderly Woman”, and the b-side “Leatherman.”

The shocker and highlight of the show came at the end when the band, wearing smiles and big silver sunglasses, kicked in an excellent version of the Who classic, “Baba O’Riley.” Eddie ended the show with a Roger Daltrey-like(former Who vocalist) swing of the microphone, leaving it hanging from the lighting fixture above as the band exited the stage.
