August grad lets nothing get in his way

- Office of Public Relations
Going off to college can be scary. Imagine not only going away, but also traveling half way around the world to a place you’ve never been, knowing no one. Imagine again you are Chinese, and your home is Macao, the Portuguese colony that will soon come under Chinese rule. This was the scenario facing Sun I. (Larry) Lei, a graduate student who will receive his master’s in business administration in August.

When Lei graduates in August there will be friendly faces in the crowd: his girlfriend and one of his brothers will fly in from Macao for the ceremony. Afterwards, they will spend about 15 days traveling in the Southeast and up to Washington D.C. before departing for Macao, where Lei hopes to work with young people, perhaps in a junior college setting, while pursuing his CPA certification.

The journey to his master’s has been an unusual one. One of eight siblings, Lei grew up in what he describes as a “poor family.” Typical of such families in Macao, he dropped out of regular classes in 1970 after completing his primary sixth grade in order to work full time to help with family expenses.

Determined to complete his education, he took classes in evening school and received his high school diploma in 1985. While working part time tutoring Cantonese to Southern Baptist missionaries, he obtained an application for Columbus College. In 1986 he was accepted and arrived in March of 1987 knowing nothing about either the school or the city.

Landing in California, he took a two-week bus trip cross-country, “not knowing where” he would end up, but sightseeing on his way to Columbus. His first thought on arrival was, “That’s a city?” Macao is the world’s densest city with tall buildings and half a million people crammed into a very small geographical area.

Thinking “city” as in New York City, Lei arrived on campus in the midst of regular registration - in the days before computerized registration. The cafeteria was filled with tables and people standing in lines, but as Lei laughingly says, he was “exhausted from the long journey and did not want to turn around and go back.”

Surviving the registration confusion, he settled down to classes and graduated magna cum laude in 1990 with a bachelor’s in accounting.

He returned to Macao and worked full time for an evening school while tutoring part-time for the Baptist missionaries.

Following his father’s death in July of 1997, Lei returned to Columbus to pursue a master’s in business administration. In the intervening years Columbus College had become Columbus State University. Lei was struck by physical changes in the campus including the new brick entrances and the now complete clock tower and science building.

Reminiscing about his undergrad days, he recalls cleaning the bricks that now form the clock tower. The campus, although larger in population, remains a friendly community where professors care about the success of their students, and students are friendly and encouraging.

Discussing the differences in educational philosophy between China and the U.S., Lei points out that Chinese schools emphasize memorization and obedience.

The Chinese language is not alphabet-based, but rather is a complex series of brush strokes forming the numerous characters, all of which must be memorized. Here in the states it is more important to be creative, to approach problems from different angles.

While he had little problem memorizing material for classes, he did encounter roadblocks when he first began to apply the facts to situations in order to come up with creative analyses or solutions.

Not the typical master’s program candidate, Lei has gone to classes full time in order to complete the requirements in a year. According to Lei, he chose to return to the states for his masters because new innovations in business come from the U.S. His graduate assistantship in the College of Business has given him valuable insight into the world of business as he learns how the world operates within that sphere.

CSU’s influence truly ranges worldwide, for with Lei’s graduation in August the university can boast of a Macao link, soon to be a link to China.
