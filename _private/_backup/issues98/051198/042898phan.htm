An older phantom speaks out

by Rosa Fennie / Guest Writer
When the reality of being a non-traditional age student really hits a person, everything becomes so frantic and important in life.

Having already wasted so many years and then finally returning to school with people much younger than myself I was at first very intimidated.

It’s also enough to make the most boisterous person clam up, therefore becoming what is called a “Phantom Student.”

Patricia Nelson Limerick, a famous frontier historian, wrote a very informative essay called, “The Phenomenon of Phantom Students,” that was published in a weekly publication for the university community at Harvard. In this essay she defines a Phantom Student as, “The radically disengaged, those staying resolutely on the academic periphery, taking large lecture classes and writing survivalist papers and exams.”

I consider myself a phantom student but the difference between the student’s Limerick speaks of and myself is the fact that I am an older student, what Columbus State University deems necessary to call an Adult Reentry Student.

Returning to college brought joy and excitement into my life and a sense of purpose. I couldn’t wait to get into class and meet new people and my professors.

I thought very naively that college would be different from high school, I just know some of my professors would surely be close to my age and talking to them would be a discussion, not just they talk and me listen, I was wrong.

My first quarter, on the first day of class, the first words I heard were: “I don’t give a damn if you come to class or not as long as you are here on test day. Whether you pass or fail is no concern of mine. My paycheck will still be the same.”

I couldn’t believe what I was hearing. I have no doubt that after hearing what this professor had to say, I had subconsciously committed myself to phantom-hood.

I wanted to be treated differently because I was an older student, I guess I got just what I asked for. I don’t know what the ratio of older to younger is at Columbus State University, but from my view it looks like 5000 to 1. In my classes the older generation takes a back seat.

Not only do we take a back seat in number but in attention also. During my adult reentry classes I took to refresh me on college life, I was led to believe that the professors paid special attention to adult students whenever possible, but I learned this is not so.

It seems as though the professors focus more on the younger students. I came to that conclusion in my first quarter

also, my music appreciation class. The professor would use examples, in order to give better understanding to his students, which would apply to younger students.

For example, he would try to show the difference in the way that Mozart dressed so formally, compared to “grunge, as you kids wear these days.” I didn’t know what grunge was. I had to ask the young person seated next to me, who just looked at me like I had no sense of fashion. So, in the second class of the day phantom-hood prevailed again because I realized that this professor was speaking to the younger students and my first professor doesn’t give a damn.

Speaking with a fellow adult student about being a phantom student, he said, “Maybe we made our selves phantoms.,” I was dumbstruck. When reading Limerick’s theories on phantoms, I had a one-track mind because of past experience. I never added myself into the reasoning but the proof was there in my friend’s words as plain as day: I had done this to myself I should have been more objective instead of being quiet, never asking questions, and being intimidated by the younger students and my professors.

Then after carefully analyzing every aspect of why I had fell into phantom-hood, I realized that I had help becoming this person that was seen and not heard.

All of the blame does not lie with me. My professors aided me in becoming invisible. For example, in one of my classes this quarter there are two younger students. They both always have something to say, one of them generally has good feedback and everyone seems to understand her. The other also gives a lot of feedback but no one understands him. Only the professor seems to grasp exactly what he means. Everyone else just sits with the look of, “What did he just say?”

To these two the professor always states, “Oh, that’s so interesting” or “Oh, that’s so deep.” If I comment, she looks at me like she is dumfounded and just says, “Oh ... Okay.” This brings to mind a passage of Limerick’s essay where she states a number of myths that include, “Professors only want to talk with other elegant, learned and brilliant conversationalists.” I don’t consider myself a brilliant conversationalist, but by the same token neither am I inarticulate.

Even though this professor is a favorite of mine she makes me question my intelligence at least fifty times before the end of class. It would have been better if she would just make up something to say to me instead of, unbeknownst to her, embarrassing me. There have been occasions where I’ve just remained silent for the rest of class for fear of being labeled an old fool.

I’ve realized that there are professors who really care about my academic progress and there are those who don’t. I’ve learned to feed off of the ones who are stimulating my mind the most and take what they’ve told me and use to my advantage in other courses.

Ultimately, what stays with me from Limerick’s essay is when she states ,Professors, like most other individuals, like snowflakes, are all different. That surely is a part of their charm.” I know now that it’s not up to my professor to baby sit me and tell me everything in detail just because I am an older student, nor is it up to them to bring me out of phantom-hood. I believe that just as life is what one makes it so is one’s education. I have four years left before I graduate, from Columbus State College and I intend to make professors see how they can hurt or help someone’s education, especially the older student. Professors need to realize that they have students from all age ranges in their classes not just eighteen and nineteen year-olds. Maybe if I could somehow make them think back to when they were in college and remember the commitment they made to themselves that made them want to be teachers. I really don’t think professors wanted a degree in teaching to alienate students.
