This letter is in response to the one printed in last week’s ROAR! column. I can’t believe someone had the nerve to write some of those things. First of all, let me say that I am a smoker. I sometimes have a cigarette while I am on campus. And I wholeheartedly agree that people should not stand in front of the doors and smoke.

Now that that is out of the way, I just have a few comments . . . 

I am curious to know when Columbus State became a “utopia.” The last time I checked, utopia was defined as “any idealized place, state, or situation of perfection.” So when was the title given to good ol’ CSU? I mean, I don’t hate the university or anything, but I don’t know anyone who is perfectly happy about getting up for classes every day. Or doing that term paper. Or the hours and hours of reading required to make decent grades. And until no one minds doing those things, CSU will not be a utopia. So for those of you waiting, don’t hold your breath.

The author of last week’s column said that adult smokers act “like children who have not learned how to read yet.” What?!? I can read perfectly well. And I can also see very well. There is a gazebo between Howard and Arnold Halls. This gazebo has ashtrays posted around the walls. Usually, when I see an ashtray somewhere, it means smoking is permitted. The same goes with the many ashtrays placed all over the campus. And I ask this to the rule-makers and to the author of last week’s column—Why are there ashtrays placed in front of the doors to buildings if there is to be no smoking close to the building? If students really aren’t supposed to smoke near buildings (“You know, buildings, those tall things you enter to learn everyday?” to quote the anonymous writer), then WHY ARE THE ASHTRAYS THERE???

The author also stated that the older smokers on campus should try to set a good example for the younger generation by not smoking. HELLO! This is college, the land where children grow up. No one comes to college to get bossed around by the students who are older. “Wisdom is supposed to come with age,” the author wrote. “It’s good to want to be on friendly terms with the youthful, but that does not mean that [older people] have to encourage this bad habit to blend in.” The author tells older smokers that we should use common sense to discourage young people from smoking. That is ridiculous. People don’t come to college to hear someone else tell them how to live their lives. College is a time for people to start making their own decisions; they can decide whether to drink, to smoke, to skip class all the time, to do homework, etc. If it is up to anyone to guide theses younger people, it is their parents, not just the older smokers on campus.

I understand that the author of last week’s column “couldn’t care less about your nasty smoking habit,” but they also say it is against the rules. This is not true. It may be against the un-enforced rules to stand right in front of a door and smoke a cigarette, but smoking is permitted on the campus. Keep that in mind next time you catch a whiff of smoke while walking by the gazebo with the ashtrays . . .

I meant no personal offense to anyone in writing this. I just wanted to comment of last week’s article, because I felt some of the points made by the author were totally unfair and irrational. Let’s just remember what I said earlier in this article, we are all adults in this school, at least physically if not mentally. For those who don’t want to deal with smokers go away. We don’t want you around criticizing us any more than you want to be around us when we smoke. There’s a lot of space outside these buildings, and not all of it is full of smokers. If you want some fresh air I suggest you walk a few feet away for the smokers. If that doesn’t help you, Pine Mountain is about 30 minutes away. Take a drive.
