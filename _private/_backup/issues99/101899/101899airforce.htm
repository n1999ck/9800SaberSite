A United States Air Force has developed a new approach to the controversial “Don’t Ask, Don’t Tell” policy of the military.
Lackland Air Force Base in San Antonio, Texas, has been a blemish on Air Force records in the past years, discharging nearly 200 service personnel in one year for declaring their homosexuality. In 1998 Lackland was responsible for 65% of all Air Force discharges on the grounds of homosexuality and comprised nearly one-fourth of the total for all service branches.

In 1999 these numbers decreased after Lackland developed a new program dealing with those service persons who announce their sexual orientation.

The program, which began in early 1999, refers trainees who declare their homosexuality to a defense counsel that advises them of their rights during a confidential meeting. If the trainee recants their declaration within a few days of statement, they are allowed to continue their service in the Air Force and the referral letter is removed from their file. 

The new policy resulted after members of the service members Legal Defense Network (SLDN) visited the base and discovered that many trainees were confused about the older policies. Some trainees believed that they would be discharged if they admitted their homosexuality, while others believed they would be jailed if they did not come out.
Air Force officers were also unsure of the legalities of the policy, believing that they had to report any service members who commented about their sexual orientation. 

The SLDN found that trainees aware of the “Don’t Ask, Don’t Tell” policy were not making statements due to the fear of physical violence and felt that they needed to hide themselves. Jim Wolffe, assistant to the Secretary of the Air Force, stated that it should be made clear to recruits that “not telling under ‘Don’t Ask, Don’t Tell’ is not a violation of Air Force core values.”

The SLDN report also found that the base was home to a hostile climate in slurs against suspected gays by Air Force colleagues. Wolffe stated that anti-gay slurs should not be tolerated, like any other racist remark, but that a serviceman attempting to report such harassment may find it legally challenging. 

According to Wolffe, such a statement should lead to an investigation of the harassment rather than the sexual orientation of the victim, but, as Wolffe admitted, there was a risk of discharge for the those victims who were not careful about their statements. 

In response to the new policy, Lackland’s discharges based on homosexuality decreased from 195 during the period of March through September of 1998, to 31 during the same period in 1999.
