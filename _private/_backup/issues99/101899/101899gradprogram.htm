Columbus State University’s forthcoming Master of Science in Information Technology Management is designed to provide professionals with new insight into changing business systems through topics such as electronic commerce, internet capabilities and management of information technology.

Approved October 13 by the University System of Georgia Board of Regents, the program has been initiated through CSU’s Colleges of Business, Science, Arts and Letters and Synovus Financial Corp., headquartered in Columbus. A $10.5 billion multi-financial services company, Synovus will identify an initial, fall 2000 class of 25 students from its corporate leadership program. Once fully operational and self-sustaining, the new master’s program will open to students from outside Synovus.

A University System of Georgia study identified a need for such a program in an increasing local demand for management-level professionals who are well-informed concerning information technology issues. Columbus’ three major information-processing companies, AFLAC, Blue Cross Blue Shield and the Synovus-controlled Total System Services Inc. (TSYS), employ more than 10,000 people locally. 
The study further projects 3,000 new information technology jobs throughout Georgia over the next several years. CSU associate dean of business Mike Daniels says the program represents a continued coninfittment of the university to meeting regional education needs. “This program bridges the gap that many times exists in technical environments between individuals with undergraduate business training and others with undergraduate technical degrees.”

The new program’s 36-semester-hour curriculum will include a choice between a “management” or “technical” track. The management track emphasizes economics, finance marketing and courses dealing with “decision support” using information technology. The technical track will deliver courses in transaction processing, client-server networking architecture, and security.

CSU has provided rapid-response computer education to local corporations since 1990, and was selected by the state to house Georgia’s first Intellectual Capital Partnership Program (ICAPP) - an undergraduate-level computer science training partnership with TSYS, and cited twice by the White House as a model program in higher education. 

CSU’s four-year-old ICAPP includes curriculum using the most recent Microsoft technologies in teaching the most in-demand computer skills from the ground up.
