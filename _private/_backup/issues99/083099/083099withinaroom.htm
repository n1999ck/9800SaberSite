Within a Room
Leslie Maxwell / Columnist

Lately, it seems as if I can’t get away from one thing ... obscenity. So I thought that I would complain about it a little bit. 

I am all for free speech and free artistic expression, but some of the things I am seeing and hearing are getting on my nerves. Whether it is obscene language or naked people cavorting on prime time television, obscenity is everywhere these days, and the problem is that most of it is absolutely pointless. 

According to one definition, obscenity is something that is created to arouse sexual desire or anger, and it has no redeeming literary, artistic, or scientific value. For instance, no one (except a very disturbed person) is aroused by the statue Venus or by scantily-clad tribal people going about their business on the Discovery Channel. And furthermore, no one gets really hysterical when Rhett Butler says “Frankly, Scarlett, I don’t give a damn.”

On the other hand, some things are entirely unnecessary. A few weeks ago, I went to the movies with some friends. The movie we wanted to see wasn’t playing at the late showing, so we were left with a choice between Run Away Bride and The Thomas Crown Affair. I’m no great fan of “chick-flicks” so we opted for Crown Affair. For those of you who haven’t seen the movie, it is another high-tech thief flick like Entrapment, but not nearly as good. Along with a lot of bad acting and a predictable plot, this movie featured Rene Russo and Pierce Brosnan in one of the longest, most bizarrely filmed sex scenes that I have ever seen. My friends and I were looking at each other and saying things like, “Yuck! When is this torture going to end?” Then, later in the movie, Rene Russo spends lots of time walking around topless for no apparent reason. Someone in the theater might have enjoyed that, but I certainly did not see the point. 

Can’t two people have a conversation fully clothed? I hope this isn’t a weird social phenomena developing - I would hate to have to take off half my clothes in order to have a meaningful conversation with someone. 

Then, of course, there is the subject of verbal obscenity. I have a friend who makes me cringe on a regular basis with his colorful and rather limited vocabulary. For example, he would say something like this: “I beep went to the beep store and they wouldn’t beepin’ sell me any beepin’ cigarettes ‘cause I beepin’ forgot my I.-beepin’-D. Beep it!” (censored version). What is the point of that many curse words? That sentence definitely doesn’t have any artistic, literary, or scientific value. 

The function of curse words, I suppose, is to provide emphasis, but isn’t that function undermined by overuse? For instance, if I say, “I like chocolate very, very, very, very, very, very, very much!” Isn’t that sort of inane? Don’t you get the point after the first “very,” and don’t I sound stupid after the second one? That sentence sounds like it either came from the mouth of a very enthusiastic three-year old or from some one who has nothing between his or her ears but air. No one demonstrates much intelligence by mouthing a sentence that contains more vulgarity than sense. In the words of my foul mouthed friend, “Cuss words are so unin-beepin-ventive!”
