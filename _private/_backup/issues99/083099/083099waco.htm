Waco investigation reopened
Amy Walters / Editor in Chief

[caption] Students gather outside Davidson Center for refreshments offered at the Back to School Bash on Wednesday, August 25. This was one of the many welcome back events that was held last week.

The FBI and the Justice Department announced last week that they plan to open a new investigation into the 1993 standoff with the Branch Davidians at their compound in Waco, Texas. The siege culminated in a fire that killed leader, David Koresh, and over 80 of his followers.

The investigation was ordered by Attorney General, Janet Reno, and FBI Director, Louis Freeh, a day after the FBI confirmed that they fired what may have been potentially flammable tear gas canisters on the final day of the 51 day siege. 

The Bureau insists that they have no evidence that the devices they fired had any cause in starting the fire. 

Former Deputy Assistant FBI Director, Danny Coulson, stated that two pyrotechnic projectiles were fired onto the grounds approximately four hours before the fire began. Coulson said that during the time between the firing of the devices and the start of the fire the devices could not have stayed hot enough to ignite a fire.

This new information has come to light recently after former Branch Davidians and family members of victims filed a wrongful death suit, charging that the FBI used incendiary devices that started the fire at the main building of the compound.

Reno and Freeh have appointed a team of 40 FBI agents to re-interview each person who was at the scene on the final day of the siege.

The FBI is denying claims that they kept this information secret from the public and is pushing the blame towards the Justice Department, which completed the final investigation of the incident.
Reno stated earlier in the week that she was “very, very troubled” at the recent release of information and pledged to “do everything humanly possible to learn all the facts as accurately as possible and make them available to the Congress and public.”

TIMELINE

February 28, 1993
Federal Agents attempt to arrest Branch Davidian leader David Koresh on weapons charges; in the shootout that followed, four federal agents and six Davidians were killed.

April 19, 1993
Federal Agents attempt a siege on the Branch Davidian compound; the main building of the compound catches fire and kills eighty followers including Koresh.

August 26, 1999
Attorney General Janet Reno announces a new investigation into the 1993 standoff.
