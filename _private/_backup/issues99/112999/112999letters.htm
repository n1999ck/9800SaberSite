Dear Editor,

After reading the article “Debate over student activity fees began Tuesday in Supreme Court,” in the Nov. 15 issue of the Saber and the Volume I Issue I of the CSU Freethought News, I began to understand why Scott Southworth and other students at the University of Wisconsin would question how their student activity fees are spent. 

The article “Opinion-by Brian Slack,” in the first issue of the CSU Freethought News, made me wonder if this guy was serious, or perhaps dropped on his head before writing the article. I don’t think Mr. Slack was justified in calling the people of Alabama weak minded because they did not vote to have the lottery. And I definitely believe he went a little over the edge by saying he’s offended by the fact that they’re allowed to breed. My question is, who bred you? Your entire article violates the very reason the Freethought Society was formed. Those people are allowed to feel, think, act, and BE a certain way even if you don’t agree. So who named you the “Oh Educated One” to downplay a group because of their beliefs? 

After attending the first meeting of the Freethought Society, I decided not to be a part of the group because I don’t share their beliefs. But I would never say they’re weak minded and shouldn’t be allowed to breed. It’s one thing to be a freethinker and another to be down right rude, ignorant, and insensitive to others. I did take the liberty of reading the principles and values of the Freethought Society. Don’t you think you’re violating those principles and values by criticizing those who don’t believe the same way you do? As I recall, the very reason for the Freethought Society was to get people to accept those who are different, regardless of if they’re gay, atheist, Oriental or deformed. 
Growing up a Christian black woman in America, I have learned to accept some people and some things as they are. Even when our beliefs are totally different. No, I didn’t grow up in the 60’s, but I did grow up in the 70’s, in a majority white town, in the South where racism was very strong (and still exists). 

I know how it feels to not think, not act and not be like others around you. But I’ve also learned that your life operates by how you choose to accept things. And my brother, you’re doing a very poor job of accepting the fact that some people believe in that higher being called God. Don’t forget that this country was founded on his grounds. And the fact that the CSU Freethought Society probably wouldn’t exist if it were not for the founding of this country, One Nation, Under God, Indivisible, With Liberty and Justice For All. 

The point I’m making is, be who you’re going to be. Although I am a strong believer in God, I would never knock you for not believing, and I would never call you names because you did share my ideas. So keep the [bull] about political crap, fake wrestling, and television not hurting our kids to yourself. 

Because politics has allowed some of us the opportunity to strive for a higher education, wrestling may be fake, but it kills, and television, let’s just say it’s a strong resemblance of people in the world. Like most people pick and choose the people they hang with and who they let their kids be associated with, the same should go for television, because there are people AND KIDS who mimic what they see and hear, on television or in real life. 

Angel Jackson
A Very Concerned Individual

Dear Editor,

This is my first semester on campus, so I read your paper each week. I have found it a useful source of information and entertaining reading. I’ve especially enjoyed the Entertainment Page. Today, while I quite enjoyed reading about Van Halen, I was very distressed to read the section entitled “Fun Facts Found on the Internet.” In fact, I found it quite offensive. 

How is perpetuating negative and outdated stereotypes about women entertaining? Some will respond, “Chill out, it’s just a joke.” Unfortunately, our jokes reflect our attitudes – an important point to remember the next time you tell or laugh at a sexist, racist, or ethnic joke.

Elizabeth Winkler
