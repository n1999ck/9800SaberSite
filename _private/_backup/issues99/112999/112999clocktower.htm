You probably pass it every day on your way to and from classes, but chances are you have never stopped to consider what it stands for. It stands at a lofty seventy feet tall and its chimes can be heard resonating throughout the campus every half-hour, but you have probably never pondered its significance. 

This remarkable thing is, of course, Columbus State University’s own clock tower, which stretches far above any other CSU landmarks and has become CSU’s most popular trademark symbol.

As a result of the feeling that the school lacked some type of central focus, CSU’s clock tower was built as a monument to the university’s long, rewarding journey from a small, makeshift college held in an abandoned factory to an ever-expanding and architecturally exquisite university. 

In a ceremony held on September 20, 1991, the clock tower was dedicated to Thomas Y. Whitley, Columbus College’s first president. Whitley is often called the “father of Columbus College” because of the enormous contribution he made to the college’s creation and success.

Even the very bricks themselves with which the tower was built contain their own rich store of CSU’s unique history. The tower’s dark red bricks are actual bricks from the abandoned factory in which CSU found its humble beginnings.

In 1958, the Shannon Hosiery Mill building on Talbotton road became Columbus College when it was purchased by the school board. It remained there until January of 1962 when it was moved to its present site. The red bricks salvaged from the old mill building and included in the clock tower sit as an ever-present reminder of CSU’s incredible progress. Costing approximately $250,000, building the clock tower was an enormous project that required the cooperation of Columbus College alumni, faculty, and students, as well as the community. Neil Fenter, a theatre teacher here at CSU, recalls that “the whole creation of it was quite an event on this campus.”

Something which relatively few people know about the clock tower is that it contains a time capsule which is to be opened on May 14, 2058, the 100th anniversary of Columbus College. 

Thomas Loughman, who was then an assistant professor in the school of business, came up with the idea for including some type of time capsule in the tower. 

President Brown liked the idea and formed a committee, which was chaired by Craig Lloyd, the college archivist, to put the project together.
The time capsule contains items such as photographs of the 1991 Columbus College faculty and staff, a course catalog, a map of Columbus, a copy of the Saber, several brochures and pamphlets, items from the bookstore, and a cafeteria price list to name a few. 

These items are all contained within an aluminum capsule that is three feet long and twelve inches in diameter. The capsule rests in a cavity in the very bottom center of the clock tower and is marked by a star-like, inscribed capstone.

CSU faculty and students alike recognize the unifying element that the tower brings to the campus. “It’s a metaphor in my mind,” says Fenter, who feels the clock tower is both “a symbol of the school” and representative of “our common bond.” 

ikewise, communications professor, Fred Mc Donald says, “It strikes me as being a kind of focal point for the campus.”

Sophomore Josh Heaton simply says, “It’s a monument to us all.”

Certainly the commemorative plaque beside the clock tower sums it up best with the statement that the tower stands to “commemorate the past, illuminate the present, and celebrate the future of Columbus College and its impact on the community.”
