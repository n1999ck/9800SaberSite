The School of the Americas in Fort Benning, Georgia was once again the subject of harsh criticism this weekend as protesters marched onto the federal property demanding that the school be closed. 

Members of the SOA Watch held their annual march on the army base, demanding the closure of the school. According to the Watch, the SOA trains soldiers who have contributed to violence and murder in the Latin American countries. SOA vehemently denies these theories.

Protesters began gathering Saturday afternoon on the grounds just outside the federal property line, searching for parking, finding old friends, and preparing themselves for the march to occur on Sunday. 

By Sunday morning an estimated 10,000 people were gathered outside the gate to Fort Benning. But not all those gathered were protesters- forty-six police officers were members of the crowd, keeping the peace between the protesters and military officials.

In addition to the police, about 500 peacekeepers were visible in the crowd. The peacekeepers, members of the SOA Watch, are designated to keep peace during the protest. 

According to Paddy Inman, who has been protesting SOA since 1996 and is director of the SOA Watch peacekeepers, stated, “We’re there to remind each other that we are a peaceful people and to remind everyone that this is a solemn occasion.” 

To teach everyone attending the protest keep the event peaceful, protesters are asked to take courses in non-violence. SOA Watch has a manual which is available to protesters which outlines the principles of the group, including: We will refrain from insults and swearing, I will carry no weapons, I will damage no property, or use our anger at injustice as a positive nonviolent force for change. 

Peacekeeper classes are more inclusive, including lectures on the principles of Jesus, Ghandi, and Martin Luther King, Jr. Classes also teach protesters rules of the law and what the consequences are if they broach the line and risk arrest. 

Sunday afternoon, the protest reached its peak as over 3,000 people crossed the line onto the federal property of Fort Benning. The march began as Father Roy Bourgeois, the founder of SOA Watch, said to the crowd, “let the solemn funeral procession begin.”

Those marching down the street ranged in age from babies being pushed by their parents to senior citizens in their eighties. Some were returning to once again voice their beliefs, while others were new to the protest.

Those protestors that crossed onto the federal property were picked up by military buses, driven to Rigdon Park, and released to walk back to the demonstration site. Twenty three protestors who were previously banned from the base may now face charges of federal trespassing and could be subject to a six month jail sentence. 

The SOA Watch protests began in 1990 when Bourgeois founded the group. Bourgeois, when speaking about the protest and its impact, stated that “we are gathered at Fort Benning to speak for those who can’t speak for themselves.”

This year’s demonstration honored six Jesuit priests, their housekeeper and her daughter who were killed ten years ago in El Salvador by SOA graduates.

The School of the Americas was founded in 1946 to train soldiers and military personnel from Latin America. The school was first established in Panama as the US Army Caribbean Training Center. Under John F. Kennedy’s Alliance for Progress in 1963 the training center was renamed as the School of the Americas. The school moved to Georgia in 1984 under the Panama Canal Treaties. 

Earlier this year Army Secretary Louis Caldera confirmed that he is considering a proposal to change the school’s name to the Center for Inter-American Security Cooperation. If accomplished, more civilian students from Latin America will be trained at the school, some combat courses will be dropped, and the authority over the school will be switched from the Army to the Department of Defense.

According to Bourgeois, even if these changes occur, the SOA Watch will still continue its protests against the school.
