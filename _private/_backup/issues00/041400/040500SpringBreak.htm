Spring Break in Saint Augustine not what we expected!
Tricia / Guest Writer

We knew Panama City would be packed. So, my friend Kathy and I (who are admittedly self-help junkies) wanted a more serene atmosphere in which to unwind and ponder the meaning of our existence. I suggested one of my favorite little cities: Saint Augustine, Florida. Founded in 1565 by the Spanish, it is the oldest city in the United States. It’s quaint little beaches and rich history make it the perfect place for quiet contemplation. We envisioned sunrise walks on the beach, afternoons antiquing, and leisurely days meandering through the galleries and cafés within the Spanish Quarter. But as we drove east on route 10, ominous signs of a different destiny unfolded.
Riding along in the passenger’s seat, I began to notice a number of Harleys rumbling by. (One of the loudest, most obnoxious sounding vehicles ever invented by man.). 

As we stopped at rest points along the way, I watched the flow of bikers roll in and out: eastward, descending upon Florida in small, shiny chrome flocks. It could mean only one thing—a biker’s convention.

I tried to breach the subject gently to friend., “Kathy . . . it appears they are hosting a ‘bike week’ somewhere in Florida.”

“Mmmm,” she said rather gloomily. “Well, I do recall an annual bike week in Daytona.” We looked at each other hopefully.

Just then, a huge tractor trailer, dressed in black and brandishing a giant Harley Davidson logo plowed by. 

Daytona . . .they must be going to Daytona.

We remained in our blissful state of denial until we reached the outskirts of Saint Augustine. When we saw the first banner proclaiming, “WELCOME BIKERS” we released a unified cry of alarm: “Oh God! No!” 

They were invaded my city. My peaceful, dignified city. Upon arriving, Kathy hopefully questioned convenience store clerks, local police officers, hotel clerks, and finally . . . the bikers themselves. 
But each in turn confirmed her fear: “Yes ma’am, It’s annual bike week in Daytona.”

“Extending all they way here into Saint Augustine?” she pursued in disbelief.

“Yes ma’am.”

We were mortified.

We decided to make our best effort, to ignore the intrusion, and carry on. We had come to Saint Augustine in search of self-clarification, and found instead—bikers. Being self-help junkies, we attempted to “learn from the experience.” 

As I walked along the street one day, I began to realize that the big majestic bikes that rolled buy were not merely Harleys. 

Just one of the many bikes parked at the center of Saint Augustine carried a retail value higher than the cost of my entire college education! These were not merely bikers. 

As Kathy and I ate in a tiny bistro, she observed two burly bikers, and matching leather attire daintily eat a green salad, then leave. No obnoxious table manners, or piles of fatty foods. 

As we walked past a rather expensive gallery I noticed a sign which read, “Attention Bikers: Free Shipping.” 

Could that possibly be correct? Bikers, in t-shirts and bandanas, patronizing extravagant galleries? One hotel sign even read “Welcome Bikers: AARP accepted.” (American Association of Retired Persons) 
AARP? Bikers? Do they belong in the same category? Apparently so. I quickly began to realize these “bikers” were working and retired professionals: doctors, lawyers, corporate businessmen! Kathy even commented on a surprising a pair of ladies at breakfast one morning, “what an interesting combination of leather and designer cosmetics.” These were not the rowdy hoodlums we had anticipated.

As we returned to our room before checking out, I began to realize the “lesson” of our experience. This influx of unlikely individuals forced me to confront a hidden part of myself. I was prejudiced. Just because we do not fit into racist or sexiest stereo-types does not mean that we do not hold preconceived notions, which is the heart of all prejudice.
