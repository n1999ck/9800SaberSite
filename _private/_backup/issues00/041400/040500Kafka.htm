The Sons by Franz Kafka
Chad Wayne / News Editor

It’s time to expand the literary field of your education. But don’t worry, this is not an assignment. And you know that a book is twice as fun to read when you read it of your own accord. 

The book I would like to suggest to all the CSU bookworms is The Sons by Franz Kafka. This is all around, a great book. For starters, it only costs $8.50 for the paperback edition if you buy it from Amazon.com.

It’s also a pretty small book, perfect for throwing in your duffel to read while you’re at the beach catching some rays.

The Sons is a collection of short stories, which can be understood independently of each other but share some similar themes. The four short stories include, “The Judgment,” “The Stoker,” “The Metamorphosis” and “Letter To His Father.”

The first story, “The Judgment” is about a man named Georg Bendemann, who is engaged to be married but must consider what to do about his ill and aging father. Early in the story, an argument erupts between the father and Georg. The argument ends with the father declaring his judgement on Georg.

This is an interesting tale that presents the reader with a web of Christian symbolism (Kafka was Jewish), Freudian psychology and peek into Kafka’s feelings toward his own father.

“The Stoker” is about 16-year-old boy named Karl who is sent to America because he has gotten a servant girl pregnant. The boy comes to America and must find his way around this unfamiliar country. Oddly enough, when the boy sales into New York, the Statue of Liberty is holding a sword in her raised arm, not a torch.

“The Metamorphosis” is probably the most famous work by Franz Kafka and is in some of the English textbooks that are used here at CSU.

The story begins with the main character Gregor Samsa waking one morning only to find that he has been transformed into a bug. There is some speculation as to what kind of bug Gregor is, but it most people agree that he is some kind of beetle.

Because he can’t get dressed or even speak, Gregor ends up being late for work. And when his family sees him, they each have a different response to their giant beetle relative.

Again, there is Christian symbolism in this story, particularly of Christ. There is also much Freudian psychology and some revelations about Kafka’s relationship with his father, mother and closest sister.

“Letter To His Father” is a key to understanding the preceding stories in the book. However, this one should be saved to read last, for it is best to analyze your first impression of a story so as not to be influenced by someone else’s suggestions.

This story is in fact a letter that Kafka wrote to his father. He never intended it to be published and he never actually gave it to his father. However, once Franz and his father were both dead, Kafka’s editor had the letter published.

“Letter To His Father” sheds light on many of the relationships between family members in his stories, but it does little to shed light on the religious symbolism.

Many people have wondered why Kafka used Christian symbolism in his stories. Kafka’s family was Jewish, but Franz was not an active participant in Judaism. Maybe you can figure out why he does this when you read The Sons. 

Why were these four short stories chosen for this book? Franz Kafka seemed to believe that these stories belonged together. He wrote to his editor in 1913 “I have only one request: ‘The Stoker,’ ‘The Metamorphosis,’ and ‘The Judgement’ belong together, both inwardly and outwardly. There is an obvious connection among the three.” 
Then, of course, “Letter To His Father” was added to the collection because it obviously illuminated major topics in the stories.

Kafka is known for his bizarre stories that often involve characters that seem to behave irrationally. But there is a method to his madness. The joy of reading Kafka is figuring out his rational behind the seemingly irrational characters.

This book is not usually carried in local bookstores, so surf on over to www.amazon.com and order your copy of The Sons.
