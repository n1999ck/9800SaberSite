New campus organization offers place of awareness, support
Jessica Trenchik / Features Editor

Pride, which officially formed in March of this year, is one of the newest organizations here at CSU. The organization was begun by Amy Walters, who now serves as president. Simply put, Pride is an organization geared toward the homosexual community and its supporters. Membership in the group is open to anyone who wants to offer their personal, professional, or academic support, regardless of their personal sexual orientation.

According to Walters, the group is for “any person who is aware of the gay community and would like to be involved and support it.”
According to Vice President Gerald Gibson, membership in Pride exists on many levels. “We’ve also tried to make it very clear that if you’re a homosexual and you don’t want anyone to know then it’s okay,” he states.

All of the members of Pride’s executive council agree that they welcome any person and that many people have come out to support the group who are not themselves homosexual.

“It’s just a place for people to meet each other,” says Shelley Mock, Pride’s secretary.

Gibson also points out that the members of Pride represent every different religious background, from “atheist to born again Christian,” he says.

In general, the organization is pleased with the turnout they have received so far.

According to Walters, attendance at meetings has ranged all the way from eight to twenty people.

“I was totally surprised when I walked into the first meeting,” says Lois Lummus, Pride’s treasurer, on the turnout.

Activities vary from meeting to meeting. So far, the meetings have consisted of activities such as guest speakers such as the group’s advisor, Dan Rose, as well as discussions about issues dealing with the gay lifestyle.

“We really wanted this to be a place where you can talk about anything,” says Gibson.

Gibson says that once the group becomes better acquainted they can identify their individual problems and bring in the necessary resources to help and to educate one another.

Upcoming activities include a picnic on May 6.

The forming of Pride did not go without its opposition here on campus.
“I think it’s sad,” says Gibson about the opposition that Pride faced during its formation. “I think it was very expected and I think it will die off.”

“Opposition to any group on campus is going to exist,” says Walters. “It’s not really fazing us because we haven’t had any problems.”
Both Walters and Gibson want to stress the point that Pride was not formed to anger anyone. The group, to them, is simply a response to the need for a way for the homosexual community to be able to come together and be a positive, involved influence on campus.

The group is as of yet still unsure as to whether they will continue to meet over the summer. The group does plan to continue in the fall. Pride holds its meetings almost every Tuesday at 12:30 p.m. upstairs in Davidson. The group always welcomes new members and email questions or comments at their address, CSUpride@hotmail.com.
