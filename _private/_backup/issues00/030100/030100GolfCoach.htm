Dr. Earl Bagley 61st inductee into Golf Coach Hall of Fame
Matthew Young / Guest Writer

Dr. Earl Bagley, Vice President of Student Affairs and Dean of Students, was inducted into the College Golf Coaches Association of America Hall of Fame on Feb. 3, 2000 in Orlando, FL. Dr. Bagley was the 61st inductee into the Hall, and only the third coach from a Georgia to receive such an honor.

Dr. Bagley took over the Columbus College Golf Team in 1984 when the team finished fifth in NCAA Division II. 

Over the next four years, the team placed in the top three, and in 1989, the team won the Division II title. 

The team again did well in 1990, and 1991, but Dr. Bagley again helped the team to another Division II title in 1992. 

In 1994, Dr. Bagley stepped down as head coach and became an assistant. 

The Golf Team once again won the Division II title in 1994 and again in 1997.

Unlike other sports such as basketball and football, most of the coaching in golf is done in practice and off the course. 

As head coach, Dr. Bagley was responsible for recruiting, managing, scheduling, and serving as a sort of father figure to the golf team members. Throughout his tenure as coach, Dr. Bagley wanted to attract good golfers to the program, but he made sure that they wanted to be successful as students. He gives thanks to his “swing guru” Hugh Royer, a former pro golfer for helping the team with its mechanics.

One of the things that Dr. Bagley tried to impress upon his players was the use of “positive images and thoughts on the course. Self motivation is very important because of the long hours involved in practice.” 

“For golf was not intended to be a fair sport, and it is easy to get discouraged on the course. Golf is a year round sport. The key to college golf, as in professional golf, is to be consistent. Fairways to greens and then two putts. If you get a birdie, great, but be consistent,” says Dr. Bagley.

One of his toughest challenges though was to “take away the excuses” from his players. 

He continued by stating, “Players will sometimes find reasons not to be successful. It is easy to get on top, but it is much harder to stay there.”

Dr. Bagley confessed that he is a “high 70’s to mid 80’s” golfer, and that he was lucky to have people to help “fine tune” his players. 

He said that there was a big difference between being a manager and being a leader. You can be a manager without being a leader and you can be a leader without being a manager. It is evident that Dr. Bagley is both.
