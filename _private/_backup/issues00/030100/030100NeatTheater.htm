XI THETA SORORITY

 

 

Neat Theater Company offers new approach to performance
Jessica Trenchik / Features Editor

What do you get when a group of young, aspiring actors come together to form a theater company with no permanent home or set rules that’s dedicated to exploring and experimenting with the Theatre Arts? 

You get the Neat Theatre Company, a repertory company that founder Troy Heard calls “an equivalent of a theatrical garage band.”

In 1996, when Heard was a sophomore at Columbus State University, he and a group of friends wanted to get together and do a play over the summer. 

This event was the birth of the Neat Theatre Company.
Since then, Neat has undertaken a myriad of projects. The company has done everything from interactive theatre to weekly improv performances.

Heard created Neat as a type of “interim” for himself. He wasn’t ready to go directly into teaching with the degree in Theatre Education he received from CSU. 

So, “instead of just sitting around working at Burger King,” Heard has been doing what he loves while getting hands-on experience and building a resume.

Heard’s role in Neat has mostly been that of a director. In fact, for a long while, Heard was “deathly afraid of acting.” Taking Oral Interpretation with Neal Fenter here at CSU is what got him past this fear. 

“He’s a big influence on my lif, and he probably doesn’t even know,” Heard says. “Because of him I enjoy acting and getting up in front of people.”

Neat is made up of what Heard lovingly refers to as a “rag-tag group of wanna-be professional actors” and operates as what is called a repertory company. 

That is to say that they cast all of their shows from a set group of actors within the company.

Neat operates in this manner for two reasons. 

First, according to Heard, they did hold open auditions at first but did not receive a big turnout. 

Secondly, and more importantly, it gives the actors “a chance to grow as performers because they try a variety of roles.”

Even though Neat does not have a formal, written mission statement, Heard says Neat’s mission is basically to “strive for quality and to go beyond what’s expected.”

Neat has dedicated itself to doing show such as Four Dogs and a Bone, which would not always be acceptable in a more conventional community theatre atmosphere. 

Basically, Neat wants to expose audiences to those scripts and performances which, for whatever reason, do not have a place in any other theatre in the area.

Heard feels that not having a permanent home, set rules, or concrete audience expectations has been a real advantage for Neat. He feels it has “allowed them to experiment.”

Similarly, not receiving any type of government funding for the arts has allowed Neat tremendous freedom. 

“Whatever [money] comes from one show pays bills and goes into the next,” says Heard. 

This allows them to do the type of experimental, avant-garde shows that are at the very core of Neat.

As previously stated, Neat does not have a permanent home. “We’ve been all over this town,” Heard jokes. 

This, too, benefits Neat’s refreshingly unconventional approach. Heard feels it is a bonus to always have new and different spaces to work in, rather than being limited to the dimensions of a particular stage. 

“I like breaking down the wall,” Heard says, meaning he likes having the audience no more than four feet from the actors.

Neat is currently on a hiatus until this summer. However, they will be back in business in June doing a production of the comedy Some Things You Should Know Before the World Ends.

Unfortunately, this summer will see the end of the Neat Theatre Company. Heard will then be working toward attending grad school at FSU where he will study filmmaking.

The good news, however, is that Heard says, “We’re going to end with a big blast.” 

Even though Heard says he can not formally announce it, Neat is hoping to do The Rocky Horror Picture Show as a grand finale. Heard say the play is “wild and crazy, which is what Neat is all about.”

In addition to his work with Neat, Heard has also been working with The Human Experience Theatre since he was a sophomore in high school. One thing Heard believes is that “Theaters should be able to support each other.”

“I think a spirit of camaraderie is more important than a spirit of competition,” he says.
