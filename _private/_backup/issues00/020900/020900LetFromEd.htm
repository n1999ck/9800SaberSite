Underage drinking too easy in Columbus
A Letter from the editor
Clarissa Andrews / Co-Editor

All right, Stephanie and I survived our first week of editing the Saber together in spite of a few unforeseeable problems... now comes the time when I have to write a real editorial. I’ve thought a lot this week about what to write—I’m more used to the technical side of putting out a paper—and I finally came up with an idea. Some of you are probably not going to appreciate what I have to say, but I’m hoping you will at least see my point.

Friday night, I was at a local bar watching my boyfriend’s band play. While I was there, I noticed a sign that said something to the extent of, “You must be 21 to enter after 9 p.m. Tues. thru Sun. Proper ID required.” Not being 21 yet, I was a little worried that I would be asked to leave before the band even got started. I wasn’t there to drink; I just wanted to hear the music.

Well, there was evidently no need for me to worry at all—no one bothered to check my ID the whole night. Nor did they bother to check the ID of anyone else I was sitting with, all of whom came in the club after 9 p.m.. Most of the people I was with were over 21, so it didn’t really matter. 

So I sat there watching some of my friends who still have a little while to go before their 21 birthday getting a little more than tipsy. As I watched them, I realized (not for the first time) how easy it is to buy alcohol in this town even if you’re not of legal drinking age.
Before I go any further, I want to clear up a couple of things. First of all, some of my friends who were at the bar with me the other night told me I shouldn’t write this editorial. They said that with all the ordinances that are already being passed in Columbus lately (you know, no alcohol or food served in places that offer exotic dancers, etc.) that I would just make the members of city council want to crack down even harder on underage drinkers. And I guess there’s a chance that it could happen. If it does, I hope all you underage drinkers out there will forgive me.

Another thing you should know is that I’m not big on the whole drinking thing. I’m not going to be holier than thou and tell you that I’ve never had a drink in my entire life, because that would be a lie. But I don’t go bar-hopping in Columbus flirting with the male bartenders in order to be able to get a drink. And I don’t have a really bad fake ID with a picture of a blonde, blue-eyed girl looking nothing like I do and try to pass as her (“Oh, um, I lost 15 pounds, dyed my hair dark, got contacts, and grew three inches. And then I got amnesia, so that’s why I can’t remember my address and my license number.”) The last thing I should warn you of is that it’s only going to be two more months before I’m 21, so I don’t really care if they pass an ordinance shutting down every bar that serves underage drinkers.

I’m pretty sure that there are a number of angry readers out there at this point. But I’ll tell you why that doesn’t worry me. My brother is 17 years old, and he’s already telling me stories of how drunk he got last weekend, and how hard he partied on New Year’s Eve. Now that worries me. When it’s easy for a 17-year-old kid to purchase, there’s a big problem with the way the laws work.

By the time I left the club the other night, there was a stack of about 12 shot glasses, tons of beer bottles, and a couple of mostly-empty margaritas on our table. No one there was carded the entire night.
It was really surprising to me to discover how easy it is to drink underage in this town. And I honestly can’t believe nothing is being done to stop it. Let’s think about it for a minute . . . which makes more sense? Passing a law saying that a forty-year-old man can’t enjoy a beer while at the Platinum Club, or enforcing the law that says 17-year-olds aren’t allowed to purchase or consume alcohol.
