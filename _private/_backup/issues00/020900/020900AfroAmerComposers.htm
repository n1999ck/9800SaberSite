Recital to honor African American composers
Chad Wayne / News Editor

On Friday, February 4, students of the CSU music department presented a recital that was “In celebration of contributions by African American Musicians” as was stated at the top of the program. Works were performed by such African American composers as William Grant Still, Florence Price, and Kirby Shaw. 

The performance lasted approximately an hour and included exerps from Still’s opera, Troubled Island, and some Dixieland music played by Columbus State University’s Hot 7 Dixieland band.

Besides Opera type performances, an ensemble performed “Ride On,” a black spiritual by Kirby Shaw. Probably the loudest applause was given up for Hot 7. 

They seemed to carry the people away with their mind possessing Dixieland tunes. Weather they wanted to or not, every spectator was tapping their feet with the rhythm.

The music performed at the recital was rich with African American heritage. Florence Price’s “Song to the Dark Virgin” (sung by Stephanie Blocker) contains both feminist and African American pride themes according to Sharon Bennett of Capital University. 
The work is a celebration of the elegance and beauty of the female body, and specifically, the African American female body. 

The song begins with, “Would that I were a jewel, a shattered jewel, that all my shinning brilliants might fall at thy feet, thou dark one.”
The words of Price’s song were written by renowned Langston Hughes. Hughes was the first African-American to earn his living as a writer. He received a scholarship to Lincoln University where he earned his bachelor degree. 

Before her performance, Stephanie Blocker said of Langston Hughes, “He wanted to tell the stories of his people without personalizing them, so the reader could step in and draw his own conclusions.”

Hughes also wrote the lyrics for William Grant Still’s “Little Black Slave Girl” which was sung by Shelvia Ivey. 

Still was influenced greatly by his mother and stepfather who both had a passion for the arts. Carrie Still (William’s mother) was an English teacher and his stepfather was a railway postal clerk.

William Still became known as the “Dean of Afro-American Composers.” His accomplishments included nine operas, five symphonies, four ballets and many compositions for voice and instruments. 

Still was the first African American to compose a symphony that was performed by a leading orchestra. He was also the first African American to conduct a major orchestra in the United States when he conducted the Los Angeles Philharmonic in 1936.

The performing students of last Friday’s recital certainly selected some of the best of African American music. As you can see, the recital contained a wide variety of music – from opera to Dixieland.
