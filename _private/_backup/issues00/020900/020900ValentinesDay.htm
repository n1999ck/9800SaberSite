History of Valentine’s Day, a holiday for lovers
Jessica Macon / Staff Writer

Valentine’s Day, the day when people pass on symbols of their feelings to loved ones . . . in modern times it is deemed a day of great emotional expression. However, the true origin of Valentine’s Day is nowhere near as simple. There is no single explanation behind this holiday’s origin, but there is one that is favored. Some of you may know about the story of St. Valentine. 

In the year 269 A.D., a Roman priest by the name of Valentine was sentenced to death by the Roman emperor, Claudius II. The man was condemned for aiding other priests and citizens in the process of conducting marriages which was outlawed by Cladius. 

He believed that without marriages, the males of the Empire would be less reluctant to go to war. Valentine was jailed for his efforts, yet gained a good friend in the jailer’s daughter. Upon his death, it is claimed that he left her a note signed “From Your Valentine.” February 14, the day we celebrate as this holiday of joy, was originally associated with February 15, a Roman celebration called the Feast of Lupercalia. 

The man from which Valentine’s Day got its name died on February 14, 269 A.D.. The day, however, carries on his name as a tribute to him by his fellow priests.

Nowadays, the loving spirit of this joyous holiday still remains and carries on the traditinon of giving valentines. However, the said valentines are more in the forms of candy, a card, perfume or anything that would help people extend their hearts to their loved ones.
Now that you know the history behind Valentine’s Day, let’s look at what the student body of CSU thinks of it. In addition to this article, a poll was done of some of our students. 

Special efforts were made to get a wide variety of age groups in order to cover the ENTIRE population of CSU. It was divided among the males and females. The question posed to each side was “What would you like for Valentine’s Day?” 

The preset options were: flowers and candy, dinner and a movie, jewelry and a card, clothing, and other, in the event that the choices did not match the person.
