Guest French horn player visits campus
Chad Wayne / News Editor

On Mon., March 27, 200 at 7:30 Jean Rife gave a stunning performance with her French horn in the Davidson Auditorium. Jean Rife is currently on the faculties at the New England Conservatory, Massachusetts Institute of Technology, University of New Hampshire and the Longy School of Music. 

Rife earned her Bachelor of music at the Oberlin Conservatory and her Masters in liberal arts in psychology at Harvard University. 

First, Rife played a traditional piece by Schumann - Adagio and Allegro, Op. 70. Next she played with a tape accompaniment called Fantasy for Horn and Tape by Kurt Stallman. The composer used recordings of other French horns to create a synthesized ensemble. 
“The Fantasy tells a story,” explains Rife. “At the beginning of the piece, the horns are all friends. Then they have a fight and by the end of the song they are no longer speaking to each other.”

Next, Jean Rife played Two Pieces for Horn Alone by her husband, Martin Pearlman who is the conductor of the Boston Baroque Orchestra. The second piece in the Two Pieces was titled “Tomorrow Morning’s Review.” 

Before performing this piece, Rife explained that the piece is a spoof on the music critics. 

“I don’t know how your critics are down here, but in Boston they’re not very polite,” she commented.

“Tomorrow Morning’s Review” was a hysterical performance containing guttural tones that sounded more like a cow in labor than a horn and air with no tone being blown through the horn. She even took out the mouthpiece and played a few measures without the rest the horn.

After intermission, Jean Rife, Patricio Cobos, professor of violin and viola at CSU, and Sue Ruby, a staff accompanist, performed a trio on the French horn, violin and piano respectively. 

On Tuesday Rife taught two classes that were open to CSU students. The first class, Yoga For Musicians was at 12:30. In this class, Jean focused on posture and breathing. She opened the class with the statement “Yoga and music – what could be more natural?”

Rife listened as Alberto Sanchez played his violin. By watching him, she realized that Sanchez was tense in his back. 

Rife showed Sanchez proper posture and then gave him an exercise that consisted of bending over, holding a small ball in his abdomen. 
The exercise released some of the tension in Alberto’s lower back and gave him better posture.

Next, Rife listened to Erin Burns, a Voice major, sing for a few minutes. Burns complained that sometimes she felt tense and inefficient, especially on stage. Again, Rife focused on posture. 
Once she showed Erin proper posture, she also spoke about breathing – giving Erin and the rest of the class exercises that encouraged the use of the entire lungs.

Jean Rife also engaged the rest of the class in exercises in breathing and posture. She later taught a master class for all horn players.
