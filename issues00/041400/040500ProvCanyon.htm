How to prevent another Providence Canyon
Chad Wayne / News Editor

Ever been to Providence Canyon – that massive sandpit just about an hour south of Columbus? 

Believe it or not, Providence Canyon used to be a flat tract of land and was used for farming purposes. 

It was, in fact, the poor farming techniques exercised on the land that caused the formation of the Canyon. Had the farmers of the late 1800’s known about no-till and contour planting, Providence canyon may never have been formed.

Elias Groff, a farmer in Lancaster County, Pennsylvania, owned a farm of about 350 acres in a small Mennonite community. For years, Elias and his father before him, had been experiencing severe erosion problems. 

Elias was one of the first people in the mid 1900’s to begin contour planting. This is a method in which the rows of crops follow the contour of the land, rather than straight, up and down the hillsides. 
By using contour planting, the rows of crops act as barriers to water that wants to flow in streams down the hillsides.

Contour planting has helped, but it hasn’t eliminated the erosion problem. Today, Steve Groff, Elias’ son, is a pioneer in farming techniques, just as his father once was. 

Steve is incorporating a method called “no-till planting” and has become a nationally known authority on the topic.

These same practices are being used in Georgia and Alabama. 
Michael Hubbs, an agronomist with the National Resources Conservation Service and who is based in Auburn, AL, stated in an interview that, “Participation in no-till farming in this state is spotty.”
Hubbs says that you can drive for hours and not see any no-till farming and then there are areas where everyone is doing it. Hubbs says that there is a higher concentration of no-till farming in northern Alabama, near Florence.

As you all know, this region is infamous for it’s red clay, and the further south you go in Georgia and Alabama there is more sand mixed with that clay. This is a problem since sand and clay are poor soils in which to maintain nutrients and to resist soil erosion. The conventional form of farming is to till under the spent crops at the end of every season and to plant new crops the following year. 

The problem with this, however, is that it leaves the ground exposed to the elements and the loose tilled soil is susceptible to high levels of erosion.

The no-till method of farming doesn’t prescribe any tilling. Rather, at the end of the growing season, the crops are knocked down and a cover crop is plated in the fields. Good cover crops are not taxing on the soil. 

In other word, the cover crop does not use much of the soil’s nutrients and provides cover for the ground, protecting it from the elements and resisting erosion. Hubbs says that some good cover crops include Black Oats and Rye and says that they are lethalpathic as well. 
Which means they have a property in them that kills some weeds. 
In the spring, the cover crop is knocked down and the new crop is planted for the growing season. When these crops are planted there is no tilling involved. Instead, special planters use balde-like disks to slice into the soil. 

Then the planter drops the seed into the slit in the ground and a weighted wheel follows behind and covers up the slit.

Hubbs claims that “Long term research shows equal to better yields. It really shows up in the low rain years.” What that means is that tilled soil will not retain water as well as soil that hasn’t been tilled. 

In a no-till field, there is a network of the old root systems and a thick layer of organic material on the surface, protecting the soil from erosion and dehydration.

In fact, this past year has been one of the driest ever in Pennsylvania. 75-year-old natives were saying that they never saw a draught so bad in their entire lives. 

Many crops of corn never grew ears on them, but instead, wasted away in the fields only to be tilled under at the end of the summer. 
However, while his neighbors contemplated recovering from this devastating draught, Steve Groff was still producing good yields from his no-till fields. 

So why don’t more people in the Georgia and Alabama switch to no-till?

Well, Mike Hubbs says that better technology is just now becoming available to farmers. 

Besides, people are slow to change. No-till is still one of those “new fangled ideas.” 

Nevertheless, no-till could be the savior for agriculture in the Southeast.To find out more about no-till farming, you can visit Steve Groff’s websight at www.cedarmeadowfarm.com or visit www.age.uiuc.edu/age221/project/1996/team11/titleb.html or www.flexicoil.com/company/s-today/conserv_notill_2.html.
