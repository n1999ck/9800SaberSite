Diversity week celebrated
Jessica Trenchik / Features Editor

March 20th through the 24th, CSU celebrated its 3rd annual Diversity Week. 

CSU’s international students set up displays in the Columbus Room to educate the Columbus community about their respective countries and cultures.

The exhibits were open for viewing everyday during the week from 12:00 p.m. to 2:00 p.m. They can be viewed at other times by special request.

“We have 15 or more international students that do exhibits for the week,” says Cathy Anderson, assistant dean of students, who is also the international advisor.

According to Anderson, the exhibits consist of “artifacts, their culture, things that they bring from home.”

In the past, the exhibits have included everything from Nigerian hats or a Turkish tea set or precious stones from Brazil.

The exhibits also included examples of the student’s country’s currency and music, such as Japanese rap and Greek top forty.
They also included traditional clothing, national flags, and videos of ceremonies and dances.

“The exhibits are hosted by the international students who provide tours for visitors,” stated Anderson.

“We had approximately 25 visitors per day. I feel it was a success.”
It is not difficult for the international students to give informative tours, says Anderson.

The international students participate weekly with International Club and have learned a great deal about one another’s cultures. 
Also, friendships between the international students have helped to cultivate their knowledge of one another’s countries.

The international students are not the only ones who participated in Diversity Week. 

According to Anderson, Sergeant James Boiling, “who is a very wide-experienced traveler,” also has an extensive collection of displays that he included in the celebration.

“He generously participates with us,” says Anderson.

Anderson, who is also the creator of Diversity Week, says the primary reason for starting it was the “enormous request for international students to speak at grade schools.”

According to Anderson, she thought “it would be great if these people could hear and meet these students on their turf and their time.”
In the past, international students have often had to turn down requests to come speak at schools because it conflicted with class schedules.

Diversity Week has proven to be a successful way of bringing the grade schools to the international student.

Along with the sponsorship of SPC, Diversity Week also included a performance by the Valdosta State University steel drum band. 
The band consists of approximately twenty students “who have mastered to some degree playing these oil drums,” says Anderson.
“[The drums are] typical of a culture innate to the Caribbean, but all of the students are Caucasian,” states Anderson. “That is the neat part. This fits right into a multicultural aspect as far as I’m concerned. I love that.”

“Each year we hope to make Diversity Week a bigger and better event,” says Anderson. 

“This year we had international students of a more traditional age, whereas in the past the leadership came from grad students. The traditional age students were unique in providing a new kind of energy.”
