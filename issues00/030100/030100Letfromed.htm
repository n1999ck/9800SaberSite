XI THETA SORORITY

 

 

Smaller schools have advantages over larger universities
A Letter from the editor
Clarissa Andrews / Co-Editor

Making the decision to switch to a smaller school in my hometown after going to UGA for over a year was not easy. I loved everything about Athens--the stores, the clubs, the variety of people, the DAWGS, my job . . . in fact, I liked everything but school.

To me, there was nothing as painful as being in an intro to biology class with 499 other people. I knew nothing about biology, so I couldn’t understand anything my professor was saying, but with all of those people, I couldn’t ask her to clarify everything she said. The class was so large that, even in an enormous auditorium, there weren’t enough seats on test days.

So after nearly flipping out in the middle of my biology professor’s class, I decided to do what I swore to myself I would never do--go to Columbus College, I mean State University. 

My first semester here, to my absolute amazement, I absolutely loved my classes. The biggest class I had was about 20 people, and the professors actually knew my face as well as my name after just a few days. I actually had one professor who was checking roll by looking around the classroom (since he knew all the students), then he stopped at me and said, “I haven’t seen you before. What’s your name?”

It was a shocking change from UGA.

Since starting CSU, I have had a few family situations that have caused me to miss more days in class than I should have. When I explained what was going on to my professors, they told me not to worry about missing class. “Just relax and do what you need to do,” they told me. “You can catch up soon.”

If these problems would have occurred while I was still at Georgia, my professors would have told me that it would be better to drop the class, because there was no way they would have time to grade late assignments.

But the day I knew for certain that attending CSU was the right choice just happened a few weeks ago. I had just finished my first test for accounting 2, and I was drained. The night before, I found out that my boyfriend and I had tickets to see one of the best bands in the world . . .

The Cure . . . at a small club in Atlanta . . . only six cities in the entire U.S. . . . their last tour ever (supposedly) . . . and my next accounting test was the next day. Uh-oh.

I was scared of what my professor would say, so I sort of rushed right through the explanation of why I was asking to reschedule my test. I could hear the voices of the nameless UGA professors in my head, saying, “WHAT?!?! You want to make up my test so you can hear a band? Give me a break!”

So there I was, begging my professor. “I’ll take the test on no sleep and with no time to study on Thursday if I have to, but please, please, puh-leeze can I take it at another time? I’ll even take it early if you want me to.”

And do you know what he said? He said, “Sure. My son went to hear a band the other night, too, so I know how important these things can be.”

Huh? 

It was so easy. With all the different professors I’ve had since I’ve been here, there might have been one that wouldn’t have been understanding. And because of these understanding professors who remember what it’s like to be in college, they have made me glad to have switched to a small school. So even though I’ll always be a Georgia Bulldog at heart, I’m glad that I switched to CSU--otherwise, I wouldn’t have gotten to see the Cure.
