Decrease in CSU enrollment causes budget reductions
Jessica Trenchik / Features Editor

The recent necessity to make reductions in the student activity budget has led to student speculation about CSU’s current financial situation. This speculation has likely been the origin of the wide spread rumors that CSU is suffering from a budget deficit, rumors which have been the cause of great alarm for many CSU students.
Budget deficit, however, would not be the correct way to describe CSU’s financial situation, according to Charles Patillo, Vice President for Business and Finance. 

The situation, says Patillo, would be more accurately labeled a budget reduction.

When the administration was planning CSU’s budget for this year, they were working under the estimate that CSU’s enrollment would increase by two and a half percent. 

In reality, it decreased by three and a half percent.

This decrease created a problem because, naturally, a large portion of CSU’s funds is derived from tuition. 

When enrollment, and thus tuition, was significantly lower than expected, the planned budget was no longer functional.

It was this situation that led to the recent cuts in the student activity budget which has spawned rumors of CSU suffering from a budget deficit. 

Patillo is careful to state that CSU is not suffering from a budget deficit, as the term deficit implies that an institution is spending more than it is taking in. 

By the end of August the administration knows approximately what type of revenues they have to work with for the upcoming year, according to Patillo. 

The decision to begin reducing the budget was made in mid-September, Patillo says. 

Patillo feels that the situation was dealt with early enough in the year so as to eliminate the need to take “drastic measures” to correct the problem.

A large percentage of the drop in enrollment can be attributed to the fact that CSU switched over from the quarter system to the semester system. According to Patillo, the drop in enrollment caused by this switch has been a driving factor behind the budget reductions.
A significant number of non-traditional students attend CSU. 
According to Patillo, it is often more difficult for non-traditional students to schedule five three hour classes than three five hour classes.

Because of this, many non-traditional students have opted to continue taking only three classes. 

However, these three classes now count for only nine credit hours, whereas before three classes would have counted as fifteen credit hours.

As a result, non-traditional students overall are taking fewer credit hours per semester. 

If a student is taking fewer credit hours, then they are paying less tuition, and thus the budget must be decreased.
CSU is not alone in this problem. 

Other universities in Georgia have experienced this same drop in enrollment, and thus tuition, because of switching over to the semester system. 

In fact, Patillo says, this drop in tuition causing the same situation for other universities outside of the state of Georgia.

As previously stated, the budget reductions rendered necessary by this situation have resulted in cuts in student activity funds. 
However, this is not the only area to experience cuts.

The athletic activity funds have also been reduced, as well as the general operating budget.

According to Patillo, the bulk of reductions were made in the area of the general operating budget. 

By reducing non-personal service expenditures, such as supplies and equipment, and choosing not to fill certain vacant positions, a good deal of the loss in expected funds was compensated for.

Patillo feels that most students would not actually recognize the fact that the budget has been decreased because the majority of changes were made to the general operating budget.

“The key is that we’ve all been working together to work through this,” says Patillo.

Patillo says that he and the rest of the administration is hopeful that enrollment will increase next year and the current reductions will no longer be necessary.
