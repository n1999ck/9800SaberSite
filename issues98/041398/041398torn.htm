Deadly tornados ravage Alabama

by Heather Warren / Associate Editor
Deadly tornadoes made a path through Alabama and Georgia early Thursday. The latest estimates said over forty people had been killed in Alabama and over eight in Georgia.

The hardest hit area was Jefferson County, Alabama where rescuers were still searching for missing people on Friday. In Jefferson County, searchers had found 41 people that had been killed by the storm on Friday afternoon and about 200 people were injured. The death toll may rise as the searchers continue there mission. Experts think three tornadoes hit Jefferson County.

Vice President Al Gore surveyed the damage on Friday in Alabama and Georgia. He called the storm “the most powerful tornado ever seen in this part of the country.” Gore also said since the impact of the tornado was unprecedented, “the response must be unprecedented,” too.

After surveying the damage the National Weather service said the tornado had winds up to 250 miles per hour in Alabama. This is the second worst storm in Alabama history. It was the strongest to hit Alabama since a 1977 twister struck Smithfield.

According to the Alabama Emergency Management Agency about 500 homes were destroyed or suffered major damage and another 400 homes suffered minor damage in Jefferson County and St. Clair County.

President Clinton at a speech in Kentucky declared major disaster areas in the worst-hit parts of Alabama and Georgia. Governor Fob James declared a state of emergency and called out the National Guard to assist in the cleanup. Governor James said “It’s the worst storm I’ve seen in my lifetime.”

The most powerful tornado cut a 15 mile long and one mile wide path and destroyed a high school, a fire station and damaged two churches.

The storms Thursday were the latest in what has become the deadliest U.S. tornado season in fourteen years, experts said. A top weather expert said this is shaping up to be the deadliest since 1984 when 122 people died.
