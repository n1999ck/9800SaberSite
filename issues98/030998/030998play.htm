Play closes on high note

by Shannon Jackson / Staff Writer

 
Translation: The Birdcage. Based on the play by Jean Poiret, La Cage Aux Folles is a riveting musical concerning the trials and triumphs of living an alternative lifestyle and a father’s undying love.

La Cage Aux Folles was originally written as a play and was later created as an original French film. Then came the Broadway musical and the more popular version, the movie, The Birdcage, starring Robin Williams as Georges and Nathan Lane as Albin.

Georges and Albin are a happily “married” couple of twenty years. They have a lovely son, Jean-Michel, who has just announced his engagement to Anne Dindon. Anne happens to be the daughter of a very important stiff necked political figure who specializes in morality. The catch is Georges and Albin are two homosexual males. The only chance of gaining Mr. Dindons approval of his daughters marriage to Jean-Michel is to pass this family off as an upstanding moral unit of the community.

The strengths and fragilities of love are portrayed in a somewhat poetic fashion, which encompasses the theme of this musical: the unconditional love of a father, the durable love of a lifelong couple, and the intoxicating effects of young love. All of this along with the musical direction and comedic consistency is what makes this into one of the biggest hit musicals of its decade.

It was definitely a big hit for the CSU theater department.

Dr. Hazel Hall, Department Head and Professor of Theater Arts and director of La Cage Aux Folles, felt the play was a success. “We had standing ovations every night,” Hall says.

Hall says the experience seemed to be positive for everyone involved. “I heard no negative comments from anyone.”

There were only a few minor problems for the cast. At one performance an actors shoe broke and in one show there was microphone malfunction, which had to be quickly corrected. Other than those and a few other very small incidents the show ran smoothly, according to Hall. “I knew the play would work. A lot of hard work went into it and I knew it was a solid play.”

Hall says she did have some concern about the play before it went into production because of the subject matter. However, Hall says that she has not heard of any negative feedback and that some people even thanked her for broaching the subject. “One woman told me she was going to tell her pastor to see the show. I’m glad we did it.”

There was no notable protest of the Williams’ film, “The Birdcage” and Hall says that was taken into consideration before the play was chosen. She says that if there had been protesters in the community, the play would not have been chosen.

“I really enjoyed it. Despite the play’s ‘interesting’ subject matter, I feel that the Theater Department did a great job of pulling off the show with a great deal of class,” One CSU student says.

The surprising twist of the Grand Finale completes the illusion which creates a paradox that is the basis of the entire show.
