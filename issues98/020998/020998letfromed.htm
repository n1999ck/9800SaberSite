Letter from the Editor

by Heather Warren / Associate Editor

 
The media was created to be a watchdog for the American people. To protect them by giving all the facts for the public to form an opinion. Today, some in the media are trying to save us from the burden of thinking by simply telling us their opinion and leaving it at that.

The Clinton situation has been an interesting one to watch. It is not the story that has held my attention, it is how many times the news programs forget to say “alleged”. It is equally disturbing how many shows have titled there segments “Crisis in the White House.”

I am just as sick of hearing about our president’s alleged sexual relations as you are, but, it is a learning experience regarding what is fact or fiction in the media. It also proves the point all to well that you cannot just read one newspaper or watch one television show and say, “Alright, now I know the whole truth.”

I think we are getting back to the era of yellow journalism that was prevalent in the 1890’s. This was when newspapers used sensational tactics to sell more newspapers. It’s not a new concept to sensationalize a story, but one would hope it would be an outdated concept by now.

This has to happen every decade or so where an alleged “scandal” breaks out and the story is more important than the facts and sources. The past couple of weeks, the media has been chasing each others leads and following each others sources. This is known as “piggybacking” in journalism. All the networks are running around in a frenzy giving opinions when they should just be giving the facts. When competition to get the story first is so fierce, opinions turn into rumors, rumors turn into allegations, and allegations turn into facts. The most important issue has become to get the story out fast with no concern regarding the facts.

On January 25 it seems that the press discovered the error of their ways. On CBS it was repeatedly explained to the viewing public to remember, these were simply allegations.

They just don’t seem to get that we are tired of it. On “Good Morning America Sunday” Sam Donaldson was making the point that the American people don’t care, but maybe they should. The reporters think we should be interested in this story because it is a moral question involving the president.

To all the reporters who think the American people have declining morals I would like to pose this possibility. It is not the decay of society that you should blame for the disinterest, it is because people are more intelligent and democratic than we once were. We aren’t going to convict him unless the facts convict him. It’s that silly little thought of fair trial and innocence until proven guilty. Maybe you’ve heard of it.

On January 31 ABC News said in an article “A variety of factors helped Clinton in the past week; one may be a public inclination to rally around him in the face of media onslaught. Americans now divide evenly on whether the news media treated Clinton fairly or not; that reflects an 11 point increase in the number who say the media have been unfair.” A CNN Poll said 72% of the people surveyed believe coverage was excessive.

Monday The Dallas Morning News reported that a Secret Service agent was ready to testify that he had seen President Clinton and Lewinsky in a “compromising situation.” This report was quickly retracted but it should never have been printed in the first place.

Everything leaked by the independent council is taken as fact. The “facts” are not confirmed by any other source. The media does not try to support these “facts” because they make great headlines. Maybe they are too good to be real.

The main issue is not who received the fair or unfair treatment in the media. I personally just want the same amount of importance placed on the facts as the who is first with the story.
