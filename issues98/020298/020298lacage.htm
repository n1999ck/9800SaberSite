Bold undertaking for CSU Theater

by Josh Eller / Entertainment Editor

 
Better known for its cinematic version “The Birdcage”, La Cage Aux Folles is the Columbus State University Theatre’s winter quarter production. Winner of the 1984 Tony Award, La Cage Aux Folles is based on the book by Harvey Fierstein with music and lyrics by Jerry Herman.

The musical will run February 19, 20, 21, 26, 27, 28, at 8:00 PM and February 22 and March1 at 2:00 PM. Tickets are $15.00 for general admission and $12.00 non-CSU students, military, senior citizens and CSU alumni. All full- time CSU students, faculty and staff receive one free ticket.

If you have any immediate questions or comments contact Dr. Hall or Steve Graver in the Department of Theatre.

La Cage Folles presents many challenges for all involved in the production. Costume designer, Steve Graver is making over 60 costumes. Much of the materials were bought in Atlanta and some were even brought from New York. The costumes are the most notable part of the production, but Graver assures that even with a limited budget there will be no problems in creating Ziegfeld-style costumes.

William Ennis, who is choreographer, says there is a great amount of skill needed to perform in a musical of this magnitude. “Everyone is dancing in heels, and on top of that, it is really challenging vocally, especially with the guys who have to sing in really high ranges,” says Ennis.

It should also be noted that all male cast members had to shave there legs. Although Ennis has conceded to being a Nair man he describes the overall task of shaving with two words “It itches." He would also like to say that through much deliberation it was unanimously agreed upon that, Phaedra played by Jason Pierce has the best legs in the troop and they look even better in hose.

Although, most participants are Theatre majors, outside talent was necessary. La Cage Aux Folles is a difficult production to perform and requires a larger than average cast totaling 28 actors and actresses.

La Cage Folles involves an older gay couple Albin and Georges who run a cabaret. George has a son, Jean-Michael, who is bringing home his bride to be, Anne, and her parents . The comedic conflict develops when it is discovered the brides father is the “Minister of Morality” who runs his political career on a ultra conservative platform. Jean-Michael persuades Georges to masquerade as the happy husband of Jean-Michael’s natural mother who is to be Albin in drag. Ironically, the overall theme of the musical, pointed out by both Steve Graver and Dr. Hall is family values, this being evident with the great lengths the father goes to accommodate his son.

As the story ends, everyone accepts and discovers the truth without prejudice. The end is the entire Dindon family being sneaked out of the cabaret to avoid embarrassment of the conservative father. This is sure to be an entertaining and enlightening show.
