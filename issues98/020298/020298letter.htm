Letter from the Editor

by Athena Curry / Editor-in-Chief
I attended a forum recently on diversity. As one speaker pointed out, no one there was kidding themselves about the true topic at hand: blacks and whites. There were only occasional references to Latinos and Asians, a few about women, persons with disabilities and other minority groups. This was probably for the best since just discussing the issues of blacks and whites has been enough to keep us busy for the past four decades.

The four person panel who spoke individually on the topic were informative and interesting, however, there was little discussed when the floor was opened to comments that I had not heard on Oprah many times.

Everyone who spoke seemed genuine. One of the problems in this country is most of us are quick to speak of loving all people regardless of skin color, but few of us are as quick to put much more on the line for our beliefs.

Many people at the forum compared people to rainbows, saying that rainbows are beautiful and so is the same diversity in people. This is true, but haven’t we heard it before?

One man stood to say that 30 years ago he attended forums at Columbus College to discuss the same topic and that people are still saying the same things. This should tell us something.

One would be hard pressed to find a person, black or white who would stand up and say that another race is inferior or inherently evil. Cross burnings are not an every day occurrence in Columbus. However, are the problems among the races over?

People are not using the word “nigger” as often as they once did. But some white people commonly whisper the word “black” as if it is an affliction.

One young black man during the forum said a friend of his told him he was “white/black”, a comment that was meant to be taken as a compliment. Many people in the audience groaned, both black and white, because these sort of comments are the common problem we are dealing with today. The woman who made the comment didn’t have to use the word “nigger” to make the point that people of different races are ignorant of one another.

It staggers me and saddens me that 30 years after the death of Martin Luther King, Jr., it is still necessary to hold discussions on a university campus about blacks and whites not eating together. Even though the obvious face of racism is diminishing, it seems the more subtle face is still here. There is no sign over the water fountain which reads, “whites only”, but less visible problems are real.

Racism today is a subtle battle. It is a battle between people who still feel the pain of oppression and people who feel blamed for crimes committed before their birth. It is a fight between those who feel feared and those who feel fear. As one person in the forum pointed out, it is battle not fought with swords, but with pens.

While many whites would never use the word nigger, they would not want their own daughter to marry outside of her race. Many blacks deal with being told by peers that they are “too white” or “sell outs” because they associate with white people.

So much of what we say and do today in race relations is a matter of political correctness, politeness or current trend and not about real understanding of the another culture.

We can continue to make colorful analogies comparing race to rainbows and repeating that we should all see people for who they are on the inside. While these cliches may be true, we should be past this first step by now. We should be past identifying and recognizing the problem and on to doing something about it.

As a woman, I must look ahead to making 75 cents on the dollar to the male peers that graduate along side me. People can tell me how far women have come, elderly women can share stories of inequalities in the past, we can attend round table discussions about it, but in the end I still want my 25 cents.

We have decided to call each other equals, we speak about loving one, but we still lack action. If our ideals were as high as we claim, we would have reached farther by now.
