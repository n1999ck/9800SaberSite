Students attacked in Guatemala

by Matthew Young / Staff Writer

 
Almost two weeks ago, a group of students from St. Mary’s College in Maryland, were on their way back from an excursion in the northern Guatemalan province of Peten where they had been viewing ancient Mayan ruins. After three weeks in the tiny Central American nation, this was their last day. At 4 p.m. on that afternoon while the group was traveling along Guatemala’s Pacific highway near the southern town of Santa Lucia Cotzumalguapa, 40 miles Southwest of Guatemala City, their bus was forced off the road by a pick-up truck and the thirteen member group was forced out of the vehicle at gun point.

The group made up of thirteen college students and three school officials were attacked and robbed. The five women in the group, all between the ages of 18 and 20, were raped by the bandits. The entire incident took 90 minutes.

``It was four armed men in a pick-up truck. They raped five women,’’ said Frank Neville, U.S. embassy spokesman. He also said police have arrested two Guatemalan men suspected of taking part in the attack and that the officials in Guatemala are continuing the search for the other bandits. However, the figure of four men was later increased to seven men.

The attack hit the campus of St. Mary’s College hard. Most were shocked and outraged that this could happen. The study tour was the college’s third to Guatemala. College officials said the two previous trips were without incident.

President Clinton, spoke to a group of high school students on Monday. ``It’s a terrible thing, what happened, and I have a lot of concerns, obviously, for the victims and their families. But we’re persuaded the government is taking appropriate action,’’ Clinton told reporters.

One of the major industries in Guatemala is tourism. Fearful of what the story could do to its economy, Guatemala’s ambassador to the United States drove to St. Mary’s College just outside of Washington DC to express his sorrow and regret to students and faculty at the College. ``At this time we are going to concentrate all our efforts in assisting in the healing of the victims in as many ways as possible,’’ Ambassador Pedro Lamport told reporters at the college in St. Mary’s City, Maryland.

As the week went on, five suspects were arrested and jailed. All five are now awaiting trial, but the other two suspects are still on the loose. In light of this situation, the Federal Bureau of Investigation was called upon by the Guatemalan government to assist in their investigation of the crimes. ``The request was made by the Guatemalan authorities for assistance, and some assistance is being provided,’’ spokesman James Rubin said. A State Department official said the help the FBI provided would ``most likely take the form of some forensic evidence support, either on the ground there or evidence could be transported to a different location.’’ He went on to say that, ``It’s not the first time we’ve done this sort of thing there or in other countries.’’

Violent attacks and robberies against tourists are not uncommon in Guatemala. The U.S. State Department warns visitors going to Guatemala about highway robbery and armed assaults.
