 Drugs found in dorms

by Bridgette Brumbaugh / Staff Writer

A Columbus State University student was removed from the dorms after officials found marijuana pieces in his room. The incident happened on December 1, 1997. Jasper Cook, Chief of Public Safety, stated that a "911" call was placed about a disturbance in dorm room A9. According to Cook the Housing Authority entered the apartment and discovered seeds and stems belonging to one of the tenants. Cook continued by commenting that no charges had been filed against this student.

Steve Smitt of the Housing Authority says that when he arrived at the scene the police and the student were talking in the parking lot. Smitt says he then went into the dorm to check for damage and unlocked the individual's bedroom door.

Smitt stated, " I saw a bag of marijuana on the desk, but I didn't touch it". He then re-locked the door, and told the police what he had found. He also mentioned that this was a simple case of someone looking out for himself because the person who made the "911" call was the student's roommate.

Larry Kees, assistant vice-president of student affairs, said, " If it [marijuana] had been found in say, the living room or kitchen, then maybe we could have determined who it belonged to and made an arrest."

The student that was found with the marijuana was not charged with a crime. He was removed from the dorms however he remains a student at Columbus State University and is facing administrative penalties. The reason he was not charged was because the officials opened his bedroom door without a warrant.

Cook says, "Based on my experience, because it was found in his room and the door was locked, it wouldn't have held up in a courtroom." Cook also stated that "We have a zero tolerance regarding drugs in the dorms."

Despite the occasional room checks, the dorms are still your home. The question is do officials have the right to search dorm rooms to look for illegal drugs or things against school policy?

In Commonwealth v. Neilson, 666 NE 984 (1996), the issue of university employees upholding students' constitutional rights in residence halls was tested.

College officials at a public school in Massachusetts left notices of a room check on 4 roommate's bedroom doors at the residence hall. The notice read they were going to search for a cat that was rumored to be in their dorm.

When the officials entered one bedroom, they saw a light in the closet and thought it might be a fire hazard. When the door was opened the officials found 2 big marijuana plants along with various items for cultivation. The officials called the campus police who then seized the illegal items. Neilson was charged with illegal possession of marijuana as well as cultivating and distributing the herb. Both the district and state supreme court suppressed the evidence because a fourth amendment violation occurred when the state college campus police performed a warrantless search of the student's dormitory room.

Citing the Fourth Amendment, defense attorney's averted a conviction. The Fourth Amendment states, "The right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures, shall not be violated, and no Warrants shall issue, but upon probable cause, supported by Oath or affirmation, and particularly describing the place to be searched, and the persons or things to be seized".

As for the CSU campus, Cook says, "We treat the dorms as if they were your home."

As a result of the Neilson case and similar cases, school officials might search your dorm and find an illegal substance, but you probably will not go to jail if they violate the fourth amendment. According to Larry Kees, assistant vice-president of student affairs, you will however be removed from the dorms and face various other penalties.
