Columbus College and University Status

by Doug Wallace / Staff Writer for 1991 Saber Staff

This article was taken from the November 21, 1991 edition of the Saber.
It’s time to “take a reading” on the quest by Columbus College for university status.

Dr. Frank Brown said last week a key event will occur within the next two or three months that will have significant bearing on whether or not the local college gets the nod.

Georgia Southern at Statesboro was the first and so far only regional college to gain university status, and that was in July, 1990. The next in line is Valdosta State, which has a tentative date set for July, 1992.

Dr. Brown said that right now everything hinges on whether or not approval is given to Valdosta. This decision should be made within the next two or three months. If Valdosta gets the go-ahead, Columbus College would be set for mid-summer, 1993.

Across the Chattahoochee River in Alabama there are 13 regional schools, all of which have university status. That includes Troy State University, Troy; Troy State University, Dothan, and Troy State University, Montgomery. Livingston University has an enrollment of only 1,761, and Troy State, Dothan, only 1,833. Four others have fewer students than Columbus College.

Regional schools in Florida, Tennessee, Mississippi, North Carolina, South Carolina and Kentucky have university status. Georgia is the only exception among Southern states.

The current budget crisis may have some bearing on the Valdosta decision, but Dr. Brown believes a fund shortage is not all that important.

Of great significance is the forth-coming recommendation on Valdosta by Chancellor H. Dean Propsp who it is believed is not keen on seeing any more regional schools gaining university status for the time being. He is said to favor a “hold off” attitude.

A decision on Valdosta by the 15-member Board of Regents should be made next January or February.

Needless to say, a strong effort In the Valdosta area has been mounted for favorable decision. “We don’t need money, just send us the title,” is their cry.

If Valdosta wins, Columbus should definitely be next in line, according to Dr. Brown. There’s no a great deal of difference between the two schools, he said.

\The Columbus community, including alumni, the Chamber of Commerce, civic groups and business have expressed a decisive interest in the local college receiving the “recognition It deserves,” Dr. Brown said.

When the “big announcement” was made in January, 1989, concerning the possibility of university status for Columbus, it was predicted the goal would be attained by July, 1993. That hasn’t changed.

Dr. Brown said that standards or criteria for university status are well within reach of Columbus College: endowments, graduate offerings, library holdings, etc. Dormitories do not figure in the requirements.

It is believed the Chancellor does not favor more regional colleges gaining the top rank because it would “dilute” Georgia’s two “flagships” - Georgia Tech and the University of Georgia. Dr. Brown sees little merit in this belief. Look at Alabama.

Two from Columbus are on the Board of Regents. They are Bill Turner and Don Leebern.

Other regional colleges In line for the top title are West Georgia, Albany State and Kennesaw State.

Dr. Brown said the college is pushing for new buildings. Top priority now goes toward a performing arts center and an arena or physical education complex. Funding, of course, is currently a “real problem.”

Plans for a data processing facility are also on the drawing board. The college is currently working with IBM for a grant.
