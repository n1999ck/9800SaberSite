Through the years

by Brian Hale / Entertainment Editor
Walking around campus this week I noticed that scattered everywhere are banners and notices on the 40th Anniversary for the school. This made me start thinking(uh oh).

Forty years is a long time. A great deal has happened in the entertainment business in the past several decades. Radio, television, and the movie industry have changed and grown in many different ways over the years.

Looking back over the extensive list of shows and bands that have come and gone over the years showed me that many of them have been forgotten and sometimes overlooked. Well, not today. This is not going to be a great journalistic work or anything of the sorts. This is simply a look back at some things that I think should not be forgotten.

“Sanford and Son” is one of those shows that should be remembered and laughed at forever. Many other television shows that have brought us through the years fall into that category. Think about the classics: “Captain Kangaroo,” “MASH,” “Bonanza,” “Little House on the Prairie,” “The Land of the Lost,” “Sesame Street,” and “Archie Bunker.” All of those shows helped to pull television through the times when our parents were still just dating each other. Now most of our parents are dating again, but not each other - that’s a whole other story.

The list goes on. Who can forget “My Three Son’s,” and “I Love Lucy?” Many people still visit Mr. Rogers’ neighborhood, and through Nick at Nite we can still enjoy the blabbering of Mr. Ed.

Don’t get me wrong, not all of the classics are from our parents’ time. We have our own classics. Don’t deny it, everybody freezes on the channel when flipping by and catching a glimpse of Zach and the gang on “Saved By The Bell.” The show’s a classic.

Don’t forget about Ms. Garrett and the girls from “Facts of Life,” or Willis and Arnold from “Different Strokes.” What kid didn’t want to be Bo or Luke from “The Duke’s of Hazzard?"

There are more. Don’t believe me? Here it goes: “Magnum P.I.,” “Misfits of Science,” “Cheers,” “Knight Rider,” “Hillstreet Blues,” “Family Ties,” “Gilligan’s Island,” “Silver Spoons”, and “Scooby Doo.” I almost left out “Green Acres,” “Leave It To Beaver,” “Chips,” and “You Can’t Do That On Television.” Oh, and DYNOMITE! Who could live without reruns of “Good Times?”

That only touches the tip of the massive amount of television shows that have brought the entertainment industry to where it is today. The 90’s brought us shows such as “Quantum Leap,” “The Simpsons,” “Seinfeld,” “Friends,” “ER,” “NYPD Blue,” and “Baywatch.” “Beverly Hills 90210,” “Melrose Place,” and all the other shows of the same topic can thank “21 Jump Street,” and “The Heights” for opening the door for that type of show.

The same goes for the talk show industry. Today's stars are only where they are because of the people that were before them. Ed Sullivan started the whole shindig back in 1948. He brought millions of people together every Sunday night at 8:00 until 1971.

By the time Sullivan was done, Johnny Carson had gotten his show rolling. Carson started in the 60’s and finally called it quits in 1992. Sullivan and Carson opened the door for Arsenio Hall, David Letterman, Jay Leno, and Conan O’Brien.

There is no telling what star will emerge next that will have these mentioned before to thank for their success. Personally, I’m hoping Arsenio jumps back in the game, but we’ll just have to wait and see.

Movie stars are in the same boat as the above mentioned hosts. They all have somebody that opened the door for them to become popular. Marlon Brando opened the door for Robert DeNiro to become as cool as he is. Al Pacino did the same for Andy Garcia. Over the years we have seen so many great stars, such as, Jack Nicholson, Jackie Gleason, Sylvester Stallone, Robin Williams, Mel Gibson, Kevin Bacon, Michael Keaton, Sean Connery, and Harrison Ford. There are so many more that it is impossible to name them all. They are responsible for all the movies we grew up with. I mean classics, such as the “Godfather “ trilogy, the “Police Academy” series, and “Revenge of the Nerds.” Those are good movies- not like the stuff made today.

Music is another avenue that has changed a great deal over the years. As far as I’m concerned, rock music is the only thing worth listening to, and that all started with Buddy Holly in the pre-1950 era.

I can’t say that it’s gotten better, but the industry has grown. The Beatles, Led Zeppelin, the Grateful Dead, Frank Zappa, David Bowie, and Eric Clapton - they started it all. Aerosmith, Ozzy Osbourne, Rolling Stones, and Bob Dylan have been constants in the music industry for decades. Duran Duran, Madonna, Fleetwood Mac, Poison, REM, Willie Nelson, Motley Crue, and Johnny Cash are up there, too. Bachman Turner Overdrive, Credence Clearwater Revival, Pink Floyd, Steppenwolf, Chuck Berry, U2, Huey Lewis and the News, and the B 52’s will forever be credited with helping keep rock and roll alive.

Another band that has remained a pillar in music over the years is Chicago. Come on, what couple hasn’t gotten together or broken up to a Chicago song? Did I mention Woodstock? The 1969 Woodstock, not the lame money hungry 1994 copy. Woodstock was not a concert. It was a historical event.

Well, that’s it for the reminiscing for now. Just keep in mind, with the new season of shows about to start, the constant spattering of new music in stores and on the radio, and new movies in theaters every week, they all got their start somewhere. They are called classics. They have been with us throughout the years, and we should not forget them.
