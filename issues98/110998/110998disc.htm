Discovery mission crucial
by Helen Godwin / Associate Editor

HOME

The shuttle Discovery, due back to Earth on November 7, is doing more than just taking the famous senator John Glenn into space. Its crew is conducting experiments that will forever affect mankind.

Though the experiments on Glenn are important, they are part of a larger scale project called the International Space Station. This is a station being built by the Americans, Japanese, Russians, Europeans, and several other countries which will be the first permanent human habitat in orbit.

With the projected start date of the station being January of 1999, Dr. Shawn Cruzen, Assistant Professor of Astronomy at Columbus State University, said, “By the year 2000, people will be living in space.”

The Discovery has taken parts of this station into space to test the affects of the space environment on this station. It is also studying the affects of space on the elderly. This is because the effects of space on astronauts are like the effects of aging, There is a loss of muscle and bone mass and many other symptoms very similar to aging.

The results of these tests on Glenn will help determine whether or not the elderly will be able to live in space.

According to Dr. Cruzen, the study of Glenn is “One of dozens of experiments being conducted.” These other tests include experiments to study the changes of the sun’s corona, which may effect global climate changes, testing the effects of the space environment on upgrade components for the Hubbell telescope, and testing ultraviolet emission not passing through the atmosphere.
