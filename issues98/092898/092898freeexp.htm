Freedom of expression at CSU

by Leslie Maxwell / Staff Writer
Do you remember those Saturday morning commercials where Bugs Bunny, Yosemite Sam, and Porky Pig proclaimed, “The Constitution works for kids, too!”? Well, the Constitution works for college students too, even though it often gets lost in bureaucratic red tape and public relations. We don’t have to check our rights, especially our freedom of expression, at the door when we come to CSU. Our First Amendment freedoms of speech and press allow people with good ideas to speak out and change things for the better.

Freedom of expression has its roots in 17th century England and America, when John Milton and Roger Williams argued for freedom of religion and unlicensed printing. Over two hundred years ago, our founding fathers felt that the freedoms of speech and press were essential for a successful democracy. They believed in the “Market Place of Ideas,” a notion that people should be allowed to express their ideas so that the public can choose the best ones to put into action. Free speech does have limits; for example, defamation and conspiracy to commit a crime are unprotected forms of speech. In 1798, the Alien and Sedition Acts were passed to end criticism of the Federalist party, which controlled the government at that time. A number of newspaper editors and publishers were prosecuted under these acts for printing negative opinions about the government. The acts were later repealed because legitimate opinions have a right to be heard, even if they are negative.

The historical connotation of “press” includes every sort of publication that is a vehicle for information and opinion. Walter Lippman said that “A free press is not a privilege but an organic necessity in a great society,” and French philosopher Albert Camus wrote, “A free press can of course be good or bad, but most certainly, without freedom it will never be anything but bad ... Freedom is nothing else but a chance to be better” (italics mine). Freedom of expression in the press insures the introduction of new ideas into organizations, government, and society.

In the Supreme Court case Rosenberger v. University of Virginia, the court defended freedom of speech and press. A student organization was denied funding for its newspaper because of its Christian perspective. The university claimed that funding a religious group would violate the Establishment clause, which forbids laws respecting any establishment of religion. The organization took the matter to court, claiming that the university had violated its freedom of speech and press. The Supreme Court said that the government (or any state entity) must abstain from regulating speech when the viewpoint or opinion of the speaker is the primary rationale for the restriction. For the university to disapprove of particular viewpoints risks the suppression of free speech and creative inquiry in one of the nation’s vital centers of intellectual life, its colleges and universities.

One Supreme Court Justice wrote, “Freedom to speak and write about public questions is as important to the life of our government as the heart is to the human body. In fact, this privilege is the heart of our government! If that heart be weakened, the result is debilitation; if it be stilled, the result is death.” These freedoms are not only the heart of the government, they are the heart of every organization. As the world changes, organizations must adapt, but without new ideas, change is impossible. Freedom of speech and press gives us the right to express our legitimate ideas and opinions without censorship from the government, or ideally, from any other authority. We have an outlet for these freedoms here at CSU: The Saber. If you have an opinion about something that needs to be changed, or a new idea, write to the Saber. Letters and articles can be placed in the Saber mailbox, which is located outside the SGA office in the Davidson Student Center. Remember, even the best ideas will not work if they are never expressed. We encourage you to exercise your freedoms of speech and press to make CSU a better place!
