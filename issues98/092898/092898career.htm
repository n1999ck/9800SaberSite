The Career Center: getting the word out

The Office of Public Relations
The Career Center in Davidson Student Center is on a mission. Concerned because students express surprise that the office is a one-stop smorgasbord of career-related information and assistance, the staff is redoubling its efforts to get information out to students.

One unique way of communication is the “Toilet Tribune,” a colorful flyer posted in strategic spots in the restroom facilities throughout campus. The first issue for fall semester is in an eye-catching shade of green. It is chock full of interesting bits of information about programs from the Career Center as well as some eye-opening facts and figures about starting salaries for various majors.

Christi Dixon of the Career Center says they plan to publish two tribunes each semester. The second issue will be coming to a stall near you around mid-term.

Two upcoming events highlighted in the current Toilet Tribune are Etiquette Day and Super Saturday.

Etiquette Day

CSU’s annual Etiquette Day is scheduled for Saturday, October 3, in Davidson Student Center. Fifty lucky participants will receive an in depth introduction to the business world through the eyes of one of the foremost experts in corporate etiquette.

Peggy Newfield has presented this seminar on campus for several years. From Atlanta, she is president of Personal Best, a communication skills consulting firm that specializes in seminars focusing on social protocol for the corporate world.

The day-long, 9 a.m.-4 p.m., seminar costs $15 which includes a full, five-course luncheon, as well as focusing on professional dress, business etiquette and networking to advantage.

Dixon urges juniors and seniors in particular to sign up for this important seminar because “today’s corporate hiring is being done more and more in a social context, such as cocktail parties or over executive lunches.”

Super Saturday

Saturday, October 17 is Super Saturday at CSU. The kickoff for the 1998-99 Cougar P.A.W.S., Super Saturday is an effort to involve students in community activities.

Following breakfast at 7:30, participants select from three areas of involvement: Habitat, Keep Columbus Beautiful (Help the Hooch), or the Exchange Club’s picnic for Big Brothers, Big Sisters. Registration is $5 per person and includes breakfast and a T-shirt.

Dixon points out that there is no requirement for commitment beyond this day, although the goal is to get students involved in the community.

An added incentive for continued participation is the certificate program. Using color-coded seals to denote different levels of participation, the certificate gives participating students a nice wall plaque that commemorates their civic involvement.

The Center has started a mail campaign to all students and recent alumni. The letter discusses some of the career-related services available and includes an introduction to the Center’s website. A brief tour of their internet address, <www.colstate.edu/student/career>, connects to many of the Center’s services. Students and recent alumni can prepare resumes for entry in the resume data bank right on line. Just follow the prompts.

The Career Center and its staff exist to assist students and alumni. Stop by and get to know them—take advantage of all they have to offer.
