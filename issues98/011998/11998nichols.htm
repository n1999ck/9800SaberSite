Judge decides Nichols fate

by Brain Hale / Staff Writer

Back to Frontpage

The Terry Nichols trial was expected to be very similar to the Timothy McVeigh trial. The trials were both expected to end with the defendants receiving the death penalty. Expectations were not met. This trial had a surprise ending.

It seems as though the two trials began to differ just before Christmas, when the five man, seven woman jury found Terry Nichols not guilty of carrying out the act of bombing the Oklahoma Murrah Federal Building on April 19, 1995. However, the jury did find him guilty of manslaughter for conspiring to carry out the bombing.

The jury went into deliberations with the split decision of guilty and not guilty, and were faced with the task of deciding the fate of Terry Nichols. After hours of deliberations, the decision was turned over to Judge Richard Matsch, because the jury had reached a standstill and was declared deadlocked.

The penalty decision is now up to Judge Matsch, but he cannot impose the death penalty. Federal sentencing guides dictate that the death penalty can only be given by a jury. Regardless of Judge Matsch's decision, prosecutors have promised to press charges against Nichols that carry the death penalty.

This decision, so far, has been an extremely unpopular one. The jury forewoman and nurse, Niki Deutchman, has received bomb threats at her home on three separate occasions since the jury failed to come to a decision.

Deutchman told the press, "There are some families of the victims that are enough upset that we've heard from them in some rather significant ways. We've been advised not to do any more media."

The families of the victims are not the only people unhappy with the way things are going in this trial. Terry Nichols' mother, Joyce Wilt, told local Oklahoma newspapers that the death penalty would have been better than what her son will now face. "My gosh, with him suffering, you know, what's his life going to be? Nothing. He has no future. Death would have been easier than him being put in there, he's being tortured for something he did not do, "she told The Oklahoma.

During court battles, prosecutors Pat Ryan and Beth Wilkinson both questioned the thinking behind Nichols' actions. Wilkinson asked the jury, "What in the world did he think the bomb would do to the people in that building?" Defense lawyer, Michael Tigar, often responded by asking the jury to not let the prosecution persuade them to anger and vengeance, reminding them that justice is what was being sought.

Timothy McVeigh is scheduled to die on June 13, and in all likelihood, Terry Nichols will still be in court.
