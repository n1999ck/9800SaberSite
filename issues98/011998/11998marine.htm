An Outdoor Classroom

by Evelyn Tolbert-Owens / Staff Writer

Back to Frontpage

December 1997 was exciting for 11 Biology and Pre-med majors and faculty members of the Biology Department, who attended Course number BIO495 in Florida. The short, high intensity intersession course in tropical marine biology was initiated in 1990 by Dr. William Birkhead and Dr. Glenn Stokes and offered by the CSU Biology Department in the CSU course catalogue.

The course allows students to study the ecosystems of Florida first hand. The marine biology intersession course entailed a six-day trip to Florida, December 6-11, to explore, see and hear about various ecosystems and to visit three marine institutes and research centers. The students got full credit for this course.

After financial help from the Student Government Fund for Student Academic Activities the cost was $220,- per student. This does not include the regular course registration fees.

The students had to rough it a little and put in a good deal of work and effort. Each student had to prepare a seminar about a certain community and present it during the trip right before the group would saw the actual community.

Seminars conducted during the trip concerned subjects such as the Everglades sawgrass community, island formation, cypress swamp, coral reef communities, turtle grass marine community, hardwood hammock community, mangrove community, hard- and soft-bottom marine communities, algae community and beach community.

Reports from some of the students and faculty members who participated were brimming with excitement about an incredible, unforgettable experience. Also, there were comments about hard work, swimming in cold water at 8:30 in the morning and roughing in on the floor for a good night’s sleep.

The group left Columbus, GA on December 6, heading for Naples, Florida to spend the night at a environmental learning center on the floor of the lab. December 7th started with a trip to Corkscrew Swamp where the students got a chance to look around a Cyprus swamp and experience the different communities inhabiting it. They traveled on through the Everglades looking at a sawgrass marsh and then continued on to Marathon Key, just north of Key West to spend the night. The next day, December 8, they traveled on to Big Pine Key. There, the students toured the Harbor Branch Marine Institute, a high-tech oceanographic research center. Their day ended at the Newfound Harbor Marine Institute on Big Pine Key where they would stay for the next three days, observing the flora and fauna present in the area, exploring the mangrove trees and tidal communities, as well as doing some snorkeling to observe coral reef communities. Oh, and let’s not forget about the night time trip to Key West to experience first hand the various nightlife communities there...

On December 10th the group started on their way back home. They stopped to spend the night at Fort Pierce, just below Cape Canaveral, at the Johnson & Johnson Deep Sea Facility where the group had the opportunity to tour the facility and look at various submersible deep sea exploration equipment. They arrived back in Columbus on December 11.

The participating students were Joan Bingham, Jana Chalkley, Shannon Cotton, Thomas Hunter, Matt Martin, Tom McCoy, Dianne O’Shea, Marcie Parrish, Elizabeth Sikes, Dorana Vasquez-Hernandez, and Tarish Fields. Faculty escorting the group were Drs. Bill Birkhead, Glenn Stokes, Julie Ballenger, and George Stanton.
Other courses such as this one to visit the Bahamas, Haiti and Panama, for hands-on experience are currently in the planning stages and the territories are being explored for their eco-systems by Dr. Julie Ballenger and Dr. Birkhead.
