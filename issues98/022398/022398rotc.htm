What is ROTC?

by Evelyn Tolbert - Owens / Staff Writer

 
Most of us have heard the term ROTC and know has something to do with the military and trying to be “all that you can be”.

ROTC stands for Reserve Officer Training Course and is offered here at CSU. It is a four year program geared to help develop the future officer leadership of the US Army.

A student is given classroom instruction on leadership techniques with strong emphasis placed on practical application in a field setting. When the student successfully completes the military science curriculum and various other requirements established by the Department of the Army, he or she will be commissioned as a 2nd Lieutenant and may serve in the active army or the reserves and national guard.

Increasing awareness about the importance of self-discipline, physical fitness and good leadership skills, the program is open to undergraduate and graduate students interested in personal growth and development.

The program is split into two major phases: The basic course and the advanced course. Each course lasts over a period of two years.

If a student enrolls at his or her own expense or with other financial aid without taking advantage of a military scholarship, they can participate in the program without signing a contract obligating themselves to ever become part of the military.

A student could become part of the reserves or national guard and draw a paycheck from the military. The time spent in the reserves or military guard will then, should the student later decide to become active army, be credited to their “years of service”. He or she would start out with a higher salary than the average Lieutenant, just joining the military, without any previous reserve or national guard experience.

If a student signs up on a military scholarship, tuition and books will be paid for, however, you will obligate yourself to the Army for a minimum of eight years after you have completed your degree. These eight years can either be spent in the active military (2 - 4 years minimum) or the reserves or National Guard (8 years minimum).

A sophomore is required to attend military classes one hour per week. A freshman would take two hours per week. PT (physical training) on Mondays, Wednesdays, and Fridays from 6:00 a.m. until 7:00 a.m. is still optional at this point. Military activities also include two 3-day field exercises per year. Physical training tests are given at the beginning and the end of the quarter/semester to determine physical fitness of the students as well as to determine the students’ progress.

Courses such as “Intro to The Army Today”, “Basic Military Skills” and “Map Reading/Navigation” Courses are part of the basic course curriculum. Non-scholarship participants can experience military lifestyles first hand to see if they would like to consider a military career.

Military activities remain the same over the last two years, however, a junior or senior will take three hours of military classes per week and PT will no longer be optional. At the beginning of the advanced course, the student who is not on a military scholarship will be officially sworn in and will sign a contract with the US Army that, upon completion of the program, he/she will join the military and serve as a commissioned officer for a minimum of eight years.

At the end of the third year the student will attend a five week camp at Ft. Louis, Washington to determine their leadership potential. Aside from this camp the student will take advanced classes on the subject of military leadership, management, military ethics, professionalism and the military justice system.
