Monday, November 9, 1998

CSU makes good use of Fulbright-Hays award

by Michael Cox / Editor-in-Chief

Thirteen faculty members from various institutions in Georgia traveled to Singapore and Malaysia this past summer. The trip was made possible by the prestigious Fulbright-Hays Grant which matches $45,000 and allowed these thirteen faculty to study and travel in Singapore and Malaysia for 4 weeks. The grant is the fifth Fulbright Grant Columbus State University has received from the United States Department of Education.

The purpose of the faculty development program is to promote mutual understanding between people of the United States and other countries through educational and cultural exchange. The program is organized by the National University of Singapore, University Putra Malaysia, Columbus State University and the Nine University and College International Studies Consortium of Georgia. The 13 members participating in the program consisted of social science and humanities professors from universities and colleges in Georgia.

Rajgopal Sashti, Director of the Nine University and College International Studies Consortium of Georgia, was able to explain the main reason for having the program in this portion of the region. “Singapore and Malaysia are located in one of the most strategic trade routes of the world between east and west”, says Sashti. He explains, “both are relatively small countries and although many in the United States know about Singapore as a result of the Michael Faye incident in 1994, today the relationship between the U.S. and Singapore is at its best.”

Mr. Sashti was able to tell me the significance of the two countries and gave me some insight regarding Singapore and Malaysia as well. He mentioned that Singapore is a small country with the world’s second most competitive economy. Its economy is very successful with a per capita of $26,000 a year. Singapore is described by Sashti as being “a gleaming, modern, western-looking city built out of nothing.” For such a small city, Singapore contains the third largest oil refining country in the world and boasts a superior education system. “Singapore’s economy is strong and solid,” says Sashti.

Malaysia was also explored during the trip and contained very interesting aspects for the participants. Apart from Singapore, Malaysia maintains global interdependence containing both money and a wealthy economy. The government encourages religious and cultural harmony with Islam being the straight religion of the country.

Singapore and Malaysia have become separated, but Malaysia has become a successful country. According to Sashti, the trip was very successful and educational in part due to the seminars, conferences, and field trips which the participants attended.

Sashti states, “Columbus State University has emerged as a leader in training and development among the 34 colleges and universities within the University System of Georgia.”
