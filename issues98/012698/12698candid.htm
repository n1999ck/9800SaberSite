Campus Candid: Dorion Williams

by Athena Curry / Editor-in-Chief

Graduate student Dorion Williams relieved a Houston Sports Medical Scholarship recently. The scholarship recognized William’s potential to be a future community leader. Williams wrote an essay, which focused on giving back to the community, in order to win the scholarship.

Williams graduated CSU with a bachelor’s degree in psychology and is now a graduate student majoring in public administration with an emphasis in student personnel. He is graduate assistant of minority affairs on campus and works as a trainer and assistant supervisor at Total Systems.

Williams is a well known, well respected and well liked figure on the CSU campus. CSU student ,Bryan Kirby, says, “He’s [Williams] a real good friend and fun to hang out with.”

Allison Rost, CSU student says of Williams, “He’s a super guy. Really laid back.” Both Kirby and Rost agree that Williams is the kind of person that makes friends easily.

Although many on campus have nice things to say about Williams’ friendly disposition, they also stress the dedication he shows in the organizations he is involved in.

Kirby says that Williams is definitely a driving force behind the success of the Student Programming Council. He says, “Dorion is great at getting entertainment events going.”

Aside from his work with the Student Programming Council as an undergraduate, Williams is a member of Summus. Summus is an organization for minority men which focuses on keeping young men in school.

Recently, the Summus men volunteered their time tutoring at Eastway Elementary School. Williams says of the students involved in Summus, “They’re really making us proud.”

Williams is a member of Alpha Phi Alpha fraternity and a member of Masonic Lodge 697.

He serves as a liaison between community youth and the mentoring program, Men of Action. Williams says, “We stress commitment and strength to young people.”

Despite the success he has achieved in the last several years, Williams was not always so motivated, “I chose to make F’s during my first two years of high school. A counselor told me that I couldn’t get a College Preparatory diploma.”

Williams says that being told he could not do something made him more determined. “I hit a brick wall and it’s been an upward climb ever since,” he says.

Williams graduated with the College Preparatory diploma and came to CSU. He says it was a big transition for him because the students at the Jr. High School and High School he attended were predominately African Americans. Williams says coming to CSU was a great chance to get to know other cultures and meet people who were different in some ways than himself. He also says it was a shock to be in such a new environment. “The environment here teaches you that you have to understand cultural differences. [Coming to CSU] Was the best thing that ever happened to me.”

Williams offers advice to others, “I’ve been through a lot. If I can do it anyone can. Go by your word.”

He says his mother and father have been a big influence in his life as well as his older brother. “My big brother kept knocking me in the head, telling me I could do anything,” Williams says.
Upon earning his graduate degree Williams plans to work in a job that involves working with college students in building new programs. Williams says, “I have a plan laid out to give back.”
