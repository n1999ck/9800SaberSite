Where were you?

On Thursday, January 15th, the Office of Minority Affairs sponsored a program dedicated to the memory of Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr.. A small group of CSU students organized this event for the enrichment of the CSU student community. Those of you unfamiliar with Dr. King’s philosophies would be given the chance to learn who this man was and what he stood for. Those of you who are aware of Dr. King’s history would have the opportunity to reflect on the important role he has played in our lives. Rev. Dr. Robert Dickerson, Jr. was invited to speak to the students of CSU, but where were you?

I was disappointed, not to mention embarrassed, by the apathy shown by the students of this university. The number of empty seats were far greater than the number of students in attendance. I am aware that some of you had to go to class or work, but I don’t understand why the rest of you didn’t bother to come to the Columbus Room on Thursday. Did everyone suddenly have something better or more important to do? Did no one see the many signs and flyers hanging around campus, advertising this program? I imagine that if this had been a basketball game, the Blizzard of Bucks, or a cookout with free food there wouldn’t have been an empty seat in the room. I would like to think that the CSU student community just doesn’t understand the significance of Dr. King’s contribution to our society. It would be sad to think that you just don’t care.

Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr. fought and died so that every youth might have an equal opportunity to succeed in life. It was his dream that people would be “judged not by the color of their skin, but by the content of their character.” Without the efforts of Dr. King, and other leaders like him, our society may still be segregated. Imagine a world where the color of your skin determined which restaurants you could eat in, which schools you could attend, which parts of town you could live in, or even which fountains you could drink from. This country may still have a long way to go before we achieve true racial harmony, but by learning about the past you can appreciate how far our society has come. It is for this reason that men like Dr. King should be remembered and honored.

For those who weren’t there, you missed a great program. You would have been moved by the remarks of the student speakers (Jason Jones, Kevin Sellers, and Maurice Williams), the poem read by Taesha Lewis, and the musical selections led by Shana Teel. The highlight of the program was an eloquent speech delivered by Rev. Dr. Robert M. Dickerson, Jr., pastor of the First African Baptist Church here in Columbus. We were urged not to forget the legacy of Dr. King. He also encouraged the audience to find and follow their own dreams.

I hope all of you enjoyed your long weekend. The holiday was set aside to pay homage to one of the greatest men in American history. May those of you who didn’t bother to attend the program take the time to learn more about Dr. King and his philosophies. You have a responsibility to yourself to not only have fun in college, but to broaden your knowledge and experience as well. Perhaps if you learn more about his accomplishments, you will be able to appreciate the significance of honoring his memory in these types of programs.

- A Concerned CSU Student
