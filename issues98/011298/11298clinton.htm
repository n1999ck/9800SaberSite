Clinton Proposal May Affect College Students

by Heather Warren / Associate Editor

Back to Frontpage

President Clinton proposed an expansion in federal work-study assistance for college students. The plan is a $70 million plan to expand federal work-study programs for college students. Clinton proposed the plan in a speech on Friday while in Texas. The proposal will be included in Clinton’s budget request for the 1999 fiscal year. The plan will represent only a modest increase in higher education spending. The new work-study money, if approved by Congress, would bring the number of students in the program to 1 million next year, up from about 940, 000 at present according to White House Spokesman Barry Toiv.

Clinton also used the speech to call attention to several measures passed last year to make college more affordable. Under the work study program, colleges receive federal money to provide jobs for students. Clinton has frequently championed the program, citing his own student work experience.

In January 1996, Clinton proposed raising the number of students in work-study programs by 50 percent, to a total of 1 million, by the year 2000. Congress created 200,000 new work-study positions in 1996, at an extra cost of more than $200 million.

In a speech on Thursday, Clinton said he also was working to ensure that people on welfare can qualify for work study aid so they can work their way through school. Several higher-education aid programs were passed in connection with last year’s deal to balance the federal budget.

They included “HOPE Scholarship” tax credits up to $1500 to help pay for the first two years of college, tax credits up to $1000, rising to $2000 by the year 2003, for third-and fourth-year college students, graduate students, and continuing education students, and provisions to use money in Individual Retirement Accounts for college costs. On the United States Government education homepage it says that “when fully phased in 12.9 million students—5.8 million claiming the HOPE Scholarship, and 7.1 million claiming the Lifetime Learning Credit—are expected to benefit each year.
