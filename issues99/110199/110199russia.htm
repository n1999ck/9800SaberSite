When I was a kid in the eighties, I remember my father and I once driving down a secluded dirt road near our rural Pennsylvania home and stopping at the remains of a never finished house. All that stood there was a cinder block basement topped with a cement ceiling that was also the top-level floor. If I was out in the woods playing and the sirens, which were posted all over the county, went off, I was to meet my family at this location. Apparently my father dubbed this basement the safest nearby structure for our family to retreat to in case of Soviet attack. Now, my father wasn’t a militant extremist or anything. He merely shared the common fears of millions of Americans: the fear that one day the United States would find itself in the throes of nuclear war with the Soviet Union. Still today, you can find houses built between the fifties and eighties that have basements made into bomb shelters. They were built with double thick cement walls, steel doors and no windows. And do you remember the eighties movie, “Red Dawn” about the Soviets parachuting into a small Montana town? 

With the collapse of the Soviet Union and the new efforts at democracy that Americans are seeing in that region, we laugh at our old paranoia of Ivan invading our shores. But even as we stand on the brink of the twenty-first century, there still remain some apprehensions about that country over there in the east. Who are those people on that cold continent far away? And why should we care anyway, if they are no longer a threat to our national security?

The United Sates may still have some great interests in Russia, according to Rajgopal Sashti, director of grants and sponsor programs for CSU. That is why Dr. Sashti and Olice Embry, professor of business management at CSU, along with a team of 13 other professors and teachers from various educational institutions in Georgia traveled to Russia last summer. According to Dr. Sashti, Russia still has the largest oil reserve in the world. Also, they are still a military threat since they maintain their military and nuclear technology but have a developing government, not fully adjusted to a democratic system.
So why send American professors to Russia? Through the Fullbright-Hayes Program, the United States Department of Education sends groups of American professors to various foreign countries and hosts professors from those countries as well. The idea is to dispel the misconceptions and mistrust between the governments of the U.S. and foreign countries. 

This past summer, Dr. Sashti says that they visited with Russian families, mingled with average Russian people on public transportation and met in conferences with Russian professors. None of the Americans that went were experts on the Russian culture or language and none of them had ever been to Russia before. They spent their time trying to understand the Russians--their culture, their lifestyles and their heritage. Dr. Sashti was convinced of the richness of the Russian culture. The group found that Russians enjoy their newfound freedom under democracy but struggle economically in the infant stages of capitalism. 

How do these trips benefit CSU students? Through these trips, professors gain a better understanding of other cultures and ways of life. They are then able to instill in CSU students a well-rounded worldview. In fact, Dr. Ambry has put together a presentation for students called “Russia A to Z Economics.”

This has been the eighth year that CSU has been awarded the $59,000 grant from the federal government to travel abroad on missions of peace. Last year a group led by Dr. Sashti went to Malaysia and Singapore, and next year Dr. Sashti hopes to take a group to Egypt and Israel.
