When I started college three years ago, I had the perfect plan. I would finish my bachelor’s degree in exactly four years, complete my masters in two years (at another school), teach for a few years (I’m a music education major), and then go back to school to finish a doctoral degree. It was supposed to be as easy as that. That dream lasted a good two quarters (and we were on quarters when I started school) before I realized it was just that--a dream.

Some departments around the University began advising sessions with students this week. Mine went fairly well for two reasons. I went in prepared to ask questions about my degree, and my advisor was not afraid to answer these questions or, if she didn’t know, to find someone who did. 

Advising is something that should be important to students and professors. If the students don’t have some idea of how their degree process is going, then advising is worthless. Anyone who walks into a professor’s office expecting them to tell you exactly what to take and when, you are sorely mistaken. You should have at least an idea of what you need to take in your major that semester, and some clue as to what core you want. Otherwise it’s a fruitless effort. 

But it’s not all about the student. The advisor has a pretty big job as well. And some aren’t really sure what they are doing. I’ve seen advisors who have taught in a department for 20 years that still don’t know what students on different tracks should be taking to complete their major requirements. 

They are the ones that look at you funny when you mention the words core curriculum. These are the advisors that tell you to take a class that you won’t end up needing, which you end up failing, which drops your GPA below 3.0, which means you lose your HOPE and have to leave school. All because of one bad advisor. 

Then there are the advisors who are very interested in each student and how each is progressing. They are the ones that sit down with you and try to work out problems with conflicting classes and substitution of credits. And if they don’t know whether a class is required or whether you really need it they find someone who does know. 

Amazing--some people actually pick up the phone and call till they get an answer for you. These people should be knighted for this. Most of their advisees will complete their program in four years (or close to four years). 

I’ve had friends who were told that they didn’t need to worry about class X. Everything would be fine if they took it later. Those people are now on the five, and some six year plan because of not taking a class when they should have. That mistake cost them a year in their college career because some advisor didn’t know what they were saying. If it sounded good coming out of their mouth, they said it.

Perhaps there needs to be a balance developed between advisors and students. The students should come in to advising prepared, but the advisors should know something about each student (at least their name), and be open to trying to help them with their problems. And the advisors need to know what students in their major need to be taking each semester. If not, we’re going to have a school full of students starting their second senior year dreaming about that great four year plan they once had. To quote a line of Les Miserables, “ I dreamed a dream.”
