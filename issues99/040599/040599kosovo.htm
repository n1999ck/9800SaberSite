Threat of war prompts opinion on campus
Clarissa Andrews / Staff Writer

By this point in time, almost everyone knows something about what is going on in Yugoslavia, even if it’s just that NATO forces initiated a large air strike last week.

Even students who rarely watch the news or read newspapers know a few details about the situation. While students might not have cared about what was happening on the other side of the world a month ago, the possibility of going to war has stirred up some opinions.

Like many students, Brent Lindley, has mixed emotions: “The United States has money, so I believe we should help the people over there. But I think we could help in other ways-it’s not necessary to risk American lives.” One way Lindley feels this could be done is by sending forces to train the Kosovars to fight for themselves.

Many Americans are comparing Slobodan Milosevic to Adolph Hitler, but Lindley says that everyone should have a chance. He believes it is unfair to make assumptions as to what Milosevic will do in the future.

Dusty Holcomb, on the other hand, is in complete support of the American army’s involvement in Kosovo. He says people have forgotten about World War II.

“Preventive action is a wise choice. I don’t want my kids to have to read about another Holocaust,” Holcomb says.

Another CSU student, who prefers to remain anonymous states, “I read somewhere that there aren’t enough American ground forces to stand man-to-man against the Serbs. If they impose the draft, I’m moving to Canada.”

There are people around campus, however, who are taking the matter as seriously as it should be taken.

Dr. Mion, political science professor, says, “I have no problem with the comparison of Milosevic to Hitler.” He says Milosevic is an evil-minded man, but objects to the idea that he is “crazy.”

According to Dr. Mion, Milosevic is very rational. “He wants Kosovo, whatever the price-if it means slaughtering Kosovars and Albanians, he doesn’t care,” Dr. Mion says.
Dr. Mion says the most difficult question about the situation is whether Americans should actually be involved. “I like to call it a lose-lose situation. The military cannot be a solution to political problems.”

Either way one looks at it, American involvement causes conflicting feelings. People are being pushed out of Albania, and there is much suffering, says Dr. Mion. Yet that doesn’t answer the question of national interest; namely, is it the United States’ interest to fight the Serbs? Should American forces go into Yugoslavia to help save lives and risk their own lives in the process? People are not in favor of this idea, but they don’t want to hear accusations at a later point of simply letting in happen.

Dr. Mion claims that his classes are divided on the subject, but feel the United States should be cautious. Few people are saying, “Let’s move in with ground troops,” but the bombing doesn’t bother people as much if the pilots come back safely.

“Yugoslavia is a sovereign country,” says Dr. Mion. “Bringing in ground troops will harden the opposition against us.”

Students should be more interested in what is going on around them, according to Dr. Mion. “Students ought to be citizens, and citizens ought to be educated, especially in a democracy.”
