While walking through the halls or browsing one of the many bulletin boards here on campus, you may have seen flyers for a volunteer program called College Bound. College Bound is not your ordinary volunteer organization. It is an innovative and creative program designed to allow college and elementary school students to have a meaningful and lasting impact on one another’s lives.

College Bound was created by the McBride Foundation in 1994 to provide disadvantaged children in grades four through six with a positive and practical introduction to the many aspects of higher education. College students volunteer to become Student Facilitators that visit an elementary school one hour a week for five weeks to discuss things like admission processes, financial aid, and campus organizations and activities. On the sixth visit, the elementary students receive a guided tour of the college campus.

One of the most unique aspects of the College Bound program is the “reverse-mentoring” concept. The elementary students become an Advisory Council to the Student Facilitator, giving the college student advice on the day to day issues of college life. 

The Advisory Council is a means of encouraging each child’s personal participation in the program by making he or she an important part of the college student’s life. It also builds self-esteem by giving the children an opportunity to share their opinions.

All together, College Bound requires about fifteen to twenty hours of the volunteer’s time. The program begins with an orientation process, which consists of a twenty-minute video that gives the volunteer a brief overview of the program. The volunteer also receives a handbook that includes a step by step outline of each visit.

The College Bound program runs about every six weeks and the number of Student Facilitators vary with each run. Jay Kimbrough, who coordinates the program here at CSU, says that ideally he “would like to see about eight to ten” Student Facilitators per run.

According to Kimbrough, the program has received extremely positive feedback from both elementary and college students alike. He feels that the program has been very successful and that students who participate in it “get the reward of helping young kids and the personal satisfaction that come from that.”

Kimbrough and Rob Smith, the program’s student assistant, organize virtually all of the program’s details and paperwork ahead of time. This way the Student Facilitator is required to do a minimal amount of preparation, and is therefore able to gain as much as possible from the experience. 

Participation in College Bound is open to CSU students of all majors and educational areas. The only requirements are that the student has completed at least one semester of college and has a reliable means of transportation to the elementary school and back. Interested students should contact either Rob Smith or Jay Kimbrough.
