New Burger King rolls in
by Helen Godwin / Associate Editor

[picture caption] With construction well under way, it will not be very long before Burger King provides CSU students with another food option close to campus.

Columbus will soon reap the benefits of a trip back in time to an old fashion trolley station.

Schuster Enterprises is currently building a new Burger King on Gentian Boulevard, across from the Columbus State University campus. The theme coincides with Columbus’ future plans for a trolley system to service CSU and Flat Rock Park.

With a predicted open date of early June, this “trolley station” will have all of the modern conveniences. It will feature Burger King’s infamous talking trash cans and an indoor playground.

Burger King’s dining area will seat 200 and there will be two rooms available to reserve for meetings or parties. One room’s theme will be a model train car and the other will have an old-fashioned diner motif.

This new restaurant will have a drive-thru designed to facilitate those in a hurry and looking for convenience.

If you want it your way right away, according to Schuster Enterprises, Burger King is the place to go because there are 1,024 possible ways for a customer to order a Whopper sandwich.

The landscape, according to Schuster Enterprises, Inc., “is designed to create a neighborhood-friendly atmosphere.” This is why the parking lot is separated from the drive through and there will be many outside lights.

Not only will this business benefit the stomachs of Columbus, but also the economy. This facility will employ 50-75 people and its payroll will be over $500,000 annually.

Faculty and student reaction has been very positive. Graylen Bishop, a part time instructor at CSU says, “It will be good for the college because it is an alternative. It would behoove them (Schuster Enterprises) to build a cross walk like what is on Victory Drive just so that it is safer for the students to cross.”

“Having competition close by will affect our sales, no matter who it is,” says Dan Silver, Food Service Director at CSU, “but, we are starting to offer various benefits to choosing us over other restaurants like combo meals so that instead of Burger King being competition for us, we can be competition for them.”

Schuster Enterprises, Inc. owns and operates 11 Burger Kings in Columbus and Phenix City. This new Burger King will be the 55th restaurant in the Schuster system.

A leader in today’s fast-food industry, with locations in all 50 states and 56 countries and territories around the world, Burger King Corporation was founded in 1954 in Miami, Florida, by James McLamore and David Edgerton.

Since its Florida beginnings more than 43 years ago when a Burger King hamburger cost 18¢ and a Whopper sandwich cost 37¢, Burger King Corporation has established restaurants around the world — from Australia to the Bahamas, and from Venezuela to Hong Kong.

Today, there are more than 300,000 Burger King employees in more than 9,400 locations worldwide.

CSU recruitment doesn't miss the boat
by Clarissa Andrews / Staff Writer

As spring semester is drawing to a close for CSU students, many high school juniors across Columbus are anxiously awaiting SAT scores, updated grade point averages, and class rankings so that they can mail their transcripts to universities near and far.

Due to a new junior recruitment program at CSU, the admissions staff is hoping those juniors will consider mailing their applications across town rather than across the state.

According to Joel Judah, president of SGA, local high school students, “may not be aware of how great CSU is - our goal is to make them aware of what’s in their own back yard.”

In order to do this, members of the Offices of Public Relations, Admissions, University College, students, faculty, staff, and alumni have worked together to devise ways to get Columbus high school juniors’ attention.

Cheryl Gaston, Director of the Public Relations, says that since January, four contacts have been made with about 300 juniors from Columbus.

First, an information packet consisting of pamphlets, a hand-signed letter from the president of the University, a video, a CSU front license plate, and a CSU car sticker was sent to every junior with a GPA of 3.0 or higher, who is actively involved in his or her school. These students were recommended by their guidance counselors. Also in the information packet was an invitation for the students and their parents to attend a free pizza party on Sunday, May 2. The party offers prospective students a chance to walk around the campus, meet students and faculty from their areas of interest, and talk to outstanding young alumni, among other things.

The second contact with the students was a phone call made by CORE students in admissions, followed by a service leadership brochure. The latest contact was a second invitation to the pizza party.

Although the reply card count is fairly low so far, Gaston has high hopes for success in the new recruitment attempts. She says that by not making sure Columbus high school students had this information in the past, CSU was really, “missing the boat.”

This year, however, Public Relations, Admissions, and all other groups involved are assuring that local students realize what a wonderful school CSU is. Gaston claims that many people’s ideas about the University have changed in the past six or so years, but she is still convinced that the local market does not know us as well as it should.

One of the best aspects of this project is that it will last through the fall, not just ending with the pizza party. Every interested junior will be in contact with a person from CSU, either a student or a faculty member, and should receive personal letters from the contact periodically to maintain interest levels.

Gaston says it is important to recruit students through personalization, not just sending out information about the college. “The best way to tell our story,” she states, “is through the people who are experiencing it.”

SGA Elections today
by Bridgette Brumbaugh / Staff Writer

Student Government elections are being held today and tomorrow and all students have a chance to vote on which candidates they want representing them. The student government affects various aspects of student life and the responsibilities of this organization, as well as its members, should be considered.

The Student Government Association’s members vote on policies, academic activities, and monetary issues, which affect the student body. A few issues brought before the Student Government included parking ticket disputes and money requests.

The association is divided into an executive council, a voting forum, and a judicial branch. Each branch has specific responsibilities for the elected officials.

The president has control over all committees and is responsible for making sure each one is addressing their area of concern. This person is the official representative of the student body and is a delegate to the Board of Regents. He or she serves on the activities committee, alumni council, and any other committees required by the university. The president appoints a secretary as well.

The vice president of scholastic affairs formulates policies involving academic areas affecting students. He or she is chair of the annual social and philanthropic projects of the group and serves on the activities and curriculum committee.

The vice president of finance prepares a budget, which is approved by the executive committee, and oversees all budget amendments and money requests. This person advises the executive committee on money issues and serves on the activities committee.

Senators vote on various issues, serve on committees, and voice their opinions. They work to get student issues addressed in forum.

The chief justice presides over the judicial council and is the judge in cases of controversy. He or she advises the executive council on constitutional matters upon request. The chief justice writes the majority opinion and files reports with the appropriate officials.

Elections are being held from 8 a.m. - 8 p.m., at the Davidson Center outdoor dining area. If it rains, the voting booth will be located inside the Public Safety Lobby of Davidson. Each student must show a picture ID and names will be verified on the official CSU roster.

Help for writers
by Leslie Maxwell / Staff Writer

Many colleges and universities around the country have writing centers where students can consult with peer tutors in order to improve their writing skills. CSU has now joined these colleges and established a writing center of its own, staffed with trained peer tutors.

At this time, the Writing Center is operating out of the Writing Lab, which is located in Arnold 207. Tutors are available by appointment to help students with their assignments. Appointments are available during a variety of times, and they last 20 minutes.

Dr. Noreen Lape, who has been instrumental in creating the CSU Writing Center (WC), says, “The Writing Center is a great place for writers of all abilities to go, share ideas, and improve their writing skills.”

The peer tutors who staff the WC have been through a semester-long training course. They are trained in a variety of techniques, and they can assist a student in any stage of the writing process. Tutors can help students understand an assignment, brainstorm ideas, or, if the student has a draft of a paper, the tutor can offer suggestions for improving it. In addition, tutors can explain teacher’s comments or answer questions about paper formats, such as MLA style.

Mrs. Cathy Fussell, coordinator of the Writing Lab, says the WC is a “place outside the classroom where students can talk about writing and become immersed in the academic community.”

Many students do not have a chance to engage in academic conversation outside of the classroom, so the WC serves as a place to talk informally about papers and generate ideas.

The WC also gives students a chance to receive feedback from a peer instead of their instructor since many students seem hesitant to approach their instructor with questions that seem “stupid” or “insignificant”.

Dr. Lape, who teaches English 1101 and 1102, says, “Many of my students who have used the WC have shown great improvement in their writing skills, and they are becoming more confident and better writers.”

The English faculty and the WC peer tutors hope that everyone will come and explore the new services that are being offered.

SGA's 'Last Nite Out' blast
by Clarissa Andrews / Staff Writer

After graduation this May, most students are planning to go out to eat with their families and close friends and then go home to veg out in front of the television, resting their brains for the first time in years. Other students who still have a while before graduation will probably do the same, just without the going out to dinner part. If a Saturday night in front of the television doesn’t sound appealing to you, the Student Government Association is offering a better plan.

On Saturday, May 15, at 8:00 p.m., SGA will host the first annual ‘Last Nite Out’ Party.

The event, held at the clock tower, will be open to all CSU students to provide an opportunity for everyone to come together one last time.

SGA President Joel Judah says, “Our hope is that [the Last Nite Out] will help to build more of a cohesive spirit among the students of CSU.”

Refreshments will include soft drinks and finger foods, such as chips and dip.

Although Judah says there will be music, it is unsure whether there will be a band or a disc jockey.

Plans for the celebration began at an SGA executive council committee meeting when the members discussed ideas to give CSU students something to do after graduation other than go home. SGA is hoping that at least 1,000 people will join the fun and that the weather will be nice.

Judah says, “It’s our goal to give back to students, and this is just another way to give something back.”

On Campus

Within a Room
by Leslie Maxwell / Columnist

All of the signs ‘round campus advertisin’ the “Festival of Southern Culture” have made me start thinkin’ about what “Southern Culture” really is, and what makes it different, say, from “Northern Culture.” Now, I’m not talkin’ about “You might be a redneck if...” southern culture, or pick-up truckin’, rebel flag flyin’, Harley Davidson tattoo-type southern culture. I’m talkin’ about the regional cultural differences that most of us are never even aware of until someone points them out.

For instance, there is a huge difference in the way northern people and southern people give commands or offer suggestions. A northern person might say, “This is wrong; do it this way,” but a bonafide South Georgia peach like me would choose a more indirect route. I would say, “Are you sure this is what you meant to do? I might be wrong, but it seems to me that it would work better this way.” A southern person hears that statement as “This is wrong; fix it,” but the northern person is thinking, “Why doesn’t she just say what she means?” So to the southern person “Do it this way” sounds a little harsh, while to the northern person, southern indirection sounds a little wishy-washy.

There are also some words, beyond “y’all” and “pop” that are only used in certain regions. Consider the word “MASH” for example. A southern person would say, “Hey, what button do I MASH to turn this thing on?” A northern person would reply, “Mash? You mean what button would you PRESS to turn that thing on?” And so the interminable arguments continue: y’all or you guys, pop or coke, and of course, pécán, pécan, or pecán. Of course, I think y’all know that I drink a coke (even if it’s a Mountain Dew and I eat pécáns (that’s pronounced pee-ca-ans).

My parents work for a northern family that owns a large hunting plantation in South Georgia, so all my life, I have been around people from up north. I can’t tell you how many times I have heard, “I just love the way you talk. It is so adorable. Say something else.” I’m surprised that I’m not emotionally scarred.

Last summer, however, I had my first chance to visit the New England states. I remember sitting in a New Jersey restaurant with my family, listening to the people at nearby tables. I was thinking, “I can’t believe that they come down south and think I sound funny; listen to them!” However, I did have the good grace not to ask the waitress to repeat herself simply for my listening pleasure. I came home from that trip with a new understanding of how northern people hear us when they visit the south. Good gracious, we must sound funny!

Personally, I think that regional dialects and cultural variations are fascinating. We all just need to remember that in the “melting pot” of America, some ingredients, like dialects and customs, just refuse to melt. We will never all be the same, but then again, isn’t a tossed salad more interesting than tomato soup? People aren’t defined by regional idiosyncrasies; instead, they are enhanced by them. I wouldn’t give up my southern twang or my Harley Davidson boots for anything, but that’s just my opinion; I might be wrong...

Islamic Exhibit at CSU
by Elke Brumbaugh / Staff Writer

[picture caption] The Islamic Association educates the campus about the Islamic culture.

Islam is a mystery to many Americans, but the CSU Islamic Association is trying to demystify the religion and way of life by educating and presenting Islam accurately to students.

On April 12 and 13 the Islamic Association hosted an exhibit in the lobby of the Fine Arts Hall. Displays included posters, books, brochures on the history and beliefs of Islam, prayer rugs, Marhaba (used in prayer), books comparing Christianity to Islam, information on the roles of men and women, tea pots and cups, money from Egypt, a wooden stand used to hold the Qu’ran while reading, and a picture of the place of prayer in Mecca.

The CSU Islamic Association has existed for approximately one year and is very eager to work with all the organizations on campus, including the Baptist Student Union, the Freethought Society, and SGA. They also coordinate with the Islamic Associations on the campuses of Georgia Tech and the University of Georgia.

The association plans to bring speakers in from our country as well as overseas to discuss Islamic issues.

The association currently meets every Wednesday at 6:00 p.m. in the library. All students are welcome to attend for open discussion.

Public Safety Means YOUR Safety
by Sgt. Jim Boling / Public Safety Officer

The phone rings - you pick it up. Someone on the other end asks for “George,” or “Mary,” or whatever. You inform this unknown party that they have the wrong number, but before you can hang up, he/she says something (it could be anything) that catches your attention, and pretty soon you’re chatting like old friends. You wouldn’t know this person from Adam, yet you talk, and he/she talks, and before you know it, you’ve exchanged names, addresses, signs of the Zodiac, etc.

Imagine the same scenario happening via computer. It seems like everyone has email today, and the same possibilities for meeting people exist in that medium as via “wrong number.” The problem is, the person you’re chatting with by email may be in the same room with you, hunched over their keyboard while watching you.

Should you worry about any of the above? You bet! Since the dawn of time, there have been predators and stalkers out there; now, however, they can satisfy their weird obsessions by electronic media, instead of running the risk of being caught by physically following their victims. Can they reach through the WWW or the Internet and attack you? Well, yes and no. Their attack may be emotional or psychological, rather than physical, but it can hurt just as bad. Even worse, they can charm their way into your confidence over a period of time, until you are actually willing to arrange a physical meeting. That’s where it really gets dangerous. No matter how long you have “known” this individual, you don’t really know them at all. Don’t EVER attempt a rendezvous such as that by yourself. You are walking on extremely treacherous ground even with someone along, but to keep a solo appointment with a total stranger is just asking for trouble.

Remember the “wrong number” phone call - you wouldn’t rush out to meet that person (face it, they’re too stupid to dial the correct number, anyway, OR, they’re just out to lure you into something you don’t want to do), so don’t treat the “Cyber-pal” any differently. They still are strangers, and like Mom always said: “Don’t talk to strangers.” It’s still good advice.

Duties:
Duties and responsibilities taken from the SGA Constitution.

President
1. Preside over the forum and act as a voting member.
2. Be the chief executive officer and be responsible for the implementation of student government policies.
3. Serve as official spokesperson and representative of the student body.
4. Chair the executive committee and have a veto requiring a majority of executive committee votes to be overridden.
5. Serve on the alumni council and other such committees as required by the university.
6. Act as a delegate to the student advisory council, Board of Regents.
7. Have the authority to sign purchase orders if the vice president of finance is unable to do so.
8. Serve on the activities committee.
9. Have a veto in the forum, which can be overridden by a three-fourths (3/4) vote of the members present.
10. Assign university committees to the vice presidents and speakers as their primary areas of concern.

Vice President of Scholastic Affairs
1. Be responsible for formulating policies concerning academic areas that affect the student body, and for advising the executive committee and the forum on all matters relative to the delegated areas of responsibility.  
2. Recommend to the university president, with approval of the forum, students who are to serve on university committees and in other university positions. Those appointed will serve at the pleasure of the president of the university.
3. Chair the annual social and philanthropic projects.
4. Have the power to delegate responsibilities of the aforementioned events to other members of Student Government Association.
5. Serve on the Activities Committee.
6. Serve on the Curriculum Committee.
7. Oversee the work of all standing committees on which students serve within that area.

Vice President of Finance
1. Prepare a proposed budget, which shall be submitted to the executive committee.
2. Be responsible for advising the executive committee and the forum on all matters relative to the delegated area of responsibility.
3. Be responsible for overseeing all budget amendments and money requests.
4. Oversee the work of all standing committees on which students serve within that area.
5. Serve on the Activities Committee.

Chief Justice
1. Preside over the judicial council and outline jurisdiction of cases under adjudication.
2. Advise the executive committee and the forum on constitutional matters upon request.
3. Report to the executive committee and the forum on judicial council activities.
4. Write the majority opinion and file a copy of reports with the appropriate university official.

Senators
1. Attend bi-weekly forums.
2. Serve and represent SGA and the student body on CSU committees and accurately report findings to the forum.
3. Bring issues to the forum that affect the students of CSU.

Letters to the Editor

In response to April 5th’s “In your face” column.

Dear Editor,

I am a non-smoker. Ever since I was little and had to endure my mother’s bad habit, I have abhorred the smell, look, and even feel of cigarettes. (She quit while I was still very young.) I have friends who smoke, but they are, for the most part, understanding of my feelings on the subject.

I do not have a problem with cigarettes and people smoking. It’s a bad habit, just like over-eating or biting your finger nails. However, this bad habit has the potential to harm those who do not smoke. What bothers me is the attitude of many smokers I come across. They are going to indulge themselves when they want, where they want, with little regard for their own health or for the comfort of others.

Ms. Adams stated that our society is becoming one of “paranoid tattle-tales.” I think our society is becoming something far worse - one of individualistic, self-oriented people that would rather enjoy what they are doing than deal with the inconvenience of considering others and the health of those around them.

I am fed up with the excuses. “It’s my car and you didn’t have to come along.” “It’s my body, I’ll do with it as I please.” “It’s my house, I’ll stink it up if I want to.” “Me! Me! Me!” Aren’t we as humans, and our relationships with each other, much more important than some habit that is detrimental in the first place? Is a twenty-minute car ride too much time to ask a person not to smoke? I don’t think so.

I truly believe that smokers have no idea how much of an imposition it is for non-smokers to be around them. If I go on a trip with a smoker, I have to wash all of the clothes I took, regardless of whether or not I wore them, before I can wear them again. The odor is that pervasive.

However, I am not going to disassociate myself with my friends who smoke because I love them for who they are and, yes, we all have our faults. However, it pains me to see them continue to bow down to this addiction day in and day out, rearranging their lives around it, always saying “I’ll quit next week.”

Smoking is an addiction. Smoking does kill you. If you smoke and know you need to quit, why not now? All you are doing is postponing the inevitable. It will be just as hard then as it will be now, if not harder. You have no idea how much your life can improve.

It would be my dream to live in a smoke-free society. There would be one less thing in this world to divide and polarize us, especially in a time when unity is so desperately needed.

Ben Wells,

Now that's hardcore
by Wes Carroll / Guest Writer

If you’re anything like me, you aren’t impressed with the mainstream, at least not when it comes to movies. I mean, who cares about big budget movies from big budget studios with big budget actors and big budget directors and big budget budgets?

“A lot of people,” you might answer. “A lot of shallow people with no depth,” I might retort. “Just because people go see Keanu Reeves’ movies like “The Matrix” (now in theaters) doesn’t mean that they don’t have depth,” you might then say. To which I would reply, “What the hell does ‘retort’ mean, anyway?”

So, to avoid this ugly scenario, let me tell you about a nice little underground movie that I heard about from my sources up in Atlanta.

First of all, it’s not a foreign film. I know that people tend to think labeling a film “foreign” makes it “deep.” Well, this is not always true.

Take, for example, the Roberto “Bob” Benigni film “La Vita e Bella (Life is Beautiful)”. That movie was certainly foreign, but in a bad way with all the subtitles and silly accents. On the other extreme, you find really great foreign films like “Young Einstein” with the internationally famous Yahoo “Bob” Serious. You didn’t see him at the Oscars making a fool of himself. In fact, he wasn’t even at the Oscars. Now that’s hardcore.

Then there’s arguably the greatest foreign film of all time, “European Vacation” starring Chevy Chase. Now that’s a great foreign film. But this underground film I’m here to tell you about is not foreign at all, but hopefully be every bit as deep. So here goes:

Apparently (and this comes from a close source I trust) they are making a new...get this...”Star Wars” movie. In case you missed them, the “Star Wars” movies were from a long time ago in a galaxy far, far away. They were a cult hit and I’m just glad that everyone didn’t see them or else that would’ve ruined a good thing. So anyway, my source tells me that this new movie (to be called “The Phantom Menace”) will be a pre-quel (literally ‘before quel’) to the first “Star Wars”.

Now, it’s such an underground movie, I fear that it may not come to Columbus at all. So, I’m campaigning hard to make sure that it gets released here. And you can help by writing to Carmike Cinemas and demanding that they show it. We have a right to see it. And I promise you that I won’t sleep until I know for sure that it will be shown in Columbus. Now I gotta go- “The Matrix” starts in 20 minutes.
