Educator of the Year announced
Press Release / Office of Public Relations, CSU

Columbus State University has named Shawn Cruzen, assistant professor of astronomy, as Educator of the Year. The announcement highlighted the April 30 Scholastic Honors Convocation in Fine Arts Hall. Other top honorees included Bill Chappell (Faculty Service Award), Mary Lindquist (Faculty Research and Scholarship Award), student Anna Mion (Faculty Cup) and Emily Nerness (Phi Kappa Phi and Academic Recognition Awards).

In addition to teaching physics, Cruzen directs the Mead Observatory at CSU’s Coca-Cola Space Science Center. He joined the CSU faculty in 1997, and has published three papers, one investigating the nature of active galaxies and two describing a sample of galaxies found within a giant void. He is currently working on another paper describing these void galaxies.

Primarily through the space science center, Cruzen’s community involvement has included directing astronomy workshops for teachers, quarterly public seminars on astronomy, distance learning projects using the GSAMS (Georgia Statewide Academic and Medical System) network and developing summer camps in space sciences. He holds a philosophy doctorate in Physics from the University of Nevada-Las Vegas.

Chappell, a CSU faculty member since 1976, serves as associate dean of the College of Arts and Letters and professor of Political Science. He is recognized for leading the development of CSU’s Master of Public Administration program as the program’s former director. He is also widely perceived as a mentor to faculty and department chairpersons, and his service on prominent campus committees is extensive. A university liaison to the community, his expertise has been consistently sought by off-campus organizations including the news media.

Lindquist is the Fuller E. Callaway Professor of Mathematics Education in CSU’s College of Education. Since joining CSU in 1984, she has authored or co-authored 19 books or monographs, written 17 chapters, published 22 professional papers and has made several presentations at national and international conferences. She also has been awarded approximately $1.7 million in research grants. She holds a doctorate in Mathematics Education from the University of Wisconsin.

In receiving the Faculty Cup, Anna Mion earned the highest CSU-student honor, signifying superior academic achievement, participation in university organizations, leadership and service.

Also honored as the outstanding 1998-99 history major, Mion (3.97 GPA) received less than an A-grade only once (a “B”) in four years of CSU course work. Other academic achievements include Phi Kappa Phi induction (spring ‘98), Board of Regents Study Abroad Scholarship (France) recipient (summer ‘97), Dean’s list 1995-99, Outstanding Junior History Major (spring ‘98), and she represented CSU as a Hope Scholar for the University System’s Appreciation Banquet for Governor Zell Miller.

Communication major Emily Nerness (4.0 GPA) received the Academic Recognition and Phi Kappa Phi Senior awards. Previous honors include the WRBL Communication Scholarship (1997 and 1999) and a listing in Who’s Who Among Students in American Colleges and Universities. She is president of the CSU Communication Club and a member of Phi Kappa Phi.

Group offers new theatre opportunities
Bridgette Brumbaugh / Staff Writer

A new cultural activity for students has arrived on campus. The new Studio Theatre group will provide students with the opportunity to produce plays that cannot be produced by the theater department.

According to Molly Henderson, the managing director, the group’s mission is to, “Challenge student views on the world around them, broaden students prospects on what theater is and how it can be produced, and present works by contemporary playwrights and students.“

This organization will be able to produce plays that may be too controversial for the school to endorse, but will still be of benefit to the student body.

The group has access to any theater space on campus and will also produce outdoor plays. Tickets will range from $5-$8 so that shows will be cheap enough for all to enjoy.

The organization was around in the early 1980s and has been revived by Gerald Gibson, the artistic director. This is an opportunity for students with busy schedules to participate in theatre and learn about production.

It is the hope of the founders that this group will offer an outlet for not only theatre majors, but for those who have not chosen theatre as their course of study as well. In this way, they plan to include people from all aspects of life here at CSU.

Any student who has a play idea, wants to act, or has written a play and wants it produced can e-mail Studio-Theatre@excite.com.

Balkan expert gives lecture on Kosovo crisis
Elke Brumbaugh / Staff Writer

Last week, the Columbus Room was filled with faculty and students eager to learn about the situation in Kosovo.

Victor Neumann, who teaches history at a Romanian university, gave a lecture on the history of the Balkans to help others understand why the killings are happening in that area.

He explained that the Serbian region is filled with a mixture of languages, religions, and cultures. This area has a long history of land disputes, so military action, in Neumann’s opinion, is pointless.

This received a strong reaction from the military students who wanted to know how he would solve the situation without the presence of ground troops. Mr. Neumann responded by saying he agreed with the Austrian solution, but did not give details.

Several other topics were discussed, including the issue of education in Yugoslavia.

Several groups sponsored the lecture, including the Office of International and Multicultural Affairs. Photographers and the news media were also present.

CSU students react to Colorado tragedy
Clarissa Andrews / Staff Writer

Just before noon on Tuesday, April 20, two students from the “Trenchcoat Mafia” at Columbine High School in Littleton, Colorado, began an attack that left fifteen dead, including themselves. By now, everyone has seen the reports of the sawed-off shotguns, pipe bombs, and the many other weapons used in this killing spree. Across the country, students and parents are terrified at the idea of the same thing happening in their towns. Everyone is asking, “What could have caused those young men to kill thirteen innocent people?” The students at CSU are no exception.

Jean Landreau says, “People are wanting to place blame for this incident. Perhaps some of them should look inward.” Although Landreau does not condone the activities of Eric Harris and Dylan Klebold, she says they were “certainly exhibiting signs of anger long before this tragedy. The wearing of Nazi insignia and uniforms, speaking German, and the Nazi salutes were attempts to get attention... the ultimate attention-getter for these two became a notoriety worthy only of the likes of their idol, Adolf Hitler.”

Another student, Jason Ezzell, feels that the events in Littleton prove “a couple of things. Guns should be a more regulated item and schools should take more safety precautions than they do now.” Ezzell recalled that, during his years at Pacelli High School, many of the outer doors were locked, which meant students had to walk around the building to enter if they got locked out. While some students complained about this, Ezzell says that “it’s better than letting a crazed maniac with a gun walk into the hallway.”

One of the many explanations offered for the killings has been the music the killers listened to-bands such as Marilyn Manson and KMFDM. Adam Brown says that rather than blame the music, people should blame the availability of guns. Many people are angry with the NRA who scheduled a conference in Denver for just a few days after the shootings, and then refused to cancel. Instead, they have agreed to limit it to one day.

According to Brown, “The NRA should be shut down, and the guns should be stored in arsenals somewhere. The Constitution gives us the right to bear arms, but that was when American soil was infiltrated by the enemy. If that happened again, the military could open its arsenals and we could ‘bear arms’ once more. Until then, there is no reason for us to own semi-automatic weapons. It’s outrageous that anyone can get hold of a TEC 9 semi-automatic, much less a minor without his parents knowing about it.”

MerriAnne McCall says that it is “amazing to me what some young people are capable of. I don’t understand what circumstances could make them have that much hatred.”

Landreau doesn’t understand either, but says that “one has to wonder if these young men had received some of the attention they craved, even being told that their behavior was unacceptable, maybe things would have been very different. There is a lesson to be learned from this tragedy for everyone.”

No one will forget the tragedy that took place in Littleton, Colorado, nor will anyone ever be able to give definite answers to the many questions being asked all over the United States. Will people continue blaming Marilyn Manson’s music, especially now that he has cancelled the rest of his tour out of respect to the victims? Will people blame the availability of weapons on the NRA? Or will the blame be placed on the internet, the boys’ parents, cliques in high school, or the thousands of other reasons the young men had for killing thirteen people? Did Klebold and Harris have help making and placing the thirty bombs found around the school? These are questions everyone wants answered...

Whatever the answers may be, the one resoundingly unanimous opinion is that this tragedy is one of the worst this nation has ever experienced.

Within a Room
Leslie Maxwell / Columnist

Well, finals week has arrived. I didn’t think it would ever get here. Finals week is comparatively relaxing for me, since most of my big assignments are usually due the preceding week.

This is the end of my first year at CSU, and it seems like just yesterday I was unloading my car at Courtyard II in the sweltering heat of August. My parents say that time seems to move faster the older you get, but if the world starts spinning any faster, I think I might fly off into space.

Anyway, since this is my last column of the year, I thought I would ramble on about the assorted things I have experienced this year. All in all, I think my experiences at CSU have turned me into a more virtuous person. Let me explain.

First of all, I have lived in the dorms, surrounded by lots of people who apparently do not sleep. That’s fine with me, until they start assuming that I don’t sleep either. My telephone rang five times last night between midnight and two in the morning. Don’t normal people sleep then? To my credit, I didn’t say any bad words, not even the fifth time I had to repeat, “No, she’s still not here.” It’s okay, though, because I am learning patience.

Next, since I live in Courtyard, I am required to buy a meal plan. For those of you unfamiliar with the dorms, we do have fully equipped kitchens, and some of us can even cook. But no, we have to shell out $730 bucks a semester for food that isn’t very good or very healthy. I have to give thumbs up to the lunchroom staff, however, because they are always polite and helpful. A few days ago, one of the serving ladies even agreed with me when I said the food looked disgusting. The cafeteria food is okay, though, because it is teaching me the holy practice of fasting.

This past week, I was sitting in class in Howard Hall, when a loud, car-alarm type noise began blasting throughout the building. Well, my classmates and I looked at each other, wondering, is it a fire alarm? Or a severe weather alert? Should we disturb our deep contemplation of poetry for this? Finally, we got up and followed our professor outside like a row of little ducklings. It was really a false fire alarm, but what if it had been a tornado alert? Do you really want to file out of the building in an orderly fashion if a tornado is coming? Or do you want to “duck and cover” if a fire from the Healthy Choice Deli is raging through the building? So, when faced with such uncertainties here at CSU, I have learned, as the good book says, to pray without ceasing.

And last of all, I hope everyone has enjoyed the random thoughts that have emerged from “within my room” this semester. I have certainly enjoyed composing them for you to read. Every week I have searched diligently (and sometimes painfully) for a topic to entertain, and maybe even enlighten you, and that has taught me perseverance. So, from your devoted columnist, best of luck during finals week - both for the students who have to take tests, and for the professors who have to grade them! I’m sure that, like me, your exertions will bring you closer to virtue, or at least closer to graduation!

ROAR!

Dear Editor,

I would like to discuss an issue that should be important to all of us - the doctors at the health clinic. Recently I became sick and had to visit the health clinic. I saw Dr. Lewis and after about a two minute exam he prescribed medicine for me and sent me on my merry way. Two days later I was back in the doctor’s office feeling worse than before. This time I saw Dr. Sexton. Again after a two minute exam, I received my medication and left. Now one week later, having gotten progressively worse, I visited Dr. Sexton again. After describing my symptoms and again taking two minutes to exam me, he decided I had allergies and gave me medicine. This time I was not convinced. I know I had symptoms that did not go along with allergies - mainly chills and body aches. So I drove to my parents house, 1 1/2 hours from Columbus, to see my family doctor. After a very thorough examination, my doctor diagnosed me with the flu and said I was very contagious. The flu is a very recognizable ailment and one that any qualified doctor should be able to recognize. I’m sure our doctors are good physicians, but they do not take enough time. They rush through the examination, listen to your symptoms, and within five minutes you are done. These doctors do not know us. They cannot decide what we have that quickly. I say we deserve the best care possible. If these doctors are not willing to take some time with their patients, then let’s get some new ones!!

CSU Student

Where did this term, ‘college idiots’ come from? It came, as a result of people getting a college degree and even a master’s degree, and still not knowing much about the field they majored in. This result comes from several reasons.

First of all, it comes from too much cramming for tests so you can try and make a passing grade. Then it is so much material that even if you’re fortunate enough to pass the test, you’re surely not going to retain it. I’m talking three to six chapter tests. I mean, get real! If a person is lucky enough to graduate, they’re only going to retain approximately ten percent of the material or even less than that.

The solution? Teachers, and the Board of Regents need to wake up and give a two chapter test bi-weekly, discuss former chapters along with latter chapters and then give a general comprehensive final test on the most important points. This is the one and only solution to the problem. And, until this problem is dealt with properly, it will continue to exist and plague our future graduates. I, for one, think this is a very serious problem, not to be taken lightly. I know, if I was a teacher, I would hate to be partly responsible for graduating a ‘college idiot’.

I, as a student am very concerned about all this cramming. I’m not talking about waiting until the last minute to study, but I’m referring to cramming three to six chapters into one test. That’s, on the average, only about five-hundred pages one is required to remember, just to get a good grade on the test. I really hope this gets published and, further, yet, I pray that every teacher will take a copy of this to their meeting with the Board of Regents. You know, I really would hate to be classified, after finishing college, with a statement like “Oh well; just another ‘college idiot’.”

A pair of upper-middle class high school kids in a small “close-knit” town have decided that their peers didn’t treat them with respect so they decided to get together a giant pile of automatic weapons and grenades and head to school seeking revenge. Well, I have my theories about why this is happening and I just want to get them out there in the open. The sooner we understand why this is happening, the sooner we can do something about it. There are four basic causes for this phenomenon.

Cause number one is movies set in the old west. Now I know that you are thinking, “Huh?” and I understand that, but hear me out. Think about the plot of every Western ever filmed. A guy or group of guys who are normally peaceful are provoked by some bad guys who inevitably kill or kidnap someone very close to the “good guy”. This may be his mom, daughter, sister, son, wife, girlfriend, sister-in-law, best pal, dog, the town sheriff, the town deputy, the town drunk (Otis), or worst of all, his horse. Lastly, I blame Keanu Reeves. These kids really looked up to Reeves’ character in “The Matrix” (now in theaters) and wanted to be like him when they dressed in black and shot everyone. So, he must be evil, too. That would explain his success as an actor despite the handicap of not having any acting ability whatsoever.

So, what do we do about this? Simple. We send, Keanu Reeves and Marilyn Manson to Iraq. While the Iraqis try to figure out what Manson is, Keanu can peacefully lull them to sleep with a scene from any of his movies. Thus, less hostility.

Kappa Sigma remembers Jefferson Davis
The Brothers of Kappa Sigma

Kappa Sigma brothers, nationwide, recently remembered Jefferson Day, named in honor of Jefferson Davis, the only honorary initiate of Kappa Sigma. The story of Davis’ initiation actually starts with Jefferson Davis Jr. Davis Jr. was one of the first initiates of an early chapter at the Virginia Military Institute. During Davis Jr.’s short life, he exemplified everything that the Order stood for then and continues to strive towards today.

The story has been documented as follows. When the chapter, to which Davis Jr. belonged, contracted yellow fever, the young man refused to seek safety. Instead, Davis Jr. stayed behind to tend to the sick and attempt to nurse his brothers in their time of need. Sadly, Davis Jr. was also claimed by the deadly plague and passed away. This act has often been described as one of the greatest examples of fraternal love in the history of Kappa Sigma.

So moved was the father, Jefferson Davis Sr., that he developed a close affiliation with Kappa Sigma and soon came to love it as his son had before him. Davis often proudly displayed the emblems of the Order, bearing to all his pride in his son. In fact, Davis was instrumental in the founding of Omega Chapter in the formative years of the fraternity. And so, because of his son’s devotion, and the contributions which he made in the name of the Order, Jefferson Davis Sr. was duly initiated into the Kappa Sigma Fraternity MCMLXII.

And so, we as Kappa Sigmas still pay homage to the devotion of the son and the father. “Greater love hath no man.” AEKDB

The Brothers of Tau Kappa Epsilon

The brothers of Tau Kappa Epsilon would like to welcome its newest members during the 1998-99 academic year. Fall semester concluded with the initiation of Drew Ayers, Ricky Dumlao, and David Dale. During the spring semester, TKE decided to have two periods of rush and two candidate periods. The first half of the semester,TKE welcomed Brandon Myers, Chris Rayner, and Dennis McAbee into the brotherhood. After Spring Break, the second rush period began in which Rob Piller received a bid and is currently going through his candidate period.

The Omicron - Tau Chapter of Tau Kappa Epsilon is very proud of these men and their leadership abilities. Ricky Dumlao is currently TKE’s representative to Greek Council and plans to run for a position on executive council. Brandon Myers has taken on the responsibility of Fundraiser Chair for TKE, a job which entails the planing and organizing of fundraising events for the fraternity. Chris Rayner was appointed to the position of Rush Chair, and is responsible for planing and executing rush events for the fall semester. Dennis McAbeeholds the position of Athletic Chair, and is charge of intramural sports on campus. Rob Piller, so far, has made excellent progress through his candidate period and TKE expects great things from him as well.

Tau Kappa Epsilon would like to congratulate all these men for their accomplishments this year. Great things are expected of them in the future.
