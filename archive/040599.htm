Davidson Center undergoes renovation
Helen Godwin / Associate Editor

Columbus State University’s Davidson Center will soon have a brand new facade.

Currently, the area across from the Student Health Center is being renovated to make room for a mini post office area. It will house mailboxes for the students living in campus housing and will be available for other students to use. There will also be a stamp machine and mailbox in this area.

At this time campus mail is only delivered to one spot on campus. Student assistance must distribute the mail around campus, including campus housing. The mini post office will eliminate this extra burden on student assistants. Mail will be delivered to the boxes once a day and at that time the stamp machine will be checked and the mailbox will be emptied.

According to Mr. Pattillo, Vice President of Finance, another renovation that is tentatively scheduled to start during the Christmas holidays of 1999 is a completely new look for the cafeteria and surrounding areas.

Public Safety will move into the P.E. Annex and that area will become a cash and carry section like what is currently available in the cafeteria. The current cafeteria area will become an all-you-can-eat dining area for students with a meal plan. This service is not currently offered because the way that the dining area is set up, there is no controlled access that can be monitored by the staff.

This $1 million dollar renovation will also include making the bathrooms handicap accessible, renovating the entrance, and several other changes.

The Board of Regents is considering what funding will be available for this project, which will happen as soon as CSU re-bids their food service contract. This has not been done for seven years, so Aramark and other food services will soon be submitting proposals for review.

Rwandan peace promoter visits campus
Athena Curry / Editor-in-Chief

Kalisa Roger Remera will address “Rwandan Genocide and U.S./Africa policy in the 21st Century” 12 p.m. Monday, April 12 in the Columbus Room. The lecture is free and open to the public. Remera will be joined by African-activist colleagues Bakary Tandia (human rights/contemporary slavery) and signer/songwriter Rebecca Wakuteka.

Presently a Rastafarian pacifist, Remera works to promote peace in Rwanda and awareness outside the borders. An attack left Remera wounded and his entire family killed during his homeland’s 1994 genocide. He dramatically underlines the importance for U.S. citizens to understand their government’s policies and their impact on ordinary citizens. He also warns of the “deadly” consequences from government-sanctioned arms trading.

Tandia, exiled from Mauritania, lobbied UN missions, the U.S. State Department and European governments in pressuring the Mauritania government to free imprisoned, anti-slavery leader Boubcar Messaoud. He has been a leading activist against his homeland’s oppressive government.

Wakuteka, a native of the Democratic Republic of the Congo, lives in Amsterdam and performs throughout Europe. Her politically-charged songs address the fighting, fear and tension endured by Central Africans.

The event is sponsored by CSU’s Center for International Education, the Student Government Association and the American Friends Service Committee - an international, Philadelphia-based organization promoting justice and peace.

For more information, contact the Center for International Education at 565-4036.

Threat of war prompts opinion on campus
Clarissa Andrews / Staff Writer

By this point in time, almost everyone knows something about what is going on in Yugoslavia, even if it’s just that NATO forces initiated a large air strike last week.

Even students who rarely watch the news or read newspapers know a few details about the situation. While students might not have cared about what was happening on the other side of the world a month ago, the possibility of going to war has stirred up some opinions.

Like many students, Brent Lindley, has mixed emotions: “The United States has money, so I believe we should help the people over there. But I think we could help in other ways-it’s not necessary to risk American lives.” One way Lindley feels this could be done is by sending forces to train the Kosovars to fight for themselves.

Many Americans are comparing Slobodan Milosevic to Adolph Hitler, but Lindley says that everyone should have a chance. He believes it is unfair to make assumptions as to what Milosevic will do in the future.

Dusty Holcomb, on the other hand, is in complete support of the American army’s involvement in Kosovo. He says people have forgotten about World War II.

“Preventive action is a wise choice. I don’t want my kids to have to read about another Holocaust,” Holcomb says.

Another CSU student, who prefers to remain anonymous states, “I read somewhere that there aren’t enough American ground forces to stand man-to-man against the Serbs. If they impose the draft, I’m moving to Canada.”

There are people around campus, however, who are taking the matter as seriously as it should be taken.

Dr. Mion, political science professor, says, “I have no problem with the comparison of Milosevic to Hitler.” He says Milosevic is an evil-minded man, but objects to the idea that he is “crazy.”

According to Dr. Mion, Milosevic is very rational. “He wants Kosovo, whatever the price-if it means slaughtering Kosovars and Albanians, he doesn’t care,” Dr. Mion says.
Dr. Mion says the most difficult question about the situation is whether Americans should actually be involved. “I like to call it a lose-lose situation. The military cannot be a solution to political problems.”

Either way one looks at it, American involvement causes conflicting feelings. People are being pushed out of Albania, and there is much suffering, says Dr. Mion. Yet that doesn’t answer the question of national interest; namely, is it the United States’ interest to fight the Serbs? Should American forces go into Yugoslavia to help save lives and risk their own lives in the process? People are not in favor of this idea, but they don’t want to hear accusations at a later point of simply letting in happen.

Dr. Mion claims that his classes are divided on the subject, but feel the United States should be cautious. Few people are saying, “Let’s move in with ground troops,” but the bombing doesn’t bother people as much if the pilots come back safely.

“Yugoslavia is a sovereign country,” says Dr. Mion. “Bringing in ground troops will harden the opposition against us.”

Students should be more interested in what is going on around them, according to Dr. Mion. “Students ought to be citizens, and citizens ought to be educated, especially in a democracy.”

On Campus

Within a Room
Leslie Maxwell / Columnist

Last Thursday, I was sitting in my Shakespeare class when one of my friends made a random remark about “that country we’re bombing.” I turned around and innocently said, “We’re bombing someone?” The heat-seeking thermonuclear warheads could have been flying over the Atlantic toward us, but I wouldn’t have known about it.

Now, to answer all of your questions: No, I do not watch the news. No, I don’t read the paper, either.

More and more this year, I have found myself isolated in a happy little academic bubble. I can tell you all about 18th-century British Literature or Elizabethan England, but when it comes to current events, I am horrendously uninformed. If it doesn’t make the Weather Channel’s morning forecast or happen on ER, I generally don’t know about it. My world is often limited to what goes on between Howard Hall and Courtyard II across the street. If Columbus suddenly came under nuclear attack, everyone would be fleeing to the nearest bomb shelter, and I would still be sitting in front of this computer writing next week’s column.

I’m always suffering from current event jet lag. During President Clinton’s impeachment trial, I would get an update every few days when I talked to my parents on the phone. I figured that if I got too out of touch, one day it would just dawn on me that Al Gore was being addressed as “Mr. President” for a good reason.

Personally, I am much more concerned about my next research paper than I am about the next development in Bill Clinton’s private life. We live in a Jerry-Springer society, but there are things I would rather not know about: stained dresses and cigars included. I guess that is my excuse for not tuning in to the evening news. I don’t mind keeping up with world events, but I don’t really care what Dennis Rodman’s latest antics are, much less what color his hair is at the moment.

I don’t think that being an uninformed student is that awful, however, and I don’t think I am the only person who exists in a blissfully ignorant academic sphere. College life is supposed to prepare us for the real world, but it is also a time for us to enjoy ourselves, concentrate on our studies, and take advantage of our last few years as students. I’m enjoying these years, because when I am out in the “real” world, I won’t have the luxury to be clueless about politics and world events. So, I’m going to stay in my bubble (or within my room, I should say) a little while longer, but if the Mother Ship lands in Muscogee County, will someone please tell me?

Women's group more visible on campus
Athena Curry / Editor-in-Chief

The committee on the status of women is making an effort to be more visible on campus. In an attempt to raise the level of interest in the organization the committee created a new website that is linked to the Columbus State University site.

According to Dianna Lomarcan, Systems Librarian and committee secretary, although the committee has existed on campus for several years the organization is becoming more pro -active on campus and in the community.

Lomarcan says, “Women’s issues are not necessarily on the forefront of people’s minds. It’s important for us to bring these issues up.”

According to the organization’s website, the committee’s purpose is to address concerns related to the employment, salaries, and wages, and general welfare of women at Columbus State University.

Also according to the website, the committee deals with problems and recommends policies and/or solutions that would change attitudes and practices that restrict the growth and development of women on this campus.

The committee also takes on the responsibility to promote awareness of women’s issues on campus.

The committee’s website includes information on CSU’s sexual assault policy, consensual relationships policy, and sexual harassment policy.

The website also includes links to information, provided by the university, regarding faculty salaries by rank and gender. The statistics show that women on campus have lower salaries than their male colleagues.

Lomarcan says the committee does not have any complaint about women’s salaries at the college now, but that part of the job of the committee is to monitor trends in salaries and address any unfair practices the committee may find.

“Hiring practices and pay are big concerns across the country. We tried to include things on the website that people would be interested in,” Lomarcan says.

At this time the committee is made up solely of women, however, Lomarcan says there is nothing in the language of the committee’s guidelines that would prevent a male from joining the committee based on gender. Lomarcan says the organization encourages involvement from men.

The Committee on the Status of Women’s website address is <http://www.colstate.edu/sofw/>.

Youth orchestra plays for area grade schools
Athena Curry / Editor-in-Chief

Friday, April 2, the CSU youth orchestra performed two concerts for area schools. The orchestra performs for area schools during fall and spring semesters.

Dianna Mann, concert manager, says the concert had a good turnout. Mann says that because elementary schools in particular lack proper orchestra instruction, it is important to introduce young people to this type of music.

The concert included a demonstration of each instrument in the orchestra so students could get an idea of what each one looks and sounds like.

Mann says, "The purpose is to create an interest in music for the students." She says that more and more young people are becoming interested in orchestral music.

"Three years experience is usually the minimum necessary to play," Mann says.

Teaching: the future is here
A Letter from the Editor
Athena Curry / Editor-in-Chief

The days of chalkboards and dry erase boards have bit the dust so to speak. At one time, multicolored chalk was an innovative way for professors to get a message across to their students. There are professors at Columbus State University who were teaching when people thought those “computer things” would never really catch on and students who were born after the internet was created.

Of course, the reverse is true with professors like Robert Oliver, 23, on the cutting edge of everything technical at the university and students (we won’t name names), whose grandchildren have forgotten more about computers than they will ever know. Still, there are those professors young and old who are doing more than keeping up with the times.

Of course, professors didn’t get to be professors because they’re dummies. Even the die hard slide rule fans in the faculty office building are making their way into the age of technology. There are those who just refuse to learn anything more than absolutely necessary, like those who can read their e-mail, but can’t attach a file or still write e-mail messages in all caps. However, many professors are ahead of the game.

Those professors who do go the extra mile should know that we students appreciate the extra effort. Many professors include lecture notes on websites that link to the CSU homepage for easy access. Almost all professors include an e-mail address on their syllabus and many include a web address for more information.

Many, if not most, if not all professors accept disks in replace of hard copies or prefer an e-mail to a phone call. Most students prefer it this way, too.

Jettie Lights, Assistant Professor of Biology, has her own website complete with up to date pictures, data and PowerPoint presentations to help her students. The effort must be time consuming, but the information is not only helpful, it is exactly what the professor ordered because she wrote it herself.

Dr. Ann Rosenthal has more than one site. Her sites include not only help for her classes, but information for any web surfer out there who’s interested. Dr. Rosenthal shares web space with her children, who each have a link to their websites on her site.

There are so many professors using the resources available, it’s hard to imagine college without all the technology. So, for all those professors out there who wrote their dissertations on typewriters and now know that java is more than coffee, bravo and thanks a lot.

Dear Editor:

President Bill Clinton has authorized the U.S. military to engage in air strikes against a country with whom we are not at war, to end a centuries old ethnic conflict which does not threaten the United States, in a part of Europe in which we have no national self-interest. Why? Why are American soldiers being sent off to die in this Balkan wasteland?

Bill Clinton has answered: “It’s a moral imperative.” The President’s “moral imperative” is motivated entirely by the desire to sacrifice American soldiers as global policemen. The essence of his policy is, “We have a duty to prevent any more atrocities from occurring precisely because we don’t have a national interest in Kosovo.”

Yet, a foreign policy that abandons our self interest is doomed to fail and can only result in tragedy. Already one U.S. plane has gone down and despite hundreds of sorties by U.S. and British fighters, Slobodan Milosevic has increased his assaults on ethnic Albanians. Milosevic knows he has nothing to fear from us. When our legitimate interests have been at stake, such as with Iraq and Osama bin Laden, America has demonstrated that it is only a paper tiger. Given America’s past failures to defend its interests and the inherent sacrifices of the current action, the bombing of Serbia is doomed to fail.

What is the solution? We must reject the sacrifice of American lives and declare that the United States exists for its own sake. We must return to the idea that our military exists to protect America - not Kosovar autonomy, oppressed Albanians, Kurdish rebels, or starving Somalians. We must immediately withdraw from this conflict, announce that our military will be used when only U.S. interests are at risk, and uphold this policy consistently. This is the only way to regain our credibility, protect America, and prevent such deadly mistakes in the future.

Sincerely,
Don Thompson

Springer ghost revisits the stage
Press Release from the State Theatre of Georgia, Springer Opera House

In 1876, Edwin Booth performed on the Springer Opera House stage as the Shakespearean Dane Hamlet and amazed audiences with his strength, versatility and rousing talent.

Eleven years after his infamous brother John Wilkes Booth assassinated President Lincoln, Edwin’s prestige as a theatre master remained undisputed and the public adored him.

Now the Ghost of the Springer leaps to life once again in a rare theatrical event filled with humor, passion and drama. April 16, in a special one-night event, the Springer Opera House and the Dorothy W. McClure Springer Theatre Academy present John Ammerman’s haunting “Booth, Brother Booth.”

Filled with compelling excerpts from some of Shakespeare’s greatest works, Booth, Brother Booth is a story of one of America’s greatest performers and the distinguished family of actors that made him a legend.

Assisted with original music, sound effects arid ‘ghost’ voices, this one-man play wrestles with the speculation of Edwin Booth’s love and competition with his brother John Wilkes. Booth, Brother Booth is a ghost play; not only of one man’s tragic past and the voices that it conjures, but a story of universal struggle and perseverance. Searching for a ray of hope against an increasing mountain of despair, yet finding solace within the world of theatre.

Written and performed by John Ammerman, “Booth, Brother Booth” premiered in 1995 at the Georgia Shakespeare Festival where Ammerman is an associate artist. Ammerman went on to perform “Booth” at the newly reconstructed Globe Theatre in London. In addition to holding an M.F.A. in acting/movement from the University of Georgia, Ammerman was a former student in technique and pantomime of the legendary Marcel Marceau. Ammerman’s previous plays include “Silent Snapshots”, “The Five Faces” and “The School for Lovers.”

Tickets are on sale now at the Springer Opera House Box Office located at 1017 Second Avenue in Columbus, GA. The one-night only performance runs April 16 at 8 p.m. at the Springer Opera House located at 103 Tenth Street. Single tickets are $16, senior citizens and Springer Society members $14 and full-time students $12. Ask for special discount information for groups and businesses.

Box office hours are Tuesday through Friday 10:00 a.m. until 4:00 p.m. Beginning April 6, extended box office hours are Tuesday through Friday 10:00 a.m. until 6:00 p.m., Saturday 10:00 a.m. until 2:00 p.m. at the Springer Box Office located at 1017 Second Avenue and one hour prior to showtime at the Springer Opera House located at 103 Tenth Street. Tickets can be ordered by phone by calling (706) 327-3688.

All Greek mixer huge success at CSU
The Brother of Kappa Sigma

Contrary to popular belief, the Greeks at CSU can coexist. This past Monday night, Kappa Sigma hosted an All Greek Mixer at Rae’s Pub. The Greek bash was a huge success and all organizations present had great representation. Among the participants were Sigma Nu, Phi Mu Kappa, Tau Kappa Epsilon, Delta Zeta, Kappa Sigma, Xi Theta, and Sigma Omega.

While Greek Week and Winterfest provide an opportunity for Greeks to get involved, these events can often create strong competition between organizations.

At this event, there were no best participation plaques or awards for enthusiasm. Monday night was strictly a chance for all fraternities and sororities to just socialize and get to know each other better, without all the competition.

Monday night also provided a great opportunity for rush. Some students present were not Greek, but have been interested in rushing. Hopefully, the unity displayed this past week will persuade these students to find out more about the Greek way of life here at CSU.

Because of the huge turn out of both Greek and non-Greek students, more of these mixers will be scheduled in the near future and, hopefully, the Greek system here at CSU will grow even more than it has in recent years.

Kappa Sigma appreciates everyone’s participation and support and hopes that this is the only the beginning of a new brand of Greek unity here on campus.

Sigma Nu implements Alcohol 101
Clayton Bryant / Sigma Nu

Sigma Nu National Fraternity has implemented an alcohol awareness program entitled Alcohol 101.

The goal of Sigma Nu is to educate its members - past, present, and future - on the consequences of alcohol abuse.

The program consists of a series of readings and tests that every candidate, as well as each brother, must successfully complete. Alcohol 101 also has an interactive CD-ROM that can be studied from any personal computer. The disk puts an individual in a virtual party and requires them to make critical decisions regarding the use of alcohol. For every decision made, good or bad, there is consequence.

In addition to Alcohol 101, Sigma Nu National Fraternity is working towards all alcohol free housing to create a better learning environment for its members.
